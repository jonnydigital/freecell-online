The History Desk is the archives team of the Solitaire Stack network. We cover where these games came from, how they evolved, who named them, and which stories about them are true. Solitaire is older than most of the games people think of as "old." Our job is to tell that story clearly.

### The subject

The patience tradition is roughly two hundred and thirty years old. The earliest documented solo card games appeared in northern Europe in the late eighteenth century, migrated through nineteenth-century England and Russia, expanded in Victorian parlors, crossed to North America with immigrants and game compendiums, and then - in 1990 - landed on every Windows 3.0 machine shipped. That last step changed the game. Klondike became the default worldwide not because it was the best patience variant but because Microsoft chose it. FreeCell followed in 1995 because one engineer, Jim Horne, ported it from a VAX.

### What we publish

We publish three kinds of history:

- **Variant genealogy.** Where did Klondike come from, and why is it called that? Which older games did Spider descend from? How is Baker's Game related to FreeCell? Variant family trees are harder to construct than they look, because rule variations drift and names move.
- **Origin stories.** The games have names, and the names usually have stories. Some of those stories are well-documented. Some are folk etymology that got copied into a 1970s paperback and then into the open web. We try to separate the two.
- **Cultural history.** Solitaire has shown up in diaries, letters, prison memoirs, and presidential administrations. Eisenhower played Canfield. Roosevelt played at the Yalta Conference. The patience tradition was, for most of the nineteenth century, considered a women\'s pastime in England - which is part of why the period sources look the way they do.

### Method

We work from primary sources where we can get them. Lady Adelaide Cadogan\'s Games of Patience, published in London in the 1870s, is the earliest English-language collection worth quoting. Dick\'s Games of Patience (1883) and the early twentieth-century Hoyles round out the Victorian canon. Russian and German sources exist but are harder to access; we cite them where we have confidence. For the Microsoft era we work from the documented history of the Windows Entertainment Pack and interviews with the original developers that have appeared in press over the years.

We take two traps seriously. One is repeating legend as fact. "Napoleon invented solitaire on St Helena" is not supported by the evidence; the games he is said to have played predate him. The other is dismissing every legend as fabrication. Some of the stories are true. When we are not sure, we say so.

### Signature pieces

- **Where Klondike Got Its Name.** The Yukon Gold Rush connection, what the evidence supports, and what it does not.
- **The Microsoft FreeCell Era.** From VAX implementation to Windows 95, and the deal-numbering system that still defines the game.
- **A Short History of Patience.** Two hundred years in about three thousand words.
- **Variant Family Trees.** An ongoing series mapping how each game in the canon descended from earlier ones.
- **What Napoleon Actually Played.** A careful look at the historical record.

### What we will not do

We will not repeat a story without a source behind it. We will not smooth over disputed claims to make a cleaner narrative. We will not quote a "fact" from another website without checking whether that site sourced it.
