The Rules Desk owns the teaching layer of the Solitaire Stack network. Every how-to page, every rule sheet, every beginner guide is our responsibility. If a new player arrives on the site having never played FreeCell, we want them to be finishing their first game within ten minutes.

### The problem we are solving

Solitaire rules are not standardized. They never were. Victorian compendiums disagreed with each other, regional traditions disagreed across Europe, and digital implementations make undocumented choices that then become canon for whoever plays them first. In Golf, some versions allow King-to-Ace wrapping; ours does, and we say so. In FreeCell, auto-move thresholds vary; we document what ours does. In Klondike, redeal limits are a policy decision, not a rule. A player who learned Microsoft Solitaire in 1998 and a player who learned from a paperback in 1975 have slightly different expectations about what the game is.

### What we publish

- **Canonical rules pages.** For every variant on the network, a single authoritative rules page: setup, objective, legal moves, win condition, common variants, and what our implementation does.
- **How-to guides.** Step-by-step teaching, written for readers who have never touched the game.
- **Variant comparisons.** When two games are close enough to confuse a player (Klondike draw-1 versus draw-3, FreeCell versus Baker\'s Game, Spider 2-suit versus 4-suit), we explain the differences directly.
- **Rules glossaries.** Terminology used across the site, linked from every rules page.

### Teaching philosophy

We borrow from classroom pedagogy. Name every piece of the board before you use the name. Show one full example before generalizing. Walk the reader through a mistake a beginner would plausibly make, and then fix it. Test the explanation on someone who does not know the game, and rewrite the parts that confuse them.

We also take common rule confusions seriously, because they are usually real. "A suit sequence" means different things in different Spider implementations. "Any card can go on an empty column" is true in some games and not others. "You lose if you cannot move" is not always the win condition. We call these out rather than glossing over them.

### Alignment with the games

Every rules page on the network is checked against the implementation that actually ships in the browser. If a player reads our FreeCell rules and then plays our FreeCell, the rules match. When we add a new variant, a rules page ships with it. When we update a rule in the engine (for example, tightening a redeal limit), the rules page gets updated the same day.

### Signature pieces

- **How to Play FreeCell.** A primer written for complete beginners, tested on real first-time players.
- **Spider Solitaire Rules and Variants.** 1-suit, 2-suit, and 4-suit explained, plus the common regional variations.
- **Klondike Draw-1 vs Draw-3.** Why they are different games, with the rule differences called out.
- **The Rules Glossary.** Linked from every rules page on the network.
- **Common Rule Confusions.** A running list of the things new players get wrong, and why the rule is written that way.

### What we will not do

We will not publish a rules page we have not checked against the running game. We will not paper over rule disagreements with phrases like "in most versions." We will not assume a reader knows what a "foundation" or a "tableau" is until we have defined it on the page.

### Reach out

If a rule on the site is unclear, contradicts what you learned, or does not match the game you just played, write to rules@solitairestack.com. Rule clarity is our core job, and we want the feedback.
