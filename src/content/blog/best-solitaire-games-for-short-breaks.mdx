You have five minutes. Maybe less. You want a card game that starts instantly, engages your brain just enough, and ends before your break does.

These seven solitaire games are ranked by how well they fit into short breaks. The ideal break game is fast to start, quick to finish, and satisfying whether you win or lose.

## 1. TriPeaks — The Perfect Break Game

**Average game time:** 2-3 minutes | **Win rate:** ~90%

TriPeaks was practically designed for short breaks. Cards form three overlapping pyramids, and you clear them by tapping cards one rank above or below your current card. No color matching, no suit tracking — just up or down.

A typical game takes under 3 minutes. The rules require zero explanation. The high win rate means you almost always finish satisfied. And the rapid-fire tapping creates a flow state that makes the time vanish.

**Break rating:** ★★★★★ — Fastest, simplest, most satisfying.

## 2. FreeCell — The Thinking Person's Break

**Average game time:** 4-6 minutes | **Win rate:** ~99%+

FreeCell is slightly longer than TriPeaks, but it compensates with dramatically more strategic depth. Every card is visible from the start, so there is no waiting for lucky draws. You can start planning immediately and make meaningful decisions from move one.

The near-perfect win rate means you rarely waste a break on an unwinnable deal. And because FreeCell is a logic puzzle with perfect information, your brain gets genuine exercise — studies suggest it activates the same cognitive pathways as crossword puzzles and Sudoku.

**Break rating:** ★★★★★ — Best balance of speed, depth, and satisfaction.

## 3. Pyramid — Quick Math Puzzle

**Average game time:** 3-5 minutes | **Win rate:** ~85%

Pyramid solitaire turns card games into a math game: remove pairs that sum to 13. The pyramid layout makes available cards obvious, and the pair-matching mechanic is instantly intuitive.

Games that go well are over in 3 minutes. Games that stall are obvious early enough that you can abandon and start fresh without wasting time. This "fast fail" quality makes Pyramid ideal for breaks — you never get stuck in a 15-minute losing slog.

**Break rating:** ★★★★ — Fast failures save time; wins feel clean.

[Play Pyramid](/pyramid)

## 4. Clock — Zero-Decision Zen

**Average game time:** 60-90 seconds | **Win rate:** ~1-2%

Clock solitaire requires absolutely no decisions. Deal cards into 12 piles (like a clock face), then mechanically flip and place cards based on their rank. Either the cards line up and you win, or they do not.

This sounds boring on paper, but Clock is the solitaire equivalent of popping bubble wrap. It takes barely a minute, requires no mental energy, and the rare win feels genuinely delightful. Perfect for the kind of break where you want to do something without actually thinking.

**Break rating:** ★★★★ — Fastest possible game, zero mental load.

[Play Clock](/clock)

## 5. Monte Carlo — Pattern Spotting

**Average game time:** 4-6 minutes | **Win rate:** ~70%

Monte Carlo lays 25 cards in a 5×5 grid and you remove adjacent pairs of matching rank. After clearing pairs, the grid compresses and new cards fill from the stock.

The visual pattern-matching is satisfying and relaxing. You scan the grid, spot a pair, tap, and the board reshuffles. There is a meditative quality to Monte Carlo that makes it ideal for decompressing after a stressful meeting.

**Break rating:** ★★★½ — Relaxing pattern matching, slightly longer.

[Play Monte Carlo](/monte-carlo)

## 6. Accordion — Minimalist Speed Run

**Average game time:** 3-5 minutes | **Win rate:** ~70%

Accordion lays all 52 cards in a single row and you compress it by matching suit or rank with the card one or three positions to the left. The minimalist design means zero setup time — you are making decisions from the first second.

Accordion is the solitaire game with the fewest rules. If you can identify suits and ranks, you can play. Games rarely exceed 5 minutes, and the one-dimensional layout means you can play on any screen size.

**Break rating:** ★★★½ — Ultimate simplicity, great on small screens.

[Play Accordion](/accordion)

## 7. Gaps — Orderly Satisfaction

**Average game time:** 4-6 minutes | **Win rate:** ~75-80%

Gaps (Montana) removes the aces from a 4×13 grid and asks you to fill the gaps with ascending suit sequences. Two reshuffles per game prevent dead ends.

The satisfaction of Gaps comes from watching order emerge from chaos. Cards slide into place one by one, building neat rows from 2 to King. It is the solitaire equivalent of organizing a messy desk — genuinely relaxing and visually satisfying.

**Break rating:** ★★★ — Slightly longer, but the organization-satisfaction is top-tier.

## Break Strategies

**2-minute break:** Play Clock or TriPeaks. Both finish before you realize you have started.

**5-minute break:** FreeCell or Pyramid. Enough depth to reset your brain without getting sucked in.

**Waiting room / commute:** Monte Carlo or Gaps. Relaxing enough to play in any environment, with no time pressure.

**Between meetings:** FreeCell wins here. The complete information means you can pause and resume without losing track of where you were. No "what was I doing?" when you come back.

The best break game is one that matches your energy level. Tired? Play Clock. Restless? Play FreeCell. Just need to zone out? Monte Carlo. Match the game to the break, not the other way around.
