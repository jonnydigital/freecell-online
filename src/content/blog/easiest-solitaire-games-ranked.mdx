Not every solitaire game needs to be a brain-melting challenge. Sometimes you want a card game you can actually win — something relaxing, satisfying, and forgiving enough that you are not staring at a locked board after three moves.

This ranking sorts solitaire games from easiest to hardest based on two factors: **win rate** (how often a skilled player can solve a random deal) and **learning curve** (how quickly a new player can start winning). Games with high win rates and simple rules rank highest.

## 1. FreeCell — 99.999% Solvable

**Win rate:** ~99.999% | **Learning curve:** Low-medium

This might surprise you. FreeCell is the easiest solitaire game to *win* — not because it requires no thought, but because nearly every deal has a solution. Out of the original 32,000 Microsoft FreeCell deals, only one (#11982) has been proven unsolvable.

All 52 cards are dealt face-up, so there is no hidden information. Four free cells give you temporary storage for maneuvering. Once you learn a few basic principles — keep free cells empty, uncover aces early, plan two moves ahead — your win rate will climb past 80% quickly.

**Why it ranks #1:** The highest solvability of any standard solitaire game combined with complete information makes FreeCell the most reliably winnable card game.

## 2. Easy FreeCell — Training Wheels FreeCell

**Win rate:** ~99.99% | **Learning curve:** Very low

Easy FreeCell pre-places aces and twos on the foundations before the game starts, giving you a massive head start. The tableau is shorter and less tangled, which means fewer moves to untangle and a gentler introduction to FreeCell mechanics.

If standard FreeCell ever feels overwhelming, Easy FreeCell is the perfect on-ramp. Once you are winning consistently, graduate to the standard game.

**Why it ranks #2:** All the benefits of FreeCell's open information with an easier starting position.

[Play Easy FreeCell](/easy-freecell)

## 3. TriPeaks — Fast and Forgiving

**Win rate:** ~90% | **Learning curve:** Very low

TriPeaks (also called Tri Towers) is one of the simplest solitaire games ever designed. Cards are arranged in three overlapping pyramids and you clear them by selecting cards that are one rank higher or lower than the current foundation card. No suit matching, no color alternating — just up or down.

Games take 2-5 minutes and the rules can be learned in 30 seconds. The high win rate keeps you engaged, and the quick pace makes it perfect for filling short breaks.

**Why it ranks #3:** Extremely simple rules, very high win rate, and the fastest average game time on this list.

[Play TriPeaks](/tripeaks)

## 4. Pyramid — Pair Matching Simplicity

**Win rate:** ~85% | **Learning curve:** Very low

Pyramid solitaire uses a simple pair-matching mechanic: remove pairs of exposed cards that add up to 13. Kings are removed alone (they already equal 13). The goal is to clear the entire pyramid.

The math is simple enough for children to play, and the visual pyramid layout makes it immediately obvious which cards are available. Luck plays a role, but experienced players win the vast majority of deals.

**Why it ranks #4:** The pair-matching mechanic is intuitive and the visual layout makes the game self-explanatory.

## 5. Klondike (Draw 1) — The Classic, Made Easier

**Win rate:** ~82% (draw 1) | **Learning curve:** Low

Standard Klondike with draw-1 rules is significantly easier than draw-3. You cycle through the entire stock pile one card at a time, which means every card is accessible and you have maximum flexibility in your choices.

Most people know Klondike as "Solitaire" — it is the default game that comes to mind when someone mentions playing cards alone. Draw-1 rules transform it from a luck-heavy game into a surprisingly manageable one.

**Why it ranks #5:** Universal familiarity plus draw-1 rules create an accessible experience almost everyone can win.

## 6. Gaps (Montana) — Pattern Completion

**Win rate:** ~75-80% | **Learning curve:** Low

Gaps removes the aces from a 4×13 grid and asks you to slide cards into the empty spaces to create ascending suit sequences from 2 to King. The visual layout is clear and the rules are straightforward — each gap can only be filled by the card one rank higher than the card to its left, in the same suit.

You get two reshuffles per game to fix mistakes, which provides a safety net while you learn the patterns.

**Why it ranks #6:** Clean visual layout, simple rules, and the reshuffle mechanic prevents hopeless dead ends.

[Play Gaps](/gaps) | [Gaps Strategy](/gaps/strategy)

## 7. Accordion — Minimal Setup

**Win rate:** ~70% | **Learning curve:** Very low

Accordion lays all 52 cards in a single row and you compress the row by stacking cards that match by suit or rank with the card immediately to the left or three positions to the left. The goal is to compress the entire deck into one pile.

Rules could not be simpler: match suit or rank, move left. The challenge is choosing which matches to make first, but even casual play yields frequent wins.

**Why it ranks #7:** The simplest possible setup and rules in any solitaire game.

[Play Accordion](/accordion)

## 8. Eight Off — FreeCell with Extra Room

**Win rate:** ~85-90% solvable | **Learning curve:** Medium

Eight Off gives you double the free cells of standard FreeCell — 8 instead of 4. The trade-off is same-suit building and a Kings-only empty column rule, but the extra storage makes the game quite forgiving.

If you enjoy FreeCell but want a bit more breathing room (at the cost of slightly different rules), Eight Off hits a nice balance.

**Why it ranks #8:** Generous free cell count compensates for stricter building rules.

[Play Eight Off](/eight-off) | [Eight Off Tips](/eight-off/tips)

## 9. Clock — Pure Luck, Pure Fun

**Win rate:** ~1-2% | **Learning curve:** None

Wait — why is a game with a 1% win rate on an "easiest" list? Because Clock solitaire requires zero decisions. You deal cards into 12 piles arranged like a clock face, then flip and place cards mechanically based on their rank. No strategy, no choices, no thinking.

Clock is the solitaire equivalent of a slot machine. When you win, it feels like magic. When you lose, it took 60 seconds and you did not have to think. It is the ultimate zero-effort solitaire game.

**Why it ranks #9:** Literally requires no skill or decisions. Easiest to *play*, hardest to *win*.

[Play Clock](/clock)

## 10. Monte Carlo — Visual Pair Matching

**Win rate:** ~70% | **Learning curve:** Very low

Monte Carlo lays 25 cards in a 5×5 grid and you remove pairs of matching rank that are adjacent (including diagonally). After removing pairs, the grid compresses and new cards fill from the stock. Clear all cards to win.

The visual grid makes pairs easy to spot, and the adjacency rule is intuitive. No sequences, no foundations, no color matching — just find and remove pairs.

**Why it ranks #10:** Simple pair-matching on a visual grid with no complex rules to learn.

## Finding Your Level

**Total beginner?** Start with TriPeaks or Clock. TriPeaks teaches basic card sequencing with a very high win rate. Clock requires no decisions at all.

**Want to think but still win?** FreeCell is the sweet spot. High win rate, complete information, and genuine strategy.

**Graduating to harder games?** Once you are winning 80%+ at FreeCell, try [Baker's Game](/bakers-game) (same layout, same-suit building) or [Spider 2-suit](/spider) for a real challenge.

The best solitaire game is the one you enjoy playing. Start easy, build confidence, and move up when you are ready.
