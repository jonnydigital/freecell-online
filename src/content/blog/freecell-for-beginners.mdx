You've probably seen FreeCell on a computer at some point — it's been included with Windows since 1995. But if you've never actually sat down and played, the eight columns of cards can look intimidating. Here's the good news: FreeCell is one of the most learnable card games that exists, and almost every deal is solvable.

## What Is FreeCell?

FreeCell is a single-player card game (solitaire) played with a standard 52-card deck. Every card is dealt face-up into 8 columns, which means there's no hidden information — you can see every card from the start. This makes it a game of pure strategy, not luck.

Your goal: move all 52 cards to four foundation piles (one per suit), building each from Ace up to King.

## The Layout

- **8 Cascades** — Columns of face-up cards. This is where all the action happens.
- **4 Free Cells** — Temporary parking spots in the upper left. Each holds exactly one card.
- **4 Foundations** — Upper right. Build Ace → 2 → 3 → ... → King, one per suit.

That's it. No stock pile, no draw pile, no hidden cards.

## Basic Rules in 60 Seconds

1. **Move cards between columns** by building down in alternating colors. A black 7 goes on a red 8. A red Queen goes on a black King.
2. **Use free cells** as temporary storage — park one card there when you need to access a card beneath it.
3. **Move cards to foundations** whenever possible. Aces go first, then 2s, 3s, and so on.
4. **Empty columns** can hold any card (and they're incredibly valuable — treat them like extra free cells).
5. **You can move multiple cards at once** if you have enough empty cells and columns to theoretically move them one at a time. This is called a "supermove."

## Your First Game: A Step-by-Step Approach

### Step 1: Scan for Aces
Look at all 8 columns. Are any Aces on top? Move them to the foundations immediately. Are any Aces buried? Note which columns they're in — uncovering them is your first priority.

### Step 2: Look for Easy Wins
Check if any 2s can go to foundations (if the matching Ace is already there). Look for natural sequences already forming in the columns — like a 10-9-8 in alternating colors sitting on top of each other.

### Step 3: Clear Short Columns
Short columns (2-3 cards) are close to becoming empty. Empty columns are your most powerful tool — they're essentially free cells that can hold sequences, not just single cards. Prioritize clearing them.

### Step 4: Keep Free Cells Open
This is the #1 beginner mistake: filling all four free cells. Once they're all occupied, you're stuck. Try to keep at least one cell open at all times, ideally two.

### Step 5: Build Down, Build Up
On the tableau (columns), you build down in alternating colors. On the foundations, you build up by suit. The game is a dance between these two directions — you need to temporarily build tableau sequences to eventually promote everything to the foundations.

## The 5 Biggest Beginner Mistakes

### 1. Filling All Free Cells
Every free cell you fill reduces your maneuverability. Think of free cells as your emergency reserves — once they're gone, one bad position and the game is over.

### 2. Ignoring Empty Columns
New players treat empty columns as "done" — but they're actually your most valuable resource. An empty column is like a free cell that can hold an entire sequence. Guard them.

### 3. Moving Cards Without Purpose
Every move should have a reason. "I can move this card, so I will" is a losing strategy. Ask: "What does this move achieve? Does it uncover an Ace? Free up a column? Extend a sequence?"

### 4. Building Long Sequences Too Early
A pretty 13-card sequence feels satisfying but often blocks other cards you need. Short, purposeful sequences that free up space are better than long, decorative ones.

### 5. Not Using Undo
[FreeCell](/game) has unlimited undo for a reason. Explore different move sequences. Try something, see if it works, and backtrack if it doesn't. This isn't cheating — it's how you learn.

## How Winnable Is FreeCell?

Here's what makes FreeCell special: approximately **99.999%** of deals are solvable. Out of the classic 32,000 Windows FreeCell deals, only one — [Deal #11982](/blog/is-every-freecell-game-winnable) — is confirmed unsolvable.

This means when you lose, it's almost always because of your strategy, not bad luck. That's what makes FreeCell so satisfying to improve at.

## What's a Supermove?

When you have empty free cells and empty columns, the game lets you move multiple cards at once. The formula: **(1 + empty free cells) × 2^(empty columns)**.

- 0 empty cells, 0 empty columns = move 1 card
- 2 empty cells, 1 empty column = move 6 cards
- 4 empty cells, 2 empty columns = move 20 cards

Understanding supermoves is the bridge between beginner and intermediate play. More empty space = more power.
