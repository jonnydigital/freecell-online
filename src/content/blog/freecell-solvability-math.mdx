Here's a number that surprises most people: **99.999% of all FreeCell deals are winnable.** Not "theoretically possible with perfect play and a lot of luck" — genuinely solvable through pure logic and planning. No hidden cards, no dice rolls, no draws from a shuffled stock pile. Just you, 52 face-up cards, and a solution that exists if you can find it.

That number isn't an estimate. It's the result of decades of computational work by mathematicians, programmers, and obsessive FreeCell enthusiasts who systematically solved millions of deals to map the boundaries of what's possible.

## The Original 32,000: Where It All Started

When Jim Horne wrote FreeCell for Microsoft in the late 1980s, he made a decision that changed the game's history: he numbered the deals. Instead of a random shuffle each time, his program used a pseudo-random number generator seeded with an integer from 1 to 32,000. Deal #1 always produced the same card layout. Deal #17,000 always produced the same layout. This meant players everywhere could attempt the same puzzles and compare results.

It also meant that every deal could be cataloged — and that's exactly what happened.

Throughout the 1990s, a community of dedicated players set out to solve all 32,000 original Microsoft FreeCell deals. They played by hand, shared strategies on early internet forums, and gradually checked off deal after deal. By the late '90s, they had solved 31,999 of them.

One deal refused to fall: **#11982.**

## Game #11982: The Impossible Deal

Deal #11982 isn't just hard. It's mathematically impossible to solve under standard FreeCell rules. No sequence of legal moves, no matter how clever, can move all 52 cards to the foundations.

The deal was first flagged as unsolvable in the late 1990s by the Internet FreeCell Project, a collaborative effort where volunteers claimed and attempted specific deal numbers. After thousands of players failed to solve #11982, programmers wrote automated solvers to confirm: no solution exists.

What makes #11982 impossible? It comes down to card positions creating an inescapable deadlock. Certain key cards are buried in columns where freeing them requires moves that themselves require those same cards to be free — a circular dependency with no way to break the cycle. The four free cells and eight tableau columns simply don't provide enough temporary storage to untangle the knot.

For the original 32,000 deals, the final tally was definitive:
- **31,999 solvable** (99.997%)
- **1 unsolvable** (#11982)

Seven other deals — #146, #495, #512, #1689, #1941, #2177, and #10692 — were once considered unsolvable candidates but were eventually cracked by human players or improved solvers. They were brutally difficult but not impossible.

## Scaling Up: What Happens Beyond 32,000?

The original 32,000 deals used Microsoft's specific random number generator, which limited the deal space. But FreeCell's actual deal space is much larger — the number of distinct ways to arrange 52 cards into 8 columns is astronomical.

Researchers have explored far beyond the original set. When the deal space was expanded to 1,000,000 using the same generation algorithm, only **eight deals** were found to be unsolvable. At 8,000,000 deals, the unsolvable count remained vanishingly small.

The most comprehensive analysis, covering billions of randomly generated deals, consistently finds that approximately **99.999%** are solvable. That's roughly 1 in 100,000 deals that can't be won — not because you played poorly, but because no solution exists within the rules.

To put that in perspective: if you played 10 games of FreeCell every day, you'd encounter an unsolvable deal roughly once every 27 years.

## How Solvers Prove Solvability

Determining whether a specific FreeCell deal is solvable is a computational search problem. Solvers explore the tree of possible game states, trying every legal sequence of moves to find one that leads to all cards on the foundations.

The most well-known FreeCell solver is **FreeCell Solver**, an open-source project by Shlomi Fish that has been in active development since 1999. It uses several search strategies:

**Depth-first search (DFS)** dives deep into one line of play before backtracking. It's memory-efficient but can get lost in long dead-end branches.

**Breadth-first search (BFS)** explores all moves at each depth level before going deeper. It guarantees the shortest solution but consumes enormous amounts of memory.

**A\* search** uses heuristics — educated guesses about which moves are most promising — to guide the search toward solutions more efficiently. The heuristic might prioritize moves that build on foundations, free up columns, or uncover deeply buried cards.

**Soft-DFS with move pruning** is what most modern solvers actually use. It's a depth-first approach that aggressively eliminates obviously bad moves before exploring them, dramatically reducing the search space.

For a typical solvable deal, these solvers find a solution in milliseconds. For the hardest solvable deals, they might explore millions of states over several seconds. For truly unsolvable deals, the solver exhausts the entire game tree — proving that no sequence of moves can win — which can take significantly longer.

## Why Games Feel Impossible (But Aren't)

If 99.999% of deals are solvable, why do so many games feel unwinnable? Several factors conspire to make solvable deals feel hopeless:

**Early column-locking.** When all four free cells fill up and multiple columns become immovable, the position looks dead. But often a specific sequence — perhaps moving cards to foundations first to create space, then using that space to reorganize — can untangle things. The solution exists, but it requires thinking several moves ahead.

**The "wrong order" problem.** You need the 3♠ to play on the 4♠, but the 3♠ is buried under six other cards, and freeing it requires... well, a 12-move sequence that isn't obvious from the current position. Human players naturally explore 2-3 moves ahead. Solvers explore 50+ moves deep without breaking a sweat.

**Anchoring on a bad plan.** Once you start building a particular sequence, you tend to keep trying to make it work. The actual solution might require abandoning your current approach entirely and taking a counterintuitive path — like deliberately moving cards off the foundations (which many players don't even realize is legal in some rule sets) or filling a free cell with a card you think you need available.

**Psychological tilt.** After 10 minutes of struggling with a deal, your pattern recognition starts working against you. You see the same stuck position and assume it's dead. A fresh set of eyes — or stepping away and returning later — often reveals moves you missed.

The gap between "solvable" and "solvable by a human in a reasonable time" is significant. Competitive FreeCell players typically win 80-95% of their games. That means even experts lose 5-20% of the time on deals that are, in fact, solvable. The remaining losses come from suboptimal play, not impossible deals.

## The Mathematical Beauty of FreeCell

What makes FreeCell's solvability rate remarkable isn't just the number itself — it's what the number implies about the game's design.

A game where 100% of deals are solvable would be too easy. There'd be no tension, no fear of failure. A game where only 50% of deals are solvable would be frustrating — half your games would be doomed from the start through no fault of your own.

FreeCell sits in a sweet spot: **almost** every game is winnable, so you know a solution probably exists, but the difficulty varies enormously. Some deals fall in two minutes. Others require 20 minutes of deep concentration. And once every few thousand games, you hit a wall that feels impossible — and it might actually be one of those rare unsolvable deals, or it might be a puzzle that demands you think harder than you ever have.

That balance — between confidence that a solution exists and uncertainty about whether you'll find it — is what makes FreeCell enduringly compelling. It's a game of pure skill operating in a space where the math is almost entirely on your side, but "almost" does a lot of work.

The next time you're stuck on a deal and ready to declare it impossible, remember: the odds are about 100,000 to 1 that a solution exists. The question isn't whether the game is winnable. The question is whether you can find the path.
