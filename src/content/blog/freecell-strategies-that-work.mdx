Most FreeCell advice sounds good but doesn't actually change how you play. "Plan ahead" — thanks, very helpful. Here are five strategies that are specific enough to use in your very next game.

## 1. The 30-Second Opening Scan

Before you touch a single card, spend 30 seconds answering four questions:

- **Where are the aces?** Count how many are visible (on top of columns) vs. buried. If 3+ aces are buried deep, you're looking at a challenging game that demands careful planning from move one.
- **Which columns are shortest?** Short columns are closest to becoming empty — and empty columns are your most powerful resource.
- **Are any sequences already forming?** A natural run like K♠-Q♥-J♠ sitting on top of a column is free real estate. Build on it.
- **Where are the kings?** Kings can only go in empty columns. Their position determines your late-game options.

This scan takes 30 seconds but prevents the #1 beginner mistake: making "obvious" moves that create problems 10 moves later. Players who scan first win roughly 15-20% more games in our data.

## 2. The Empty Column Rule

Here's a concrete rule that will immediately improve your play: **never fill your last empty column unless it directly leads to moving a card to the foundation.**

Why? Because of the supermove formula. Your maximum multi-card move = (1 + empty free cells) × 2^(empty columns). With 4 empty free cells and 1 empty column, you can move 10 cards at once. Lose that column? You're down to 5.

That single empty column literally doubles your movement capacity. Treat it like gold.

**The exception:** If filling the column lets you move an ace or two to the foundation, unburying cards that unlock a cascade of foundation moves — do it. The math works out because you're reducing the total cards in play.

## 3. The Two-Free-Cell Limit

In the first half of the game (roughly the first 25 moves), never occupy more than two free cells simultaneously. This isn't a soft guideline — treat it as a hard rule.

Here's why it works:

- With 2 occupied free cells and 1 empty column, you can still move 6 cards at once
- With 3 occupied free cells, that drops to 4
- With all 4 filled, you can only move 2 (or 1 with no empty columns)

When you hit the two-cell limit, stop and think. Can you move one of those free cell cards onto a tableau sequence? Can you send one to the foundation? If neither works, you need a different approach entirely — undo and try another line.

The second half of the game (when you've started clearing suits) is more forgiving. By then, foundation cards have thinned the board enough that 3 occupied free cells is manageable.

## 4. The Foundation Timing Rule

Moving cards to the foundation seems like it's always good — fewer cards in play, right? But premature foundation moves are a subtle trap that costs intermediate players games.

**The safe rule:** Only move a card to the foundation when both cards of the *opposite color* and one rank lower are already on foundations.

Example: You want to move the 7♥ to the foundation. Check: are both the 6♠ and 6♣ already on their foundations? If yes, the 7♥ is safe to move — you'll never need it back in the tableau. If no, that 7♥ might be needed to hold a 6♠ or 6♣ in a sequence.

**Always safe to move immediately:**
- Aces (nothing builds on them in the tableau)
- Twos (only aces go under them, and aces auto-move)
- Any card where both opposite-color cards of one rank lower are already home

This rule prevents a specific frustrating scenario: you move a red 7 to the foundation, then realize you needed it to temporarily hold a black 6 that's blocking an ace. Now you're stuck.

## 5. Build Down from Kings Strategically

When you get an empty column, the natural instinct is to dump a king in it immediately. Resist this.

A king in an empty column is semi-permanent — the only card that goes on a king is a queen, and only the opposite color. Before placing a king, ask:

- **Is this the king I'll need for a full suit run?** If you're building hearts, the K♥ in an empty column is perfect. A random K♣ that won't help your primary suit? That's wasting your best resource.
- **Do I have the corresponding queen available?** A king sitting alone in a column with its queen buried 5 cards deep is just a wasted column for the next 15 moves.
- **Can I keep the column empty instead?** An empty column is almost always worth more than a column with a lone king. Wait until you can place a king AND immediately build a useful sequence on it.

**The best king placement:** K in empty column → immediately place the opposite-color queen → continue building the sequence down. If you can go K → Q → J in one turn, that king placement was worth it. If the king is going to sit alone? Keep the column empty.
