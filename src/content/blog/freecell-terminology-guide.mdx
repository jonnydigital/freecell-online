Reading FreeCell strategy advice without knowing the terminology is like reading a recipe in a language you half-understand. You get the gist, but the specifics are fuzzy. Here are the 10 terms that unlock every strategy guide, tip page, and forum discussion about FreeCell.

## 1. Cascade

The eight columns of cards on the playing field. When you start a game, all 52 cards are dealt into 8 cascades — four with 7 cards and four with 6. This is where you build sequences and maneuver cards.

**Why it matters:** When someone says "clear a cascade," they mean empty an entire column. That's one of the most powerful moves in the game.

## 2. Free Cell

The four temporary storage spots in the upper-left corner. Each holds exactly one card. The game is literally named after them — they're what make FreeCell different from other solitaire variants.

**Why it matters:** Free cells are your flexibility budget. Fill them all and you're paralyzed. Keep them open and you can rearrange almost anything. The general rule: never fill more than two at a time unless you have a concrete plan to empty them.

## 3. Foundation

The four piles in the upper-right where you build each suit from Ace to King. Getting all 52 cards to the foundations is how you win.

**Why it matters:** Moving cards to foundations is usually good, but not always. Sometimes keeping a card on the tableau gives you more options than promoting it early. Advanced players know when to delay foundation moves.

## 4. Supermove

Moving multiple cards at once as a single action. In strict rules, FreeCell only allows single-card moves — but the game automates the process of using free cells and empty cascades as intermediate steps.

**The formula:** (1 + empty free cells) × 2^(empty cascades)

With 2 empty free cells and 1 empty cascade, you can supermove up to 6 cards. This is why empty columns are so valuable — each one doubles your supermove capacity.

**Why it matters:** Understanding supermove math is the single biggest leap from beginner to intermediate. Check our [strategy guide](/strategy) for the full breakdown.

## 5. Tableau

The entire playing area — all eight cascades plus the cards on them. Some guides use "tableau" and "cascade" interchangeably, but technically the tableau is the whole layout while a cascade is one column.

**Why it matters:** "Tableau building" means arranging cards in descending order with alternating colors on the cascades. This is how you organize cards before promoting them to foundations.

## 6. Alternating Colors

The rule for building on cascades: red cards go on black cards, black cards go on red cards, always descending. 9♥ (red) goes on 10♠ (black). 5♣ (black) goes on 6♦ (red).

**Why it matters:** This is FreeCell's core building rule. Variants like [Baker's Game](/bakers-game) use same-suit building instead, which makes them dramatically harder.

## 7. Auto-Complete (Auto-Finish)

When all remaining cards can be moved to the foundations in order with no decisions left, the game plays itself out. Cards fly to the foundations automatically.

**Why it matters:** Triggering auto-complete is a satisfying milestone in each game. Our [FreeCell](/game) detects this automatically and accelerates the animation — sit back and watch the cards cascade home.

## 8. Deal Number (Seed)

Every FreeCell game has a number that determines how the cards are shuffled. Deal #1 always produces the same layout. This means you can share specific games with friends or replay famous deals like [#11982](/blog/is-every-freecell-game-winnable) (the only unsolvable deal in the classic 32,000).

**Why it matters:** Deal numbers are how the FreeCell community discusses specific puzzles. "Try Deal #617" means something — it refers to one exact card arrangement. Play any deal by number in our [Deal Explorer](/deals).

## 9. Blocking (Buried)

A card is "blocked" or "buried" when it's underneath other cards in a cascade and can't be accessed without moving the cards above it first. An Ace buried 5 cards deep is a problem you need to plan around.

**Why it matters:** Recognizing blocked cards is the first step of opening analysis. Before your first move, identify which critical cards (Aces, 2s, and key sequence connectors) are buried, and plan your early moves around uncovering them.

## 10. Empty Column (Void)

A cascade with no cards in it. In FreeCell, any card can be placed in an empty column, making it strictly more powerful than a free cell (which holds only one card). Empty columns hold entire sequences via supermoves.

**Why it matters:** Empty columns are the most valuable resource in FreeCell. Creating and preserving them is the hallmark of expert play. See our guide on [common mistakes to avoid](/freecell-mistakes-to-avoid) for more on column management.
