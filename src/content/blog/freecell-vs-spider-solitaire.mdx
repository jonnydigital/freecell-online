After classic Klondike, FreeCell and Spider Solitaire are the two most popular card games on the planet. Windows bundled both for decades, and millions of players swear by one or the other.

But which is actually harder? The answer depends on how you define "hard."

## The Quick Answer

**FreeCell is harder to lose. Spider is harder to master.**

FreeCell's 99.999% solvability means almost every deal *can* be won — the question is whether *you* can find the solution. Spider (especially 4-suit) has many truly unsolvable deals, but easier deals are more forgiving of imperfect play.

## Win Rate Comparison

| Variant | Solvability | Typical Win Rate | Expert Win Rate |
|---------|------------|-----------------|----------------|
| FreeCell | 99.999% | 50-65% | 85-95% |
| Spider 1-Suit | ~99% | 60-70% | 90%+ |
| Spider 2-Suit | ~30-35% | 20-30% | 40-50% |
| Spider 4-Suit | ~10-15% | 5-10% | 15-25% |

The numbers tell a clear story: **4-suit Spider is dramatically harder than FreeCell**. But 1-suit Spider is arguably easier.

## Perfect Information vs Hidden Information

This is the fundamental difference between the two games:

**FreeCell** deals all 52 cards face-up. Every card is visible from the start. There are zero surprises — every loss is a solvable puzzle you failed to crack. This makes FreeCell a pure logic game, more like chess than poker.

**Spider** deals cards face-down in the tableau and has a stock pile. You're making decisions with incomplete information. When you deal a new row from the stock, you might get exactly what you needed — or a disaster. This randomness element means even perfect play can't guarantee a win.

## Strategy Depth

### FreeCell Strategy
- **Free cells** are temporary storage — [managing them is the core skill](/strategy)
- **Empty columns** multiply your moving power exponentially
- **Supermoves** let you shift entire sequences using free cells and empty columns
- **No hidden information** — every mistake is your fault

### Spider Strategy
- **Building same-suit sequences** is the priority (completed suits are removed)
- **Stock management** — knowing when to deal new cards is critical
- **Color mixing** vs. suit-pure building is the key tension
- **Recovery plays** — turning bad stock deals into workable positions

Both games reward planning ahead, but the *type* of thinking is different. FreeCell is about resource management (cells and columns). Spider is about sequence building under uncertainty.

## Which Should You Play First?

**Start with FreeCell** if you:
- Like puzzles with definite solutions
- Want to know that every loss is fixable
- Prefer systematic, logical thinking
- Are competitive about win streaks

**Start with Spider** if you:
- Enjoy some randomness and surprise
- Like the satisfaction of clearing entire suits
- Don't mind lower win rates
- Want a more relaxed, less "solvable" experience

## The Verdict

FreeCell is the tighter, more demanding game — but it's also more *fair*. Every deal is a puzzle with a solution, and getting better at FreeCell feels like genuine skill growth.

Spider (4-suit) is objectively harder to win, but some of that difficulty is randomness rather than skill. 1-suit Spider is a great warmup, but 4-suit is one of the most punishing solitaire variants in existence.

Our recommendation: **play both**. [FreeCell](/) for the puzzle challenge, [Spider](/spider) for the sequence-building satisfaction. They exercise different parts of your brain, and switching between them keeps both fresh.
