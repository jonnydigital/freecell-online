Some solitaire games are designed to be won. Others are designed to make you question whether winning is even possible.

This ranking sorts solitaire games from hardest to least-hard based on **win rate** (how often optimal play solves a random deal), **strategic depth** (how many meaningful decisions per game), and **punishment factor** (how quickly one mistake can doom a deal). The hardest games combine low win rates with deep strategy and zero margin for error.

## 1. Scorpion — The Silent Killer

**Win rate:** ~10-15% | **Strategic depth:** High | **Punishment:** Extreme

Scorpion looks deceptively like Spider, but it is far more punishing. Seven columns of cards with three columns partially face-down. You build descending same-suit sequences and can move any face-up card along with everything below it — but there is no stock pile to bail you out and no redeals.

The killer mechanic: once you run out of face-down cards to reveal, you have exactly the cards in play. If your suits are tangled, there is no escape. One wrong move in the first ten turns can make the game unwinnable, and you often will not realize it until 40 moves later.

**What makes it #1:** The combination of no stock pile, no redeals, and cascading consequences makes Scorpion the most punishing solitaire game in common play.

## 2. Spider 4-Suit — The Final Boss

**Win rate:** ~10-15% | **Strategic depth:** Very high | **Punishment:** High

Spider Solitaire with all four suits is widely considered the ultimate test of solitaire skill. Two full decks (104 cards), ten columns, and you must build eight complete descending same-suit sequences to win.

The difficulty comes from suit management across four suits. You can temporarily stack cards of different suits (which is what makes it playable), but only complete same-suit sequences can be moved to foundations. This means every cross-suit stack is technical debt you must eventually repay.

**What makes it #2:** The sheer number of cards (104) combined with 4-suit management creates more possible game states than almost any other solitaire variant.

## 3. Beleaguered Castle — No Free Cells, No Mercy

**Win rate:** ~20-25% | **Strategic depth:** High | **Punishment:** Extreme

Beleaguered Castle uses the same foundation-building goal as FreeCell but removes free cells entirely. All 52 cards are dealt into eight columns around four pre-placed aces. You build by any suit in descending order on the tableau.

Without free cells, you have zero temporary storage. Every card must go either on a valid tableau position or directly to a foundation. This creates agonizing decisions where unblocking one card permanently blocks another. Many deals are unsolvable, and recognizing dead positions early is a crucial skill.

**What makes it #3:** Removing free cells from the FreeCell formula eliminates the safety net that makes FreeCell forgiving.

## 4. Baker's Dozen — Cramped and Unforgiving

**Win rate:** ~65-70% | **Strategic depth:** Medium-high | **Punishment:** High

Baker's Dozen deals 52 cards into 13 columns of 4 cards each. Kings are automatically moved to the bottom of their columns during the deal, which helps — but there are no free cells and no redeals. You build foundations up by suit and tableau columns down by any suit.

The difficulty comes from the cramped layout. Thirteen short columns mean very little room to maneuver, and without free cells, you must plan precisely to avoid locking cards in unrecoverable positions.

**What makes it #4:** The combination of many short columns and no temporary storage creates a tight, unforgiving puzzle.

[Play Baker's Dozen](/bakers-dozen) | [Baker's Dozen Strategy](/bakers-dozen/strategy)

## 5. Baker's Game — FreeCell's Harder Original

**Win rate:** ~50-65% | **Strategic depth:** Very high | **Punishment:** High

Baker's Game is the game FreeCell was derived from. Same layout — 8 columns, 4 free cells, 4 foundations — but with same-suit building instead of alternating colors. This single rule change cuts available moves by roughly 75%.

Every card placement must match suits, which eliminates the cross-color shuffling that FreeCell players rely on. Free cells become even more precious because you cannot temporarily park cards on opposite-color columns. About 25% of deals are simply unsolvable.

**What makes it #5:** A single rule change transforms FreeCell from 99.999% solvable to ~75% solvable. That is the power of same-suit building.

## 6. Forty Thieves — Double-Deck Endurance

**Win rate:** ~10-20% | **Strategic depth:** High | **Punishment:** High

Forty Thieves uses two decks (104 cards) dealt into ten columns of four face-up cards each. The remaining 64 cards form a stock pile dealt one at a time with no redeal. Build foundations up by suit; build tableau down by same suit.

The difficulty is relentless: same-suit building with 104 cards and only one pass through the stock. You must make nearly perfect use of every card drawn or you will run out of moves long before the stock is exhausted.

**What makes it #6:** The combination of two decks, same-suit building, and a single-pass stock creates overwhelming complexity.

[Play Forty Thieves](/forty-thieves) | [Forty Thieves Strategy](/forty-thieves/strategy)

## 7. Yukon — Hidden Information Plus Full-Column Moves

**Win rate:** ~25-30% | **Strategic depth:** High | **Punishment:** Medium-high

Yukon looks like Klondike but plays very differently. Cards are dealt into seven columns with the same Klondike layout plus additional face-up cards on columns 2-7. There is no stock pile. You can move any face-up card along with all cards below it, regardless of sequence.

The hidden face-down cards create uncertainty, and the ability to move arbitrary groups of cards creates an explosion of possible moves. Choosing which moves to make — and which to avoid — requires deep tactical evaluation.

**What makes it #7:** No stock pile means you must solve the deal with only what is on the table, and face-down cards add uncertainty.

[Play Yukon](/yukon) | [Yukon Strategy](/yukon/strategy)

## 8. Flower Garden — The Exposed Reserve Trap

**Win rate:** ~30-35% | **Strategic depth:** Medium-high | **Punishment:** Medium

Flower Garden deals 36 cards into six columns ("the garden") and 16 cards into a fully visible reserve ("the bouquet"). You can play any bouquet card at any time, which sounds generous — until you realize that any-suit building and no free cells make the garden incredibly hard to untangle.

The bouquet creates a false sense of security. Having 16 available cards feels powerful, but the garden columns are so tightly packed that getting cards in and out requires precise planning.

**What makes it #8:** The open bouquet reserve provides options but does not compensate for the rigid garden layout.

[Play Flower Garden](/flower-garden) | [Flower Garden Strategy](/flower-garden/strategy)

## 9. Seahaven Towers — Kings-Only FreeCell

**Win rate:** ~85-90% solvable, ~60-70% practical | **Strategic depth:** High | **Punishment:** Medium

Seahaven Towers gives you 4 free cells (called towers) and uses same-suit building with a Kings-only empty column rule. It deals 50 cards into 10 columns of 5, with 2 cards starting in the towers.

The Kings-only restriction fundamentally changes how you value empty columns. An empty column is useless unless you have a King to fill it — creating situations where clearing a column actually hurts you.

**What makes it #9:** The Kings-only rule transforms empty columns from universally valuable to situationally dangerous.

[Play Seahaven Towers](/seahaven-towers) | [Seahaven Strategy](/seahaven-towers/strategy)

## 10. Klondike (Draw 3) — Deceptively Difficult

**Win rate:** ~30-35% (practical) | **Strategic depth:** Medium | **Punishment:** Medium

Yes, regular Klondike with draw-3 rules belongs on a hardest list. Most casual players never realize that draw-3 Klondike is genuinely difficult — theoretical solvability is around 75-79%, but practical win rates for experienced players hover around 30-35%.

The draw-3 mechanic means you only see every third card in the stock, and the cards you need are often trapped behind cards you cannot use. This introduces a heavy luck element that makes consistent winning nearly impossible.

**What makes it #10:** The gap between perceived difficulty (it is "just Solitaire") and actual win rate (~30%) makes Klondike Draw 3 a stealth hard game.

## Ready to Test Yourself?

If you made it through this list and want to start with the hardest game you can actually learn, try [Baker's Game](/bakers-game). It uses the same layout as FreeCell — which most people already know — but the same-suit building rule makes it dramatically harder.

For the ultimate challenge, [Spider 4-Suit](/spider) rewards hundreds of hours of practice with the deepest strategic experience in solitaire.

And if you want to warm up first? [FreeCell](/freecell) has a 99.999% solvability rate and teaches fundamentals that apply to every game on this list.
