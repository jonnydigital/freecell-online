Every copy of Windows from 1995 to 2012 shipped with FreeCell. Hundreds of millions of people played it. But the game's origins go back almost two decades before that — to a university mainframe system called PLATO and a medical student who wrote the first digital version as a side project.

## The PLATO Days (1978)

In 1978, Paul Alfille was a medical student at the University of Illinois. The university was home to PLATO — the **Programmed Logic for Automatic Teaching Operations** system — one of the earliest networked computer platforms. PLATO had terminals with orange plasma displays and a thriving community of students writing games, chat programs, and educational software.

Alfille knew a card game called Eight Off (sometimes called "Baker's Game"), where you build sequences by suit. He modified the rules to allow alternating-color builds — red on black, black on red — making the game more accessible and introducing a new strategic layer. He called his creation **FreeCell**.

He coded it for PLATO in the TUTOR programming language. The game spread through the PLATO community, where users could play on terminals connected to the mainframe. It was a hit — but it stayed within that ecosystem. PLATO was a walled garden, and when users graduated, they left FreeCell behind.

For over a decade, FreeCell existed only in the memories of PLATO users and in a few scattered reimplementations.

## Jim Horne and the Microsoft Deal (1988–1995)

In 1988, Jim Horne — a programmer at Microsoft — wrote a FreeCell implementation for the company's internal use. It ran on MS-DOS and became popular among Microsoft employees. Horne's version introduced something that would define FreeCell for decades: **numbered deals.**

Rather than shuffling randomly each time, Horne's program used a pseudo-random number generator seeded with a deal number from 1 to 32,000. Type in deal #1 and you'd always get the same layout. Type in #7 and you'd get the same layout every time. This seemingly simple decision transformed FreeCell from a casual card game into a puzzle game — players could compare strategies on identical deals, return to unfinished ones, and collectively catalog which deals were solvable.

Microsoft included FreeCell in **Windows 3.1's Win32s Entertainment Pack** in 1992 as a tech demo for the new 32-bit subsystem. It was literally a marketing tool — a fun reason to install Win32s on your 16-bit Windows. Then, in 1995, it shipped with **Windows 95**, and the world changed.

Suddenly, hundreds of millions of people had FreeCell on their computers. And unlike regular Solitaire (Klondike), which has a significant luck component, FreeCell was almost entirely skill-based. You could see every card from the start. If you lost, it was your fault. That addictive combination of fairness and challenge hooked an entire generation.

## The Great Hunt: Is Every Game Winnable? (1990s–2000s)

The numbered deal system created a natural question: **are all 32,000 deals solvable?**

The FreeCell community set out to answer it. Players worldwide attacked the problem systematically. Internet groups formed. Dave Ring organized the "Internet FreeCell Project" in the mid-1990s, coordinating solvers to work through every deal number.

One by one, the deals fell. 31,998 of 32,000 were confirmed solvable. Then 31,999. Finally, only one remained: **Deal #11982.**

No one could solve it. Not casual players, not obsessive experts, not the computer solvers that were starting to emerge. Eventually, it was mathematically proven impossible — the initial card layout of Deal #11982 creates a position from which no sequence of legal moves leads to a win.

The discovery became FreeCell lore. Deal #11982 is the game's Everest — a single impossible deal among 31,999 solvable ones, giving FreeCell a **99.997% solvability rate** for the original Microsoft deals. Later analysis of millions of random deals puts the overall solvability rate at approximately **99.999%**.

## Microsoft FreeCell: The Golden Age (1995–2012)

From Windows 95 through Windows 7, Microsoft FreeCell was a fixture of office life. The game was responsible for untold hours of procrastination. IT departments reportedly tried to remove it from corporate builds. Hospital administrators complained about nurses playing between shifts.

The interface barely changed across Windows versions — the classic green felt background, the four free cells in the top left, the four foundation piles on the right. Microsoft knew better than to fix what wasn't broken.

During this era, FreeCell quietly became **one of the most-played computer games ever made.** It shipped on every PC sold worldwide for 17 years. No other game had that distribution.

Windows 8 (2012) changed things. Microsoft replaced the built-in games with downloadable apps from the Windows Store. FreeCell was bundled into **Microsoft Solitaire Collection**, which included ads and a premium subscription. The simple, ad-free FreeCell that came with Windows was gone.

## FreeCell in the Browser Era

The shift to web and mobile created a new ecosystem for FreeCell. Freed from Microsoft's bundled game, FreeCell found new life as:

- **Browser games** — HTML5 and JavaScript made it possible to play FreeCell in any web browser without downloads or plugins. Sites like [PlayFreeCellOnline.com](/) recreated the classic experience with modern features like responsive design, statistics tracking, and daily challenges.
- **Mobile apps** — FreeCell became one of the most popular card game categories on iOS and Android app stores.
- **Solver programs** — Advanced algorithms can now solve virtually any FreeCell deal in seconds, enabling features like hint systems and automated difficulty ratings.

The browser era also brought new game modes that weren't possible in the original: [Streak mode](/streak) for consecutive wins, [daily challenges](/daily-freecell) that give every player the same deal, [Storm mode](/storm) for speed, and variant games like [1-Cell](/freecell/1-cell), [2-Cell](/freecell/2-cell), and [3-Cell](/freecell/3-cell) FreeCell.

## Why FreeCell Endures

FreeCell has survived for almost 50 years because of a rare combination of properties:

- **Perfect information** — every card is visible from the start. No hidden decks, no luck of the draw after the initial deal. If you lose, it's because of your decisions, not bad luck.
- **Nearly universal solvability** — 99.999% of deals are winnable. You almost always *can* win. The question is whether you're skilled enough.
- **Depth without complexity** — the rules fit on an index card, but the strategy fills books. Easy to learn, genuinely hard to master.
- **The puzzle factor** — numbered deals make each game a discrete puzzle. "Can you solve Deal #617?" is a question with a definitive answer.

From a PLATO terminal in 1978 to your browser today, FreeCell's core hasn't changed. Four free cells. Eight columns. One objective. The game Paul Alfille created as a side project became one of the most-played games in human history.
