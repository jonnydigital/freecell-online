A standard 52-card deck has been producing distinct solitaire variants for at least 250 years, and the count is still growing. Estimates of the total number of named variants range from a few hundred to over a thousand, depending on whether you count regional variants of the same base game as distinct or identical.

What's surprising about this variety isn't the number -- it's how many meaningfully different games you can build by adjusting a small set of structural parameters. This piece maps those parameters and explains why changing a single rule can produce a game that feels completely unlike its parent.

## Key Takeaways

- Most solitaire variants share a common structural vocabulary: **stock, tableau, foundation, waste pile, and reserve**. Variants emerge from changing the rules governing each component.
- The four most powerful design variables are: **tableau building rule**, **information completeness**, **temporary storage availability**, and **card movement unit**.
- A single rule change -- same-suit vs alternating-color tableau building -- cuts the solvability of FreeCell from 99.999% to roughly 75% in Baker's Game.
- The vast majority of known solitaire variants can be placed in about a dozen structural families based on which design variables they share.
- Understanding these variables helps players pick games to learn, predict difficulty, and transfer skill between variants.

## The Basic Component Set

Almost every solitaire variant uses some combination of five structural zones:

**The tableau** is the main play area, the columns of cards where most activity happens. Almost all variants have a tableau.

**The stock** is the face-down pile of undealt cards that players draw from during the game. Some variants (FreeCell, Baker's Game, Eight Off) have no stock -- all cards are dealt to the tableau at the start.

**The waste pile** holds cards drawn from the stock that can't be immediately placed. Klondike's waste pile is a single face-up card; some variants allow the whole waste pile to be searched.

**Foundations** are the target piles where cards are built up by suit to complete the game. Almost all variants have foundations.

**Reserves or free cells** are temporary storage locations outside the tableau. FreeCell's four free cells are the most famous example, but reserves appear in many forms across the variant family.

The variant library is the result of designers varying the rules for each of these components -- and occasionally eliminating some components entirely.

## The Four Major Design Variables

### 1. Tableau Building Rule

How cards stack on each other in the tableau is the most consequential design variable in solitaire.

The main options are:

- **Alternating color, descending** (Klondike, FreeCell): a black 7 goes on a red 8, regardless of suit
- **Same suit, descending** (Baker's Game, Eight Off): a 7 of spades goes only on an 8 of spades
- **Any suit, descending** (Spider 1-suit, Yukon, Beleaguered Castle): any 7 goes on any 8
- **Specific rank relationships only** (Pyramid, Tri-Peaks): cards interact based on numerical combination rather than stacking

Alternating-color building (FreeCell, Klondike) is the most permissive of the ordered options. At any given column position, two out of four suits are eligible to stack, which creates a dense network of legal moves.

Same-suit building cuts eligible stackers to one out of four suits -- a 75% reduction in potential moves. This explains most of the difficulty increase when moving from FreeCell to Baker's Game. The layout and free cells are identical; the building rule alone accounts for the solvability dropping from 99.999% to ~75%.

Any-suit building (used in Spider 1-suit) seems like it would be the easiest, and in isolation it is -- any card can go anywhere eligible. But Spider introduces a new constraint: only same-suit sequences can be moved as a unit and only same-suit completed sequences go to foundations. This means any-suit stacking creates temporary flexibility at the cost of long-term suit management obligation.

### 2. Information Completeness

How much of the deal is visible to the player from the start shapes the strategic character of the game more than any other variable.

**Complete information variants** (FreeCell, Baker's Game, Eight Off, Flower Garden) deal all cards face-up. No information is withheld. The player can verify solvability in principle, plan arbitrarily far ahead, and learn from every loss because every outcome was caused by a specific decision.

**Partial information variants** (Klondike, Yukon, most tableau games with face-down cards) hide some portion of the deal. Players must make decisions under uncertainty. Skill still dominates luck in the long run, but any individual game can be lost to a bad reveal rather than a bad decision.

**Near-complete hidden information** (Clock Solitaire) makes the game nearly or entirely luck-determined. The player's choices barely affect the outcome.

The information dimension interacts strongly with the solvability variable. Complete-information games can be analyzed -- in principle, you can verify if a given deal is winnable before you make a single move. Partial-information games can't: the winning path may require face-down reveals to go a certain way, and you can't know in advance whether they will.

This distinction has practical implications for players. In a complete-information game, a persistent stalemate is almost always a planning error. In a partial-information game, it might be the deal's fault.

### 3. Temporary Storage

The presence, quantity, and rules governing temporary storage may be the most direct dial for difficulty adjustment in the open-layout variant family.

FreeCell's four free cells are the prototypical example. Each free cell can hold exactly one card, with no restrictions on which card. The player can use them to temporarily set aside cards that are blocking needed moves.

Increase free cells to 8 (Eight Off) while tightening building rules, and the extra storage compensates for the stricter constraints. The game's difficulty lands in a similar range to FreeCell despite the harder building rule.

Eliminate free cells entirely (Beleaguered Castle) while keeping the same layout and alternating-color building, and solvability drops to roughly 20-25%. Every card must go on a valid tableau position immediately; there's no buffer for temporarily inconvenient cards.

The same principle explains why empty tableau columns function as powerful free cells in most variants. An empty column can hold any card (or in some variants, only a King), which makes it more versatile than a single free cell. Expert players in FreeCell and Spider prize empty columns highly because they multiply the effective temporary storage available.

### 4. Card Movement Unit

In some games, you can only move a single card at a time. In others, you can move complete sequences. In others, you can move any face-up group regardless of sequence. This variable dramatically changes the game's tactical character.

**Single card only**: Games where only one card can move at a time (classic Klondike, many traditional Patience games) require moving sequences piece by piece. Every element of a sequence must have a temporary home while the sequence is rebuilt elsewhere.

**Complete sequences**: Games that allow moving completed in-suit sequences as a unit (Spider) reward careful sequence maintenance because the payoff is a major increase in mobility.

**Supermoves**: FreeCell and similar games technically allow only single-card movement, but with enough free cells and empty columns, the player can execute what amounts to moving large groups in sequence. This is the basis of the FreeCell "supermove" concept -- the game allows it because the move sequence is feasible, even if every individual step is a single card.

**Any face-up group** (Yukon, Wasp): Some variants allow moving any face-up card along with everything stacked on top of it, regardless of whether the group forms a legal sequence. This creates enormous flexibility that is offset by the hidden-card uncertainty in those same games.

## The Structural Families

Applying these four variables produces a rough taxonomy of the solitaire family:

**Open Builders** (FreeCell, Baker's Game, Eight Off, Seahaven Towers): All cards visible, some free cells, foundation-first goal. Variations come from building rules and free cell counts. See the [freecell vs baker's game comparison](/freecell-vs-bakers-game) and [freecell vs eight off comparison](/freecell-vs-eight-off) for how small rule changes separate these games.

**Closed Builders** (Klondike, Yukon, Canfield): Partial information, stock pile, foundation goal. The defining genre in popular culture. Risk is shared between player skill and card reveal luck.

**Packing Games** (Spider, Scorpion, Wasp): Two-deck or sequence-focused games where the goal is completing same-suit sequences, often without a predefined foundation-building sequence. Strategy is about suit management over the long game.

**Reserve Games** (Forty Thieves, Napoleon at St. Helena): Large tableaux with single-card draws from a stock, no free cells. Endurance and careful stock management dominate strategy.

**Matching Games** (Pyramid, TriPeaks, Golf): Cards interact by numerical combination rather than sequential stacking. Luck plays a larger role. Shorter sessions, lower strategy ceiling.

**Pure Luck** (Clock Solitaire): Deal-determined outcome with near-zero player agency.

## What This Means for Choosing What to Play

The structural taxonomy makes it easier to navigate the variant library with a specific goal in mind.

If you want **maximum skill expression**, stay in the Open Builders family. Complete information and defined temporary storage mean your decisions fully determine the outcome. FreeCell is the most accessible entry point; [Baker's Game](/freecell-vs-bakers-game) is the natural step up.

If you want **comfortable uncertainty** with skill still mattering, Closed Builders (Klondike, Yukon) balance predictable rules with deal-to-deal variation.

If you want **long-game strategic depth**, Packing Games (Spider in particular) reward extended investment in suit management thinking that doesn't develop quickly.

If you want **short, complete sessions**, Matching Games run faster and feel complete in 5-10 minutes.

## Why New Variants Keep Appearing

The variant space is not exhausted. New named variants continue to appear because the design parameter space is genuinely large. Changing two variables simultaneously (say, any-suit building with 6 free cells and a 2-deck stock) produces a game that may not have existed before, and if it plays well, it gets named and passed on.

Most new variants don't survive because the variable combinations either produce games that are trivially easy, provably impossible for too many deals, or unpleasant to play despite being technically sound. The variants that survived in the tradition generally solved a real design problem: they found a combination that is challenging but fair, learnable but not trivial, playable alone without specialized equipment.

That filter is why the classic variants (Klondike, FreeCell, Spider) are classics. They're not just old. They happen to occupy well-calibrated spots in the design parameter space that produce good games.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How many solitaire variants actually exist?**

Reliable estimates put named variants in the hundreds, with some sources citing over 500 distinct games. The count depends on whether you treat regional variants (same base game, slightly different rules) as separate or identical. The core "major" variants that are played by millions of people number around 20-30.

**Can you create a new solitaire variant just by changing rules?**

Yes, and it's been done continuously for 200 years. Most combinations of existing rules produce uninteresting or unplayable results, but occasionally a new combination produces a game with its own character. The structural vocabulary in this article gives you the toolkit to experiment.

**Why doesn't changing the deck size create entirely new games?**

It does. Games using two full decks (Spider, Forty Thieves, Napoleon at St. Helena) have a fundamentally different character from single-deck games because the doubled card population changes probabilities and interaction patterns. The same logic applies to stripped-deck variants (removing 2s through 6s, for example), which produce shorter, tighter games.

**Is there a "purest" form of solitaire?**

There's no consensus answer, but FreeCell is sometimes cited as the closest to a pure logic puzzle because of complete information, near-100% solvability, and no luck component after the deal. Klondike is cited as the "purest" expression of the classic patience tradition because of its historical primacy.

**Which structural variable has the biggest impact on difficulty?**

Building rule strictness and information completeness together account for most of the variance in difficulty across the major variants. Same-suit building with hidden cards (a combination that appears in no major variant, interestingly) would produce a very difficult game. Alternating-color building with complete information (FreeCell) produces the most accessible strategic game in the family.
