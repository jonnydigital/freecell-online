Ask Google "how many solitaire games are there" and you will get a vague answer. The real answer is both specific and wild: **there are over 500 documented solitaire variants**, with estimates ranging as high as 1,000+ if you count regional variations, rule tweaks, and games that were invented once, written down somewhere, and forgotten.

That number surprises most people. If your entire solitaire experience is Klondike, FreeCell, and maybe Spider, learning that there are hundreds of other patience card games feels like discovering your neighborhood has 500 restaurants you never noticed.

## The Documented Count

The most authoritative source is David Parlett's *The Penguin Book of Patience* (1979), which catalogs over 250 distinct solitaire games. Since then, online databases have expanded the list significantly:

- **Solitaire Laboratory** (maintained by Michael Keller) documents approximately 600+ distinct games
- **Wikipedia's list of patience games** includes 200+ entries with many sub-variants
- **BVS Solitaire Collection** software offers 535 playable variants
- **Pretty Good Solitaire** includes over 1,100 games (counting rule variations)

The exact count depends on what you consider "distinct." Is Klondike Draw-1 a different game from Klondike Draw-3? Most catalogers say yes. Is "Australian Patience" different from "Yukon"? They share 90% of their rules but have key differences. The boundary between "variant" and "separate game" is blurry.

A reasonable estimate: **500-600 genuinely distinct solitaire games**, with another 300-500 minor variations on those games.

## Why So Many?

### 1. The Rules Space Is Enormous

A solitaire game is defined by a handful of design choices:

- **Number of decks** (1, 2, or more)
- **Tableau layout** (columns, rows, fans, pyramids, circles)
- **Building rules** (alternating color, same suit, any suit, rank matching)
- **Foundation rules** (ascending by suit, descending, alternating)
- **Reserve/stock mechanics** (draw 1, draw 3, redeal rules)
- **Movement restrictions** (single cards, sequences, any face-up card)
- **Empty space rules** (any card, Kings only, nothing)
- **Special mechanics** (free cells, waste piles, reserves, cells)

Combine these variables and the number of possible games grows exponentially. Most combinations produce games that are either trivially easy, hopelessly impossible, or boring — but a surprisingly large number hit the sweet spot of being interesting to play.

### 2. Centuries of Invention

Solitaire has been played since at least the late 18th century. That is roughly 250 years of people sitting alone with a deck of cards, getting bored with their current game, and thinking "what if I changed this one rule?" Every generation of card players contributed new variants.

The French and English were particularly prolific inventors. Napoleon was (perhaps apocryphally) said to play patience during exile. The Victorian era saw a solitaire boom, with books cataloging dozens of games. Each book included a few originals alongside established classics.

### 3. The Computer Explosion

The arrival of computers in the 1990s multiplied solitaire variants dramatically. Before computers, inventing a new solitaire game meant playtesting it by hand — dealing hundreds of games with physical cards to check if the rules were fair and interesting. That limited innovation to dedicated enthusiasts.

Computer solitaire programs changed everything. Developers could test millions of random deals instantly, fine-tuning rules until win rates hit satisfying levels. Software collections competed on variant count, driving developers to create (or rediscover) ever more games. Pretty Good Solitaire's 1,100+ games would be inconceivable without computer testing.

## The Major Families

Most solitaire games belong to one of about a dozen families that share core mechanics:

### Klondike Family (~30 variants)
Build alternating-color tableau columns. Cards partially hidden. Stock/waste pile. The most familiar family, including Klondike, Yukon, Alaska, Australian Patience, and Thumb and Pouch.

### FreeCell Family (~15 variants)
Open tableau (all cards visible). Free cells for temporary storage. Includes FreeCell, Baker's Game, Eight Off, Seahaven Towers, and Penguin.

### Spider Family (~20 variants)
Build same-suit sequences in the tableau. Deal from stock in waves. Includes Spider (1/2/4-suit), Scorpion, Wasp, and Spiderette.

### Forty Thieves Family (~25 variants)
Two decks, strict same-suit building, single-card stock deals. Includes Forty Thieves, Streets and Alleys, Beleaguered Castle, and Busy Aces.

### Pyramid Family (~10 variants)
Remove pairs that add to a target number. Includes Pyramid, TriPeaks, Golf, and Black Hole.

### Canfield Family (~12 variants)
Named after a casino game. Reserve pile, tableau building, specific foundation start card. Includes Canfield, Demon, and Acme.

### Clock/Mechanical Family (~8 variants)
Minimal or no decisions. Cards placed by rank/suit into predetermined positions. Includes Clock, Calculation, and Monte Carlo.

### Fan Family (~15 variants)
Cards dealt into small fans or groups. Includes La Belle Lucie, Shamrocks, Bristol, and Baker's Dozen.

### Building Family (~20 variants)
Pure foundation-building focus with various tableau support. Includes Aces Up, Accordion, and Gaps.

## The Most Obscure Solitaire Games

For every well-known game, there are a dozen you have probably never heard of:

- **Crescent** — Two decks, 16 fans, alternating foundation directions (some build up, others down)
- **Miss Milligan** — Forty Thieves variant with a "waiving" mechanic that lets you temporarily store sequences
- **Penguin** — FreeCell variant where the first card dealt sets the foundation base rank and one column starts empty
- **Bisley** — All aces start on foundations, four Kings on a separate row, build both up and down simultaneously
- **Gaps** — Remove all aces, slide cards left into gaps, create ordered rows. Deeply satisfying when it works
- **Flower Garden** — 6 tableau columns plus a "bouquet" of 16 reserve cards you can play at any time
- **Sir Tommy** — Often cited as the oldest solitaire game (possibly 18th century). Deal cards one at a time into four waste piles, build foundations up

## Why Does This Matter?

Two reasons. First, if you are bored with your current solitaire game, there are literally hundreds of alternatives you have never tried. Many of them are excellent. The solitaire genre is vastly deeper than Klondike → Spider → FreeCell suggests.

Second, the sheer number of variants demonstrates something about games and human creativity. Solitaire is a constrained design space — one or two decks of standard playing cards, one player, simple spatial rules. Yet within those constraints, people have invented 500+ distinct games that are genuinely fun to play. Constraints breed creativity.
