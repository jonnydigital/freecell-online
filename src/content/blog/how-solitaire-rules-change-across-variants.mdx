Every solitaire game uses the same 52-card deck. Yet somehow, there are over 500 documented variants, each with a distinct personality. How do a few rule changes transform the same materials into completely different experiences?

The answer lies in six fundamental rule dimensions. Every solitaire variant is defined by how it configures these dimensions. Understanding them will help you learn new games faster and appreciate why your favorites feel the way they do.

## 1. Information Visibility — Open vs. Closed

The single biggest factor in a solitaire game's character is how much you can see.

**Open games** deal all cards face-up. You see everything from the first move, which means every win or loss is determined entirely by your decisions. These games feel like logic puzzles.

- [FreeCell](/): All 52 cards visible. ~99.999% solvable.
- [Baker's Game](/bakers-game): All 52 visible, same-suit building. ~75% solvable.
- [Eight Off](/eight-off): All 52 visible, 8 free cells. ~85-90% solvable.

**Closed games** hide some cards face-down or in a stock pile. You make decisions with incomplete information, which means luck plays a significant role. These games feel more like traditional card games.

- [Klondike](/klondike): 21 cards hidden in the tableau, 24 in the stock. ~30-40% win rate.
- [Spider](/spider): Multiple face-down cards per column. ~33% win rate (4-suit).
- [Clock](/clock): Almost entirely hidden. ~1% win rate.

**Semi-open games** split the difference. [Yukon](/yukon) deals all cards into the tableau but starts most face-down — you can see the top cards but not what's buried underneath.

**Why it matters:** Open games reward planning and calculation. Closed games reward adaptability and risk management. Your preference here usually predicts which variants you'll enjoy.

## 2. Building Rules — How Cards Stack

Every solitaire game has rules for how cards stack on the tableau (the main playing area). These rules vary along two axes: color and suit.

### Color Rules

- **Alternating color** (most common): Red on black, black on red. Used in [FreeCell](/), [Klondike](/klondike), and [Yukon](/yukon). Gives maximum flexibility because each card has two possible target suits.
- **Same suit**: Only spades on spades, hearts on hearts. Used in [Baker's Game](/bakers-game), [Eight Off](/eight-off), and [Spider](/spider). Dramatically harder because each card has exactly one target.
- **Any suit**: Any card of the correct rank can stack regardless of color or suit. Used in [Flower Garden](/flower-garden) and some [Baker's Dozen](/bakers-dozen) variants. The most forgiving option.
- **Same color**: Red on red, black on black. Less common, used in a few obscure variants.

### Rank Direction

Almost all solitaire games build the tableau in descending order (lower ranks on higher ranks). The foundation always builds ascending (Ace up to King). A few variants like [Gaps](/gaps) use rank adjacency rather than strict ascending/descending order.

### Practical Impact

The building rule is the single biggest determinant of difficulty within open games. FreeCell and Baker's Game use the exact same layout, but switching from alternating-color to same-suit building drops the solvability rate from ~99.999% to ~75%. That one rule change transforms a reliably winnable puzzle into a game where 1 in 4 deals is impossible.

## 3. Temporary Storage — Free Cells, Reserves, and Stock Piles

How a game provides temporary card storage defines its rhythm and strategy.

**Free cells** are the most flexible option. Each holds one card of any type, which you can retrieve at any time. [FreeCell](/) has 4 free cells; [Eight Off](/eight-off) has 8. Free cells give you explicit maneuvering room and make the "how many cards can I move" calculation transparent.

**Stock piles** give you a stream of new cards to work with, but the order is fixed. [Klondike](/klondike) has a 24-card stock dealt 1 or 3 at a time. You can cycle through the stock but can only access the top card.

**Reserves** are pre-dealt card pools. [Flower Garden's](/flower-garden) "bouquet" is a 16-card reserve where every card is accessible. [Canfield](/canfield) has a 13-card reserve where only the top card is available. Reserve accessibility varies widely between variants.

**No storage at all**: Some games like [Pyramid](/pyramid) provide no temporary storage — you work only with what's on the table.

The storage mechanism shapes the core tension of each game:
- Free cells: *Which card deserves this precious slot?*
- Stock piles: *Will the card I need come up before I run out of cycles?*
- Reserves: *How do I sequence draws from this pool optimally?*

## 4. Empty Column Rules — What Goes Where

When you clear an entire tableau column, what can fill it? This seemingly minor rule has an outsized impact on strategy.

**Any card**: [FreeCell](/), [Baker's Game](/bakers-game), [Klondike](/klondike). An empty column acts as a super free cell — any card or sequence can go there. This is the most flexible option and makes empty columns extremely valuable.

**Kings only**: [Eight Off](/eight-off), [Scorpion](/scorpion), some [Spider](/spider) variants. Only Kings can fill empty columns, which means clearing a column is only useful when you have a King ready to place. This prevents the "chain reaction" plays that make FreeCell so satisfying.

**No filling**: [Baker's Dozen](/bakers-dozen) and some variants don't allow empty columns to be filled at all. Once a column empties, it stays empty. This makes every card removal from a column a consequential, irreversible decision.

**Why it matters:** Games with flexible empty column rules tend to be more forgiving and have higher win rates. The Kings-only restriction in [Eight Off](/eight-off) is a major reason it's harder than [FreeCell](/) despite having double the free cells.

## 5. Winning Condition — Build, Match, or Clear

Not all solitaire games are won the same way.

**Foundation building** is the most common goal: move all cards to foundation piles, building each suit from Ace to King. This is how [FreeCell](/), [Klondike](/klondike), [Spider](/spider), and most classic variants work.

**Pair matching** requires removing cards in matching pairs. [Pyramid](/pyramid) removes pairs that sum to 13 (e.g., 7 + 6, Queen + Ace). [Monte Carlo](/monte-carlo) removes adjacent pairs of the same rank. The goal is to clear all cards from the table.

**Sequence ordering** means arranging cards in a specific order. [Gaps](/gaps) asks you to arrange each suit in order from 2 to King in a row, with gaps in the right places. [Clock](/clock) requires placing each rank on its corresponding hour position.

**Column clearing** is the goal in some games like [Accordion](/accordion), where you compress the entire deck into a single pile through specific merging rules.

The winning condition shapes the entire feel of a game. Foundation-building games reward methodical planning. Pair-matching games reward pattern recognition. Sequence games reward spatial reasoning.

## 6. Movement Restrictions — Supermoves, Sequences, and Limitations

The rules governing *how* you move cards between columns vary significantly.

**Single card movement**: The purest form. You move one card at a time. [FreeCell](/) technically uses single-card movement, but supports "supermoves" — moving multiple cards at once as a shortcut for what would be a series of individual moves through free cells and empty columns.

**Sequence movement**: Games like [Klondike](/klondike) and [Yukon](/yukon) let you pick up an entire properly-ordered sequence and move it as a unit. In Yukon, you can even move unordered piles as long as the connecting card follows the building rule.

**Any-group movement**: [Yukon](/yukon) is the most permissive — you can pick up any face-up card and everything on top of it, regardless of whether those cards form a valid sequence. This makes Yukon feel chaotic and freeing compared to FreeCell's precise logic.

**Limited moves**: Some games restrict the total number of moves or redeals. Klondike Draw-3 typically allows 1-3 passes through the stock. [Clock](/clock) gives you exactly 4 chances per rank position.

## The Design Space

These six dimensions create a vast design space. Consider just the binary choices: open vs. closed information, alternating vs. same-suit building, free cells vs. stock pile, any-card vs. Kings-only empty columns. That's already 16 possible combinations — and most dimensions have more than two options.

This is why there are over 500 solitaire variants and why new ones keep being invented. The design space is enormous, and many interesting corners remain unexplored.

Your preferences across these dimensions predict which games you'll enjoy:

The beauty of solitaire's rule-variation system is that there's a game for every mood, every skill level, and every amount of time. Understanding *why* games feel different — not just *that* they feel different — helps you navigate this enormous family of games and find the ones that fit you perfectly.
