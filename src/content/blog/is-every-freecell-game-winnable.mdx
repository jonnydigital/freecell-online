It's one of the most common questions in the solitaire world: can you win every FreeCell game? The short answer is **almost** — but not quite. And the story behind those rare impossible deals is one of the most fascinating in gaming history.

## The Numbers

Of the original 32,000 deals in Microsoft FreeCell (numbered 1 through 32,000), exactly **one** was proven unsolvable: **Deal #11982**.

When researchers extended the range to 1 million deals, they found **8 unsolvable deals** total:
- #11982
- #146692
- #186216
- #455889
- #495505
- #512118
- #517776
- #781948

That's a solvability rate of **99.9992%**. For every million random FreeCell deals, roughly 8 are impossible. The rest can all be won with perfect play.

## How Do We Know?

In the early 2000s, a group of dedicated FreeCell enthusiasts calling themselves the "FreeCell Project" systematically attempted to solve every deal in the original Microsoft range. They used a combination of human play and computer solvers to work through all 32,000 games.

Deal #11982 was the holdout. After thousands of attempts by both humans and algorithms, nobody could crack it. Eventually, exhaustive computer search — checking every possible sequence of moves — proved it mathematically impossible.

The proof isn't just "we couldn't find a solution." It's "we checked every possible path and none of them lead to a win." That's the difference between unproven and proven impossible.

## What Makes a Deal Unsolvable?

Unsolvable FreeCell deals typically share characteristics:

**Circular dependencies.** Card A needs to move, but it's blocked by Card B, which is blocked by Card C, which is blocked by Card A. No amount of free cell shuffling can break the cycle.

**Critical cards deeply buried.** When low-rank cards (Aces, 2s, 3s) are trapped at the bottom of cascades behind cards that need the buried cards to move first.

**Insufficient workspace.** The combination of 4 free cells and 8 cascades simply doesn't provide enough temporary storage to untangle the specific card arrangement.

In Deal #11982, the Aces are deeply buried, and the cards above them form interlocking dependencies that no sequence of moves can resolve.

## Why Does This Matter?

FreeCell's near-perfect solvability is what makes it special. When you lose a FreeCell game, it's almost always your mistake — not bad luck. The deal was solvable; you just didn't find the path.

Compare this to Klondike solitaire, where roughly 18% of deals are outright impossible. In Klondike, losing might mean you played badly, or it might mean you never had a chance. You can't tell the difference, which makes it harder to learn from your losses.

FreeCell's transparency is both its gift and its challenge. You know the solution exists. You know you should be able to find it. When you don't, the failure is yours to own — and yours to learn from.
