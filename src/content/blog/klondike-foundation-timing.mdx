Most Klondike players learn to move cards to the foundation as quickly as possible. It feels like progress. The foundation piles grow, the tableau looks cleaner, and the game seems to be going well.

Then, two minutes later, you are stuck. A 4 and a 5 sit in the foundation that you needed in the tableau to build a sequence. The game is lost because you removed cards you should have kept.

Foundation timing is one of the quieter skills in Klondike, but it decides more games than most players realize.

## Key Takeaways

- Aces and 2s are always safe to send to the foundation. No sequence-building in Klondike uses them.
- 3s are usually safe, with one exception you can check in a few seconds.
- Cards ranked 4 and above should stay in the tableau until you verify the card one rank lower, opposite color, is visible.
- A card in the foundation cannot come back. In Klondike, that is final.
- The [Klondike cheat sheet](/klondike-cheat-sheet) has a quick-reference version of these rules.

## Why the Foundation Feels Safe and Isn't

In FreeCell, sending a card to the foundation early almost never hurts you. The game gives you free cells to manage everything in view. Klondike is different. Many of your cards are still face-down, and you do not know what is coming.

When you send a 6♠ to the foundation, you lose it as a build target. If a red 5 comes up later and has nowhere to go, it is wasted. The card you sent up turned a future move into a dead end.

The foundation is permanent in Klondike. Cards cannot return to the tableau. Every foundation play is a one-way door, so the question to ask before each one is: could I still need this card in the tableau?

## Aces and 2s: Always Safe

Send Aces and 2s to the foundation the moment you see them. There is no sequence in Klondike that uses an Ace or a 2 as a build target. The only place they belong is up.

The one edge case: in a position where you need to keep an Ace or 2 in the tableau to enable a face-down flip this turn, you might delay by one move. In practice, you will almost never see this. Move them up.

## 3s: Usually Safe

A 3 can be used as a build target for a 2, but since 2s go to the foundation immediately, no 2 should ever be looking for a tableau home. That means a 3 in the tableau does almost nothing that an empty space wouldn't do better.

Send 3s to the foundation unless you see a specific reason to keep one. That reason would be: a 2 of the matching color is face-down somewhere and might come up needing somewhere to land. This situation is uncommon and gets rarer as the game progresses.

## The 4-and-Above Rule

This is where most players go wrong.

Before sending any card ranked 4 or higher to the foundation, check whether the card **one rank lower, opposite color** is visible in the tableau.

Here is why. If you send red 7 to the foundation, you eliminate it as a landing spot. A black 6 now has one fewer place it can go. If that black 6 is still face-down, you may have just blocked your own game.

The check is fast:

- Before sending 7♥ up, look for 6♠ or 6♣ in the tableau. If neither is visible, hold the 7♥.
- Before sending 9♠ up, look for 8♥ or 8♦. Not visible? Wait.

If the card one rank below is already on the foundation, in a visible tableau pile, or provably accessible from the stock, you are clear to send yours up. If not, keep it for now.

For deeper cards like 8s, 9s, and 10s, apply the same check with more patience. Higher-ranked cards serve as tableau anchors longer than low ones do.

## Sending Up in Sequence Matters

The foundation requires cards in exact sequence: A, 2, 3, 4, and so on per suit. You cannot send a 6 up until the 5 of the same suit is already there.

This means foundation timing is sometimes constrained by the sequence itself. But the danger runs in the other direction: sending a card up too early in the sequence before lower cards of similar rank are safely placed elsewhere.

For example, if you send 5♥ to the foundation but 4♠ and 4♣ are both still buried face-down, every red 3 that surfaces is now looking for a black 4 that is not available. You made two future flips harder with one premature foundation play.

## A Simple Decision Sequence

When a card becomes eligible for the foundation, run through this in order:

1. Is it an Ace or 2? Send it immediately.
2. Is it a 3? Probably send it. Glance for buried 2s if you want to be cautious.
3. Is it a 4 or higher? Find the card one rank lower, opposite color. Is it visible or already in the foundation? If yes, send yours. If no, wait.

This takes about three seconds per card. Over the course of a game, those three seconds consistently prevent the mistake that ends most winnable deals.

## Foundation Timing and Column Digging Work Together

There is a second layer to this. The sooner you send low cards up, the fewer landing spots exist in the tableau. That matters most when you are trying to flip face-down cards in the deep right-side columns.

If you need to move a red 4 off a buried card to reveal it, that red 4 needs a black 5 to land on. If your only black 5 is already on the foundation, the flip is blocked. The [column excavation guide](/blog/klondike-which-column-to-dig-first) covers how to prioritize flips, and foundation timing is the quiet constraint running underneath all of it.

The practical habit: when you flip a face-down card and get something low, do not send it to the foundation just because you can. Check what it might be needed for first. The goal is not a tall foundation. It is a finished game.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is it ever wrong to send Aces to the foundation immediately?

Rarely. In a position where you need an Ace to stay in the tableau to unlock a face-down card this turn, you might delay by one move. This almost never happens in practice. Move Aces up without hesitation.

### How do I know if a card is buried?

A buried card is face-down anywhere in the tableau, or unaccounted for across stock cycles. If you are playing [Draw 3](/klondike/draw-1-vs-draw-3) and have not seen a card in multiple passes, treat it as buried until proven otherwise.

### Does this strategy change between Draw 1 and Draw 3?

The foundation timing rules are the same. Draw 1 gives you full stock access, which makes it easier to verify where low cards are. In Draw 3, you see less of the stock on any given pass, so the cost of a premature foundation play is higher.

### Can I undo a foundation play?

In most online versions of Klondike, yes. Undo is available. But relying on undo to reverse bad foundation plays is not the same as developing the habit of checking first. Use undo to learn from mistakes, not as a substitute for the three-second check. You can [play Klondike free](/klondike) with full undo enabled.

### Does every lost Klondike game come down to foundation timing?

No. Some deals are statistically unwinnable, and some losses come from missed tableau moves. Foundation timing is the mistake category that catches most intermediate players because it is invisible in the moment. The harm shows up four or five moves later, by which point the real cause is hard to trace.
