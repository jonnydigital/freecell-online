When someone says "I'm playing Solitaire," they almost always mean Klondike. It's the version that shipped with Windows 3.0 in 1990 and quietly became the most-played card game in human history. Billions of games have been played on computers alone — and that's before counting the centuries of play with physical decks.

But despite its familiarity, most players have never learned the actual name of the game they're playing, let alone the strategy behind it. This guide covers everything: what Klondike actually is, how it differs from other solitaire variants, and how to meaningfully improve your win rate.

## What Is Klondike Solitaire?

Klondike is a single-player card game played with a standard 52-card deck. The goal is to move all cards to four foundation piles, building each suit from Ace through King.

The layout consists of:

- **7 tableau columns** — dealt left to right with increasing card counts (1 card in the first column, 2 in the second, up to 7 in the last). Only the top card of each column is face-up.
- **A stock pile** — the remaining 24 cards, dealt face-down.
- **A waste pile** — where cards from the stock are turned over.
- **4 foundation piles** — where you build each suit from Ace to King.

Cards on the tableau are built in descending order, alternating colors. A red 9 goes on a black 10. Only Kings can be placed in empty tableau columns.

## Draw 1 vs Draw 3: The Big Decision

The single biggest variable in Klondike is whether you're drawing one card at a time from the stock or three.

**Draw 1 (Turn 1)** turns over one card at a time. You cycle through the entire stock and can access every card on every pass. This is the more forgiving mode, and it's where most players should start. Win rates with solid play sit around 80-85%.

**Draw 3 (Turn 3)** turns over three cards at a time, but you can only play the top card of each group. This means roughly two-thirds of the stock is inaccessible on any given pass. Win rates drop to around 30-40% even with strong play. Draw 3 is a fundamentally different challenge — it demands much more careful planning around which cards you choose to uncover and when.

The strategic differences go deep enough that we wrote a dedicated breakdown: [Draw 1 vs Draw 3 — What Actually Changes](/klondike/draw-1-vs-draw-3).

The short version: Draw 1 is a logic puzzle where good play almost always wins. Draw 3 is a strategic challenge where good play improves your odds but can't guarantee victory.

## Core Klondike Strategy

Even experienced players leave wins on the table by following instinct rather than strategy. A few principles make a significant difference:

### Uncover Face-Down Cards First

Your top priority should always be revealing hidden cards. A face-down card is a locked resource — you can't plan around what you can't see. When choosing between two roughly equal moves, pick the one that flips a face-down card.

### Don't Rush Cards to the Foundation

This is the most common mistake. Moving a red 4 to the foundation feels productive, but if you later need that red 4 on the tableau to uncover a buried card, you've created an unsolvable position. In Klondike, unlike FreeCell, you typically can't pull cards back off the foundation.

Build foundations evenly. If your hearts are at 6 but your spades are at 2, slow down on hearts and work on catching up.

### Manage Empty Columns Carefully

Empty tableau columns are powerful — but only if you fill them with Kings. Since only Kings can go in empty spaces, creating a gap when you have no King to place there just wastes a column.

Before clearing a column, ask: do I have a King ready to go in? If not, is clearing it still worth it to uncover face-down cards? Usually the answer is no.

### Stock Pile Awareness

In Draw 3, pay attention to the order of cards in the stock. The sequence doesn't change between passes, so you can predict which cards will be accessible and plan your tableau moves accordingly. This is the single biggest skill gap between casual and strong Draw 3 players.

## How Klondike Differs from FreeCell and Spider

All three are solitaire games. All three involve building tableau columns and moving cards to foundations. But the actual gameplay couldn't be more different.

### Klondike vs FreeCell

The biggest difference: **hidden information**. In Klondike, most cards start face-down. You're making decisions with incomplete information and managing uncertainty. In [FreeCell](/freecell), all 52 cards are visible from the first move. Every FreeCell game is a pure logic puzzle with a knowable solution.

FreeCell also gives you four free cells — temporary storage spaces that don't exist in Klondike. This makes FreeCell more forgiving of mistakes (you can park cards temporarily) but demands deeper planning since there's no luck to bail you out.

If you enjoy the planning aspect of Klondike but want to eliminate the luck factor entirely, [give FreeCell a try](/freecell).

### Klondike vs Spider

[Spider Solitaire](/spider) uses two full decks (104 cards) and 10 tableau columns. The goal is to build complete King-to-Ace sequences of a single suit, which are then removed from play.

Spider is generally considered harder than Klondike, especially at [higher difficulty levels](/spider/how-to-play). Where Klondike has one mode variable (draw count), Spider has three distinct difficulty settings based on how many suits are in play.

The strategic texture is different too. Spider rewards building long in-suit sequences and managing a much larger tableau. Klondike rewards efficient uncovering of hidden cards and careful stock management.

## Quick-Reference Tips

Looking for something actionable right now? Here are five tips you can apply in your next game:

1. **Always play an Ace or Deuce to the foundation immediately** — there's no scenario where keeping a 2 on the tableau helps you.
2. **Prefer moves that reveal face-down cards** over moves that just reorganize visible cards.
3. **Build foundations evenly** — don't get one suit to 8 while another is stuck at Ace.
4. **In Draw 3, count the stock** — know what's coming before you commit to tableau moves.
5. **Don't fill empty columns without a King** unless it's your only path to uncovering a critical card.

## Why Klondike Endures

Klondike has outlasted every digital trend since 1990. It survived the transition from desktop to mobile, from physical cards to touchscreens, from minutes-long play sessions to seconds-long ones. The reason is simple: it hits a sweet spot between skill and chance that keeps games interesting without making them punishing.

Draw 1 Klondike is relaxing and almost always winnable. Draw 3 is a genuine strategic challenge. And the rules are simple enough that anyone can learn them in two minutes.
