An empty tableau column in Klondike Solitaire is called a space. Unlike most solitaire games, Klondike puts a hard restriction on spaces: only a King can start a new sequence there. That rule turns a simple empty column into one of the most consequential decisions in the game.

Creating a space costs moves. Using one badly wastes those moves. Using one well can unlock a sequence of plays that was otherwise impossible.

## Key Takeaways

- A space is an empty tableau column. Only a King can begin a new sequence in one under standard Klondike rules.
- Creating a space takes work. Clear the column by moving cards onto existing sequences or cycling through stock until you can empty it.
- Don't rush to fill a space. An open space is more valuable than a space with the wrong King in it.
- The best King to move in is the one with the most cards already available to build on it, not whichever King is closest.
- Check the [Klondike cheat sheet](/klondike-cheat-sheet) for a quick reference on when spaces and foundation moves interact.

## What a Space Actually Gives You

Without a space, every card must land on an existing sequence. That limits your options considerably. A 7 can only go on a red 8 or a black 8 depending on its color. A King has nowhere to go at all.

With a space, you gain one slot that holds any card temporarily. You can move a sequence there while you rearrange a buried column, then move it back. A well-timed space unlocks three or four moves that were otherwise stuck. A space filled with the wrong card just shrinks your tableau.

## When to Work Toward a Space

Not every position needs a space. Early in the game, when face-down cards still fill most columns, spaces are often premature. Your priority is flipping those hidden cards, and clearing an entire column to create a space takes more moves than the payoff justifies.

Spaces become worth pursuing when:

- A sequence you need to move has nowhere to go, and a space would give it a temporary home.
- Two columns need to swap cards and there is no legal direct path between them.
- You have a buried face-down card that you cannot reach without first moving a long run out of the way.
- The opening phase is over, most face-down cards are revealed, and board flexibility becomes the bottleneck.

If none of these apply, hold the space idea. Premature column-clearing is a common mistake in the mid-game. For a structured approach to which columns to work on first, see the [column digging guide](/blog/klondike-which-column-to-dig-first).

## Which King to Move Into a Space

This is where most players underperform. Any King can go in a space, but not all Kings are equal.

The King worth moving in first is the one that immediately attracts cards you already have. If a red Queen is visible in the tableau, a black King is the right call. If a black Queen is available, a red King fits. You want the new sequence to start building immediately.

A King that enters a space and sits there alone for three turns has not earned its spot. It is occupying real estate that could be used for temporary storage.

The second factor: check whether the King you're considering is currently blocking something. If a King is sitting on top of a face-down card you need to flip, moving it to a space does two jobs: it clears the blocked flip and starts a new sequence.

If neither factor applies and you are choosing between Kings purely by color, the tie-breaker is suit preference for foundation purposes. Look at which suits are lagging in your foundation piles and give that suit's King the space.

## When to Leave a Space Empty

This is the move most players never make. You create a space, and the impulse is to fill it immediately. Resist that impulse.

An empty space has more flexibility than a space with a King in it. An empty space can hold any card, even temporarily. You can move a sequence into it, take a card from the other side of it, and move it back.

Before dropping a King in, ask:

- Is there a card that needs to move right now and has nowhere else to go?
- Can I use the space for one temporary move, then fill it with the right King?
- Is a buried card I need reachable in the next two moves if I use the space for routing instead of filling?

If any of those is yes, use the space first and fill it after. [Klondike opening strategy](/klondike-opening-strategy) covers a related principle: don't commit to a structure before you know what you're committing to.

## Spaces in Draw 1 vs. Draw 3

In Draw 1, creating a space is easier. Full stock access means you can usually draw the card you need to finish clearing a column. Spaces appear more often and can be used more freely.

In Draw 3, spaces are rarer and more expensive. You see only a third of the stock per pass, so clearing a column may take multiple full cycles. When you do create a space in Draw 3, treat it carefully. The [Draw 3 cycle guide](/blog/klondike-draw-3-cycle-math) explains how stock access limits your options and why spaces become more critical as a result.

## Spaces and Foundation Timing

One interaction trips up intermediate players. When you send a card to the foundation, check whether that play leaves any tableau card with nowhere valid to land. If yes, use the space as temporary storage for that stranded card rather than filling it with a King right now. The [foundation timing guide](/blog/klondike-foundation-timing) covers which cards are safe to send up and which need to stay in the tableau longer.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can any card go in an empty column in Klondike?

In standard Klondike, no. Only Kings can begin a new sequence in an empty tableau column. Some variant rules allow any card to fill a space, but the classic game restricts it to Kings. Check the specific rules for whichever version you're playing.

### What if I have an empty space but no King is accessible?

Use the space for temporary storage. Move a partial sequence into it to clear access to a buried card, complete the flip, then move the sequence back. You lose the space as a permanent slot but gain a flip, which is almost always the better trade.

### Is clearing a full column to make a space always worth it?

Not always. Clearing a column costs moves and can disrupt existing sequences. The payoff needs to justify the cost. If you can see a clear plan that uses the space for two or three useful moves immediately, the trade is worth it. If you are clearing a column hoping something useful will happen later, it probably isn't.

### Does the space strategy change at the end of the game?

In the endgame, spaces matter less because you are mostly sending cards straight to the foundation. The [Klondike endgame strategy](/klondike-endgame-strategy) page covers when to stop managing the tableau and start clearing toward the win condition. By that point, most tableau decisions resolve themselves quickly.
