Vegas Klondike is the only version of the game where you can quantify exactly how badly you're losing. You start $52 in the hole. Each card you place on a foundation pays $5. To break even, you need 11 cards on the foundation. To profit, you need 21 or more, which means getting more than a third of the deck cleared. Most games don't get there.

That's the honest version of Vegas scoring, and it's worth understanding before you play, because it changes how you think about every move.

## Key Takeaways

- Vegas Klondike starts you at negative $52 and pays $5 per foundation card placed
- Break-even requires 11 foundation cards. A full win pays $208 net profit ($260 minus the $52 buy-in)
- Most Vegas games are played Draw-3 with a single pass through the stock, which makes the already-hard game significantly harder
- The "expected value" of a Vegas Klondike game is negative, which is why it was a casino game
- Strategy shifts in Vegas mode: maximize cards moved, not just wins

## The Actual Payout Table

The Vegas scoring formula is simple:

- **Buy-in:** -$52 (paid upfront, before the first card is moved)
- **Per foundation card:** +$5
- **Maximum payout:** $260 (52 cards × $5)
- **Net maximum profit:** $208 ($260 minus the $52 buy-in)

To see the math at each milestone:

| Foundation cards moved | Gross payout | Net position |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | $0 | -$52 |
| 11 | $55 | +$3 (break-even zone) |
| 20 | $100 | +$48 |
| 30 | $150 | +$98 |
| 40 | $200 | +$148 |
| 52 | $260 | +$208 |

The break-even point (11 cards) is lower than most players expect. Getting 11 cards to the foundation means having roughly two complete suits, or a smattering across all four. That's an achievable goal even in a mediocre game.

The full win ($208 net) requires clearing every card, which happens in Draw-3 with a single pass in roughly 5 to 10 percent of games, depending on play quality.

## Why Vegas Rules Use Draw-3 and Single Pass

The version of Klondike actually offered in Las Vegas casinos used Draw-3 with a single pass through the stock, no recycling. Some house rules allowed three passes instead of one, but the default was one.

This combination isn't arbitrary. Draw-3 with unlimited passes can be won by a skilled player roughly 35 percent of the time. A single pass through the stock drops that ceiling to around 10 to 15 percent even with optimal play. The house needs an edge, and the single-pass rule provides it.

What this means practically: in true Vegas rules, you will often run out of stock with useful tableau moves still available. Cards you need are buried and you have no more draws. That's the constraint you're optimizing against.

**The single-pass game is fundamentally about stock ordering.** The sequence of cards in your stock is your entire supply. Once you've seen each card once and it's sitting unplayed in the waste pile, it's gone. Plan accordingly.

## How Vegas Scoring Changes Strategy

Standard Klondike strategy optimizes for wins. Vegas scoring optimizes for foundation cards placed, which is a related but different objective.

In standard scoring, a game where you place 8 foundation cards and lose is just a loss. In Vegas scoring, placing 8 foundation cards means you're at -$12, which is a better outcome than placing 0 and staying at -$52. The partial result has real value.

This changes a few specific decisions:

**Foundation play becomes more aggressive.** In standard Klondike, you often hold back mid-rank cards from the foundation to keep flexibility on the tableau. In Vegas mode, the $5 per card is real. If a card can go to the foundation and holding it back won't clearly unlock more cards, send it up.

**Stock management is critical in single-pass.** You have exactly one chance to see each card. Before drawing from the stock, check whether any tableau move would reorder which card comes up next. In a single-pass game, you never get a second look.

**Empty columns matter more.** Creating an empty column in Vegas mode is valuable because it lets you shelter a card from the stock temporarily. With no recycling, you can't wait for a better moment. An empty column buys you one extra card's worth of flexibility.

## The Expected Value Question

If this were an actual casino game with $52 buy-in and $5-per-card payout, what would the expected value be per game?

With Draw-3 single-pass and average play (roughly 8 to 12 foundation cards per game on average, depending on skill), the expected payout sits around $40 to $60 gross, against a $52 buy-in. Expected value is roughly -$2 to -$12 per game for a decent player.

For comparison, a skilled poker player can achieve a positive expected value. Vegas Klondike, by design, cannot. The single-pass restriction ensures the house retains an edge even against strong play.

This is not a criticism of the format. It's the math that makes Vegas Klondike interesting as a scoring challenge. You know the odds are against you. The question is how close to break-even you can get.

## Playing the Single-Pass Game

Since you only see each stock card once, here is the priority order that maximizes expected foundation cards:

1. **Play any Ace or Two from the waste pile immediately.** These are low-risk foundation plays and they're gone if you don't use them.

2. **Before drawing, check tableau for moves that change the waste pile position.** If a useful card is one position behind the current waste card, one tableau move might expose it.

3. **Use empty columns as parking spots.** In a single-pass game, parking a card temporarily to reach what's behind it is often correct.

4. **Delay mid-rank tableau moves** if making them would advance the waste pile past a card you're waiting for. Sometimes not moving is the right move.

5. **Accept the outcome.** Some single-pass games are over in 20 draws. The stock sequence determines your ceiling, and there's no second chance.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What does the $52 buy-in represent in Vegas Klondike?

It represents a standard deck of 52 cards. The historical framing was that you were "buying" each card in the deck for $1. At $5 per card sent to the foundation, you needed 11 cards just to recover your initial stake. The buy-in varies in some versions (a $1 buy-in with $5 payout produces different expected values), but $52/$5 is the traditional ratio.

### Can you actually play Vegas Klondike in real casinos today?

It's rare. The game was offered at some Las Vegas casinos in the 1990s but largely disappeared as table minimums made it unprofitable to staff. The format lives on in digital scoring systems. If you find a casino offering it today, it's almost certainly a tourist attraction rather than a serious gaming offering.

### Is Vegas scoring available on standard Klondike apps?

Many digital Klondike implementations offer a Vegas scoring mode, but they often use three passes through the stock rather than one. True single-pass Vegas is less common digitally because it produces short games that frustrate casual players. Check the settings of your specific app to see what the pass limit is.

### What's the highest possible Vegas Klondike score?

The maximum is $208 net (placing all 52 cards, earning $260 against the $52 buy-in). In Draw-3 single-pass, this requires both a favorable stock sequence and strong play. It's rare but achievable.

### How does Vegas scoring compare to the Windows-style points system?

They measure different things. Windows scoring rewards individual actions (flipping a card, moving to the foundation) and penalizes recycling the waste pile. Vegas scoring measures only the final outcome: how many cards reached the foundation. Vegas scoring is cleaner for evaluating overall game quality. Windows scoring is more granular and rewards correct technique throughout.
