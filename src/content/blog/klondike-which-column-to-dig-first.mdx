A fresh Klondike deal hides 21 cards face-down across the seven tableau columns. Column 1 hides nothing. Column 7 hides six cards. Almost every game you lose, you lose because the card you needed stayed buried under one of those piles. So the real strategic question in Klondike is not "what can I move?" It is "what should I uncover next?"

## Key Takeaways

- A move that flips a face-down card beats a move that only rearranges face-up cards, almost every time.
- Start digging the deep right-side columns (5, 6, and 7) early, while you still have room to maneuver.
- When two moves each flip a card, favor the column with more cards still buried under it.
- Do not empty a column unless you have a King ready to take the space.
- The stock is a supplement, not a plan. Tableau flips win games.

## The Deal Is Lopsided on Purpose

Klondike's opening layout stacks the hidden information on the right side of the board:

| Column | Face-down cards |
|--------|----------------|
| 1 | 0 |
| 2 | 1 |
| 3 | 2 |
| 4 | 3 |
| 5 | 4 |
| 6 | 5 |
| 7 | 6 |

Columns 5 through 7 hold 15 of the 21 hidden cards. That is where your missing Aces, low cards, and blocking cards usually live. A player who spends the whole early game picking at columns 1 through 3 is working the shallow end of the pool while the game is decided in the deep end.

## Flip First, Build Second

Every turn, you face the same quiet choice: make a move that reveals a face-down card, or make a move that just tidies up your face-up cards.

Take the flip. New information changes what is possible. A tidy tableau does not.

There are exceptions, and they matter. Do not flip a card if the move sends something to the foundation that the tableau still needs. Our list of [Klondike beginner mistakes](/blog/klondike-beginner-mistakes) covers that trap in detail: the short version is, keep low cards available for tableau building until the cards one rank below them are visible.

## When Two Flips Are Available, Dig the Deeper Pile

Say you can play a red 8 onto either of two black 9s, and each move would flip a face-down card. Which black 9 do you choose?

Favor the column with more cards still buried beneath it. Here is the reasoning. A column with five face-down cards needs five separate uncovering moves before it is fully open, and each of those moves requires the right face-up cards to be in place at the right time. That gets harder as the game goes on and your options shrink. A column hiding only one card can usually be opened whenever you like.

Start the long excavation while your mobility is high. Save the easy digs for later, when you may need a quick flip to keep the game alive.

## Empty Columns Are Only Worth Something With a King in Hand

Clearing column 1 or 2 early feels productive. You get an open space, and open spaces are powerful in most solitaire games.

In Klondike, only a King can move into an empty column. If no King is available, that empty space does nothing. Worse, you spent moves creating it that could have gone toward flipping cards elsewhere.

Before you race to empty a short column, check: is there a King face-up in the tableau or findable in the stock? Is it a King you actually want, meaning one whose matching Queen is reachable? If not, leave the short column alone and put your moves into the deep piles.

## How the Stock Fits Into a Digging Plan

The stock holds 24 cards, and in [Draw 3 mode](/klondike/draw-1-vs-draw-3) you only see one out of every three on each pass. Beginners treat the stock as the main source of new cards and the tableau as a place to park them. That is backwards.

Stock cards are most valuable when they unlock tableau flips. A 6♠ from the stock is not exciting because it exists. It is exciting if placing it on a red 7 lets you move a red 5 next turn and flip the card underneath. When you decide whether to play a stock card, ask what it uncovers, not just whether it fits.

If you are cycling the stock repeatedly and nothing on the tableau has flipped in several passes, stop and re-scan the board. Either you are missing a move, or the deal is one of the minority that cannot be won, which we cover in our [Klondike probability guide](/klondike-probability).

## A Simple Priority Order for Any Position

When it is not obvious what to do, work down this list and take the first move that exists:

1. Flip a face-down card in a deep column (4 or more still buried).
2. Flip a face-down card anywhere else.
3. Play a stock card that sets up a flip on your next move.
4. Send a card to the foundation, if the tableau no longer needs it.
5. Rearrange face-up cards only when it creates a landing spot you will use.

This is not a solver. It is a habit. Players who follow it stop losing games to buried cards they never tried to reach. For a printable one-page version of these priorities, see the [Klondike cheat sheet](/klondike-cheat-sheet).

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Should I always dig the biggest pile first?

Early in the game, yes, favor the deep columns. Late in the game, flip whatever you can. The priority flips because late-game mobility is scarce, and any new card can restart a stalled position.

### Is it bad to move a King to an empty column right away?

Move the King only if its Queen is visible or likely reachable. A King parked on an empty column with no Queen behind it just rebuilds the wall you tore down.

### Does this change in Draw 1 vs Draw 3?

The digging priorities stay the same. Draw 1 gives you full access to the stock, so tableau flips and stock play blend more freely. In Draw 3, the stock is unreliable, which makes tableau excavation even more important. See our [Draw 1 vs Draw 3 comparison](/klondike/draw-1-vs-draw-3) for the full breakdown.

### How do I practice this?

Play a few deals where you only allow yourself moves from the priority list above. It feels restrictive for the first game or two, then it becomes automatic. You can [play Klondike free](/klondike) with unlimited undo, which makes it easy to test a line, back up, and try the excavation in a different order.
