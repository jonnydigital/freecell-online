Solitaire is the most-played computer game in history. Not Tetris, not Minecraft, not Fortnite — solitaire. Microsoft's version alone has been installed on over a billion computers. But the game didn't start on a screen. Its roots stretch back centuries, through drawing rooms, prison cells, and at least one very famous French emperor.

Here's how a simple card game became a global phenomenon — twice.

## Origins: Europe, Probably the 1700s

The exact origin of solitaire is frustratingly unclear. The earliest reliable references appear in the late 18th century, in Scandinavian and German texts. A Swedish book from 1783 mentions a card game played alone as a form of fortune-telling — laying out cards to see what patterns emerged, with the outcome interpreted as an omen.

The game likely evolved from these fortune-telling exercises into a structured challenge: could you get all the cards into order? The shift from divination to game probably happened gradually, and in multiple places at once.

By the early 1800s, solitaire card games were well-established across Northern Europe. They appeared in French game anthologies and German puzzle collections. The rules varied wildly — dozens of distinct games existed under the broad umbrella of "playing cards alone."

## Patience vs Solitaire: A Naming Split

If you've played solitaire outside of North America, you've probably heard it called "Patience." This isn't a different game — it's the same family of games with a different label.

**Patience** is the term used across most of Europe, including Britain, France, and Scandinavia. The French called it *la patience* or *la réussite* (the success). The name emphasizes the temperament required: careful, unhurried play.

**Solitaire** is the North American term, derived from the French word for "alone" or "solitary." In Europe, "solitaire" more commonly refers to the peg-jumping board game (also known as peg solitaire), which can create confusion.

Both names describe the same thing: card games designed for a single player. This guide uses "solitaire" since that's what most of our readers know, but if you see "Patience" in older texts or British sources, it's the same world of games.

## The Napoleon Connection

No history of solitaire is complete without mentioning Napoleon Bonaparte — though historians argue about how much of the connection is real.

The popular story goes like this: after his final defeat at Waterloo in 1815, Napoleon was exiled to the island of Saint Helena in the South Atlantic. With little to do and few companions, he supposedly spent hours playing solitaire card games to pass the time. Several solitaire variants carry his name — "Napoleon at St. Helena," "Napoleon's Favorite," and others.

The problem is that the evidence is thin. Contemporary accounts of Napoleon's exile mention card games, but they don't always specify solitaire. Some historians believe the Napoleon-solitaire connection was embellished or invented by later writers who wanted to add romantic drama to their game books.

What's more likely: Napoleon played various card games during his exile, some of which may have been solitaire variants. The association stuck because it made for a great story — the fallen emperor, alone on a remote island, playing cards against himself. Whether he played the specific games that bear his name is anyone's guess.

Regardless of Napoleon's actual habits, the connection helped popularize solitaire across 19th-century Europe. If it was good enough for an emperor, it was good enough for everyone else. For more on the game's deeper past, see our [history of solitaire](/history) page.

## The Victorian Era: Solitaire Goes Mainstream

Solitaire hit its first peak of popularity in the mid-to-late 1800s. Queen Victoria and Prince Albert were reportedly enthusiastic players. The game became a fixture in Victorian parlors — respectable, quiet, and suitable for playing alone during the long evenings.

This era produced the first published collections of solitaire rules. Lady Adelaide Cadogan's *Illustrated Games of Patience* (1870) was one of the earliest English-language solitaire books and described dozens of distinct games. These books codified the rules that had previously been passed down informally, giving games standardized names and procedures for the first time.

It was during this period that many of the variants we know today took their recognizable forms. [Klondike](/klondike), the game most people simply call "Solitaire," likely crystallized into its modern rules sometime in the late 19th century — though its exact origin remains debated.

## Windows Solitaire: The Game That Taught the World to Click

Everything changed on May 22, 1990, when Microsoft shipped Windows 3.0. Bundled with the operating system was a simple Klondike solitaire game, written by a Microsoft intern named Wes Cherry.

Here's the part most people don't know: **Windows Solitaire wasn't included for entertainment. It was designed to teach people how to use a mouse.**

In 1990, most computer users were accustomed to keyboard-driven interfaces. The graphical user interface was new, and the mouse was unfamiliar. Dragging and dropping — the core interaction of Windows — was a foreign concept to millions of users. Solitaire taught drag-and-drop intuitively: pick up a card, move it somewhere, let go. No instruction manual needed.

It worked spectacularly. People who were intimidated by their new computers would sit down to play a quick game of solitaire and inadvertently teach themselves the fundamental skills of the graphical interface. The game was so effective as a training tool that it remained bundled with Windows for decades.

Wes Cherry, incidentally, never received royalties for the game. As a Microsoft intern, his work belonged to the company. He later said he was fine with it — he was just happy people enjoyed playing.

## FreeCell: The Thinking Person's Solitaire

[FreeCell](/freecell) joined the Windows lineup in 1995, bundled with Windows 95. The version was created by Jim Horne, a Microsoft employee and longtime FreeCell enthusiast who had been playing the game since the 1970s.

FreeCell was different from Klondike in a crucial way: all cards were dealt face-up. Nothing was hidden. This transformed solitaire from a game of luck into a game of pure logic. The four free cells — temporary storage spaces that gave the game its name — provided just enough flexibility to make nearly every deal solvable.

Horne's implementation included 32,000 numbered deals, and players set out to solve every single one. This community effort, spanning years of collaborative work, eventually determined that Deal #11982 was the only impossible game in the set. Out of 32,000 deals, 31,999 were winnable. That near-perfect solvability rate became part of FreeCell's identity and appeal.

The game attracted a dedicated following of players who approached it as a serious logic puzzle. Win streaks of hundreds or even thousands of games became a point of pride. You can read more about this in our article on [FreeCell and Microsoft's legacy](/microsoft-freecell).

## Spider Solitaire: Windows 98's Hidden Gem

[Spider Solitaire](/spider) arrived with Windows 98 (and later received an updated version in Windows ME and XP). Using two full decks and 10 tableau columns, Spider was significantly more complex than either Klondike or FreeCell.

Spider's three difficulty levels — [1-suit, 2-suit, and 4-suit](/spider/1-suit-vs-2-suit-vs-4-suit) — gave it unusual range. The 1-suit version was relaxing and approachable. The 4-suit version was one of the hardest solitaire variants most players would ever encounter. This scalability helped Spider build a massive player base that included both casual players and serious enthusiasts.

With Klondike, FreeCell, and Spider all shipping in Windows, Microsoft had accidentally created the most-played game suite in history. Office workers, retirees, students — hundreds of millions of people played these games daily.

## The Mobile and Browser Era

The rise of smartphones in the late 2000s brought solitaire to a new platform. App stores filled with solitaire games almost immediately — it was a natural fit for touchscreens and short play sessions. Solitaire became one of the most downloaded game categories on both iOS and Android.

Browser-based solitaire followed a similar trajectory. As web technologies improved, developers built solitaire games that ran directly in the browser with no download required. Sites like this one — [PlayFreeCellOnline.com](/) — carry forward the tradition of free, accessible solitaire that you can play anywhere.

The core appeal hasn't changed since those Victorian parlors: a single player, a deck of cards, and a puzzle to solve. The technology evolved from physical cards to pixels to touchscreens, but the satisfaction of clearing the board remains exactly the same.

## Three Centuries and Counting

Solitaire has survived because it solves a timeless problem: how to entertain yourself with nothing but a deck of cards and a few minutes to spare. It doesn't need an opponent, doesn't need equipment beyond the cards, and scales from mindless relaxation to genuine mental challenge depending on the variant.

From anonymous European card tables to Napoleon's exile to Wes Cherry's intern project to your browser right now, solitaire has been quietly adapting to whatever medium people use. It was here before computers. It will be here after whatever comes next.

The game endures because the game works. It always has.
