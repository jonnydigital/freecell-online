Solitaire players know when a game feels impossible. But "feels impossible" and "actually is impossible" are different things, and the difference matters. A game that's genuinely unsolvable should be abandoned immediately. A game that merely feels unsolvable might be 20 moves from a win.

The mathematics of solvability tells you which is which. And it explains why game designers keep returning to a small set of structural choices that determine whether a solitaire variant is a logic puzzle or a lottery.

## Key Takeaways

- **FreeCell** is one of the most rigorously studied solitaire variants: 99.999% of standard deals are solvable, and computers can prove it for each deal individually.
- **Klondike's** solvability rate is still genuinely uncertain -- estimates range from 79% to 91.5% depending on the model, because hidden information makes perfect analysis impossible.
- **Spider 4-Suit** has a solvability rate of around 30-40%, with many deals provably impossible before you make a single move.
- The key structural factors that determine solvability are: information completeness, temporary storage, and the ratio of moves to card positions.
- Game designers who understand this math can tune difficulty precisely. Most classic variants arrived at their settings by feel, not calculation.

## What "Solvable" Actually Means

A solitaire deal is solvable if there exists at least one sequence of legal moves that completes the game (all cards to foundations). That definition has two important implications.

First, solvability is a property of the initial deal, not the player. A solvable deal can still be lost by a player who makes wrong moves. An unsolvable deal cannot be won by any player, no matter how skilled.

Second, proving a deal is unsolvable is computationally harder than proving it is solvable. To show a deal is solvable, you just need to find one winning path. To show it's unsolvable, you need to show that every possible path leads to failure. For games with large state spaces, that exhaustive search can take a very long time -- which is why Klondike's solvability rate remains contested.

## FreeCell: The Best-Understood Case

[FreeCell](/freecell) is unusual in solitaire mathematics because it has near-perfect analysis. Every card is dealt face-up, so the initial game state is fully visible. There is no hidden information, no random element after the deal, and no stock pile with unknown order. This means the entire game tree can in principle be searched completely.

The definitive study of FreeCell solvability comes from Microsoft's numbered deal set. Jim Horne's original implementation of Windows FreeCell contained 32,000 numbered deals. Over several years in the 1990s and 2000s, collaborative human and computer analysis determined that exactly one of those 32,000 deals was unsolvable: deal #11982. The other 31,999 were provably winnable.

Computer analysis of the full extended deal set (using arbitrary random seeds) has consistently found that approximately 99.999% of randomly generated FreeCell deals are solvable. The remaining 0.001% are provably unsolvable deals that tend to share a structural signature: critical cards are buried under other critical cards with no path to extrication.

**Why FreeCell is so solvable:** The four free cells function as a temporary storage buffer that multiplies the number of viable move sequences. Any single card can be temporarily parked, giving the player flexibility to reorder stacks in ways that would otherwise be blocked. The alternating-color tableau building rule also helps: more cards are eligible to stack on any given column, increasing the density of legal moves.

Raise the free cell count to 6 or 8, and solvability approaches 100%. Lower it to 2, and solvability drops substantially. The free cell count is the primary difficulty dial in this family of games. You can see this in action by comparing [FreeCell to Baker's Game](/freecell-vs-bakers-game) -- same layout, same free cells, but same-suit building cuts solvability from 99.999% to roughly 75%.

## Klondike: The Unsolved Problem

Klondike is the most-played solitaire variant in the world, and its solvability rate is still not precisely known. That seems like a paradox -- surely we can just run a computer solver on millions of deals? But the hidden information makes this harder than it sounds.

The problem is state-space explosion combined with hidden cards. In Klondike, face-down cards in the tableau and the unplayed stock pile create uncertainty that branches into enormous numbers of possible game states. A computer solver must track not just the visible position but all possible orderings of hidden cards.

The most rigorous analysis comes from researcher Shlomi Fish, whose 2020 work on the FC-Solve project estimated that approximately **79% of Klondike deals** are winnable with perfect play -- and crucially, this was a lower bound, not a definitive count. Other estimates using different assumptions have gone as high as 91.5%.

The practical upshot: somewhere between 8% and 21% of Klondike deals are genuinely impossible. When you are stuck in Klondike and can't see any path forward, the odds are meaningful that there is no path. With FreeCell, if you're stuck, you almost certainly missed a move.

This distinction matters for how you approach each game. In Klondike, learning to recognize unsolvable positions early saves time. In FreeCell, that same inclination to give up early is almost always wrong.

## Spider 4-Suit: Complexity at Scale

[Spider 4-Suit](/solitaire-difficulty-ranking) occupies a different mathematical universe. The game uses two full decks -- 104 cards -- across 10 tableau columns, and requires building 8 complete sequences by suit. The state space is enormous.

Computer analysis suggests that roughly **30-40% of Spider 4-Suit deals** are solvable. That's the theoretical rate with perfect play; practical human win rates are substantially lower (10-20%) because the complexity of optimal play exceeds what most humans can maintain over a full game.

The mathematical reasons for this low solvability are structural:

1. **No free cells.** Unlike FreeCell, there is no temporary storage buffer. Cards must stack somewhere on the tableau or go directly to a completed sequence.
2. **Suit interaction.** Moving a card onto a stack of a different suit creates a cross-suit group that cannot be moved as a unit. Over 104 cards, these interactions accumulate into configurations that are geometrically impossible to resolve.
3. **Stock dependency.** The 50 cards that start off the tableau (dealt in 5 rounds of 10) introduce random elements that can create unsolvable positions regardless of play.

Spider 1-Suit is almost entirely solvable (estimates above 99%) because single-suit sequences can always move as a unit. The jump from 1-suit to 4-suit isn't a smooth gradient -- it's a cliff, because suit management introduces combinatorial complexity that multiplies with every additional suit.

## The Three Levers of Solvability

Looking across the full variant family, three structural factors determine where a game falls on the solvability spectrum:

**1. Information completeness.** When all cards are visible (FreeCell, Baker's Game, Eight Off), the player can verify solvability mentally and plan optimally. When cards are hidden (Klondike, Yukon), uncertainty creates situations where optimal play is undefined and solvability is harder to analyze. Games with perfect information have higher effective solvability rates because players can avoid the dead ends they can see coming.

**2. Temporary storage.** Free cells, reserve piles, and cells are all forms of temporary storage that increase the density of legal moves. More temporary storage generally means higher solvability. FreeCell has 4 cells; Eight Off has 8; Beleaguered Castle has 0 and its solvability drops accordingly to around 20-25%.

**3. Building rule strictness.** Alternating-color building (FreeCell, Klondike) allows more cards to stack on any given column position than same-suit building (Baker's Game, Spider). Stricter building rules reduce the number of legal moves at any position, which compresses the game tree and increases the probability of deals with no winning path.

## Why This Matters for Practice

Understanding solvability changes how you should play each game.

In **FreeCell**, you should almost never give up. If a deal feels impossible, you are almost certainly missing something. Go back to the beginning of the deal, look for the free cell you forgot to use, and try a different first move. The mathematics says a solution almost certainly exists.

In **Klondike**, learning to recognize unsolvable positions early is a genuine skill. When your stock pile has cycled once and you have no useful moves, the deal may simply be lost. Experienced Klondike players can often identify dead positions within the first two stock cycles and stop there rather than playing out 20 more futile moves.

In **Spider 4-Suit**, accept that roughly 2 in 3 deals you start are not winnable, and focus on identifying the tell-tale signs of an unsolvable deal early: critical same-suit sequences that are reversed, foundation-ready sequences locked under five cross-suit stacks with no free columns in sight.

## A Note on Computational Solvability

Modern solitaire solvers can prove the solvability of individual FreeCell deals in milliseconds. Klondike deals with fully known card ordering can be solved (or proven unsolvable) in a few seconds to a few minutes depending on deal complexity. Spider 4-Suit deals can take hours even for high-powered solvers on particularly complex configurations.

This computational difficulty parallels the human difficulty. The reason Spider 4-Suit is hard for humans and hard for computers is the same: the state space is enormous, and finding the right path through it requires exhaustive search that overwhelms both silicon and neurons.

For practical play, this means that when a Spider 4-Suit game feels intractable after 45 minutes, you are not simply being outplayed by the cards. The problem is genuinely, mathematically difficult.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the solvability rate of standard FreeCell?**

Computer analysis of millions of randomly generated FreeCell deals consistently finds that approximately 99.999% are solvable. In Microsoft's original 32,000-deal set, exactly one deal (deal #11982) was proven unsolvable. [Play Free Cell](/freecell) and you're almost certainly playing a winnable deal.

**Why is Klondike's solvability rate uncertain when FreeCell's isn't?**

FreeCell deals all cards face-up, so the complete game state is known from the start. Computers can exhaustively search the game tree. Klondike has hidden cards and a partially unseen stock pile, which creates enormous uncertainty about the full state space. Different analysis methods produce different estimates.

**Does understanding the math actually help you win more?**

Yes, in a specific way: knowing when to quit. If you understand that Klondike has a meaningful unsolvability rate, you can give up on clearly dead deals rather than playing them out pointlessly. Knowing that FreeCell is almost always solvable should push you to look harder before quitting.

**Are there solitaire games with 100% solvability?**

A few games approach 100% solvability through design. Clock Solitaire has exactly one winning condition and one losing condition determined purely by card order -- its solvability is 7.7% with zero player agency. Games with very large reserve piles or many free cells approach 100% by giving the player enough temporary storage to manage any deal. The tradeoff is that such games become trivial.

**What makes a solitaire deal "provably impossible"?**

A deal is provably impossible when every possible sequence of legal moves eventually leads to a position where no further progress can be made and the game cannot be completed. The proof requires exhaustive search of the game tree from the initial state. For most variants, this search is too large for humans but feasible for computers on individual deals.
