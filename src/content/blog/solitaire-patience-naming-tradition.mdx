Ask someone in London what they're playing when they lay out a row of tableau columns and work through a stock pile one card at a time. They'll say Patience. Ask someone in Chicago the same question. They'll say Solitaire. These are not different games. They're the same family of games with a 200-year-old naming split that reflects something real about how cards travel across cultures.

The divergence is small enough to seem trivial. It isn't. The two names carry different assumptions about what the game is for, who plays it, and what it asks of the player. Understanding that split is a useful lens on the game's entire history.

## Key Takeaways

- "Patience" is the predominant term across most of Europe, particularly Britain, France, and Scandinavia. "Solitaire" is the North American default.
- Both names emerged in the early 19th century and have coexisted for over 200 years without either displacing the other.
- The word "solitaire" in European usage typically refers to the peg-jumping board game, not card games -- a distinction that still creates confusion today.
- The names reflect different cultural framings: "Patience" emphasizes temperament and process; "Solitaire" emphasizes the solitary nature of play.
- Globalization through the internet and apps has softened but not eliminated the split, as "solitaire" has become the dominant term for digital card games internationally.

## Where the Names Came From

The French term *la patience* appears in published card game references as early as the 1780s. It described the attitude required to play well: unhurried, methodical, willing to deal out the same cards repeatedly. The game was explicitly about tolerating uncertainty -- you laid out the cards, you worked through the sequence, and you waited to see if the cards would cooperate. *Patience* named that waiting.

The French also used *la réussite*, meaning "the success," for some variants -- reflecting an outcome-focused framing that never spread widely. *Patience* won out because it named the experience of play, not just the hoped-for result.

*Solitaire* as a term for card games is more complicated in French. The word had prior claim in Europe as the name for the peg-jumping board puzzle (known in French as *le solitaire* or *solitaire de Peg*), which was popular in drawing rooms across France and England in the 18th century. This pre-existing use of "solitaire" for a different game made it an awkward label for the card variant in European markets -- using it would create ambiguity between two unrelated games.

In North America, the peg game didn't have the same cultural footprint. When Klondike and other card games became popular in the late 19th century, especially during the Klondike Gold Rush era that is sometimes (if uncertainly) linked to the game's spread, the obvious label was "solitaire" -- solo play, nothing more to it. No confusion with board puzzles.

## The Victorian Codification

Both names got their canonical form during the Victorian period, when the first serious published collections of card game rules appeared in English and French.

Lady Adelaide Cadogan's *Illustrated Games of Patience* (1870) was one of the earliest English-language books to compile and standardize solitaire rules. The title fixed "Patience" as the English term for British readers. Over the following decades, British game publishers produced dozens of Patience books, each adding to a body of standardized rules that were passed through families and clubs.

In France, *Les Patiences* remained the standard term through the 19th century. German equivalents (*Geduldspiel*, patience games) followed similar patterns.

The North American publishing tradition developed separately. By the late 19th and early 20th century, American and Canadian publications used "solitaire" consistently. The two traditions developed their own canonical variant lists, names, and rules with minimal cross-referencing.

This divergence in publishing infrastructure mattered. A game that appeared in British Patience books under one name might appear in American solitaire books under a different name with slightly different rules. Klondike is a case study: it appears in British Patience literature under various names including "Fascination," "Triangle," and "American Patience" -- none of which is the name any North American player would recognize.

## What the Names Assume About the Player

There's a subtle framing difference embedded in the two names that goes beyond geography.

**Patience** frames the game as a test of character. You are practicing something: the ability to work methodically through an uncertain process without forcing outcomes. The game may not resolve in your favor -- many Patience games have low win rates -- but the value is in the process itself. This frames loss as neither failure nor misfortune. It's just how patience works.

**Solitaire** frames the game primarily as a context: you are playing alone. The emphasis is on the structural condition (one player, no opponent) rather than the psychological demand. Loss is a neutral outcome of that lone engagement with a shuffled deck.

These aren't competing philosophies -- they're two different things to notice about the same activity. But they've influenced how each tradition talks about the games. British writing about Patience has historically emphasized variants with lower win rates and longer, more demanding sequences of play. American solitaire culture, filtered through Microsoft's influence, has been more attentive to solvability, speed, and the satisfaction of clearing the tableau.

## The Microsoft Inflection

The naming divergence mattered less after 1990 than it did before.

When Microsoft shipped Windows 3.0 with a Klondike game labeled simply "Solitaire," that name got installed on hundreds of millions of computers in every English-speaking country -- including Britain, Australia, and Ireland, where "Patience" had been the standard term for a century. The Windows product didn't say "Patience." It said "Solitaire."

Over the following decade, as Windows penetration grew, "Solitaire" became the default digital term even in markets that would historically have used "Patience." The shift was generational: people who learned the game through Windows learned the Windows vocabulary. People who learned it from their parents or grandparents' physical card habits kept the old vocabulary.

This created a transitional cohort -- common especially among adults now in their 50s and 60s -- who use both terms interchangeably and don't notice the inconsistency. For younger generations who encountered the game primarily through digital products, "Solitaire" is the unmarked default.

The British card game tradition hasn't abandoned "Patience" entirely. The British card game authority (the English-language Wikipedia article on "Patience (card game)" treats Patience as the primary term, with "Solitaire" as the American variant). And British puzzle publishing still uses Patience in contexts that predate Windows.

But in practice, for most players under 50 in any English-speaking country, the game is Solitaire.

## The Ambiguity That Persists

One consequence of this history is persistent ambiguity in search and discovery. A European searching for online card games might search for "patience card game" and find fewer results than a North American searching "solitaire." Search engines now handle this reasonably well through semantic understanding, but for several years in the early web era, players in different regions genuinely had trouble finding the same games online because the vocabulary didn't match.

This also affects competitive gaming and community organization. When online solitaire communities formed in the late 1990s and 2000s, they organized almost entirely around the "solitaire" vocabulary. British players who entered those spaces were a minority using the "wrong" word and found fewer resources specifically speaking to the Patience tradition they knew.

The practical divergence has narrowed today but hasn't disappeared. Searching "patience game" in the UK still surfaces different results than "solitaire" in the US, even for identical games.

## A Note on Other Naming Traditions

English is not the only language with a naming split. In German, the card game is *Patience* or *Patienzenspiel*, borrowed directly from French. In Swedish and Danish, it's also *Patience*. In Italian, it's *Solitario* (closer to the English Solitaire). In Spanish, it's *Solitario* in Latin American markets but sometimes *Paciencia* in Spain.

The pattern broadly mirrors the English split: Romance-language countries (French, Spanish, Italian) and their colonial markets split based on when and how they adopted the term, while Northern European languages followed the French lead toward "Patience." Russian uses *Пасьянс* (Pasyans), directly from the French.

The effect is a patchwork of naming traditions that all describe the same activity. When solitaire researchers and players correspond across countries, vocabulary disambiguation is a routine first step.

## What the Name You Use Reveals

If you call it Patience, you almost certainly learned the game from a source that predates 1990 -- a family member, a book, or a British cultural context. You probably know variants that most North American players have never heard of: Miss Milligan, Sir Tommy, Demon, Grandfather's Clock.

If you call it Solitaire, you probably learned it through Windows or a mobile app. Your canonical frame is Klondike, with possibly FreeCell as a second game. The variants you know by name are the Microsoft roster.

Both lineages are legitimate. The game is the same. But the vocabulary you use carries 200 years of history about where card games have traveled and what they've picked up along the way.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is Patience the same as Solitaire?**

Yes. "Patience" is the term used in British English and most of Europe for the same family of single-player card games that North Americans call "Solitaire." The games themselves are identical; only the vocabulary differs.

**Why does "Solitaire" mean a board game in some European countries?**

In France and other European countries, "solitaire" was already in use as the name for the peg-jumping board puzzle (peg solitaire) before the card game needed a name. Card players used "Patience" to avoid confusion with the board game. North America didn't have the same prior attachment to peg solitaire, so the card game could use "Solitaire" without ambiguity.

**Did Microsoft's Windows cause the naming shift?**

Windows Solitaire was a major factor in spreading the "Solitaire" label to non-North American markets. It didn't cause the divergence -- that was already 150 years old -- but it introduced the American vocabulary to hundreds of millions of people who had previously used "Patience."

**What are Patience games that most North Americans have never heard of?**

Miss Milligan, Sir Tommy, Demon (also known as Canfield in North America, though the rules differ), Grandfather's Clock, and La Belle Lucie are all classic British Patience games with established traditions but minimal presence in North American digital solitaire apps. They represent the part of the family tree that the Microsoft era didn't preserve.

**Which name is "correct"?**

Neither. Both are standard in their contexts. Style guides for British English prefer "Patience" for the card game family. Style guides for American English prefer "Solitaire." The only error is using one term in a context where readers will find it confusing or unfamiliar.
