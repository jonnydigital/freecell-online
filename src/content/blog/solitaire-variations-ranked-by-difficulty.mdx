Not all solitaire games are created equal. Some are relaxing time-killers where you win more often than you lose. Others are punishing logic puzzles where a single mistake thirty moves ago quietly doomed you. And a few are so difficult that even perfect play can't save you most of the time.

This ranking covers every major solitaire variation — 25+ games sorted into five difficulty tiers based on **win rate** (how often optimal play solves a random deal), **decision complexity** (how many meaningful choices you face), and **forgiveness** (how badly one mistake hurts).

## Tier 1: Beginner-Friendly (Win Rate 80%+)

These games are welcoming, forgiving, and designed to be won more often than lost. Perfect for new players or relaxing sessions.

### 1. FreeCell — The Perfect Puzzle
**Win rate:** ~99.999% | **Skill ceiling:** High | **Forgiveness:** High

[FreeCell](/strategy) is paradoxically both the easiest and one of the most strategic solitaire games. Nearly every deal is solvable, so losses are always learning opportunities rather than bad luck. All 52 cards are visible from the start, eliminating the frustration of hidden information. Four free cells provide generous temporary storage.

What prevents FreeCell from being *too* easy is the depth of its decision tree. You may win 99% of games, but winning 99.9% requires genuinely strong play.

[Play FreeCell](/) | [Strategy Guide](/strategy)

### 2. Easy FreeCell — Training Wheels On
**Win rate:** ~99.99% | **Skill ceiling:** Medium | **Forgiveness:** Very high

[Easy FreeCell](/easy-freecell) starts with aces and twos already on the foundations, giving you a head start. It's the gentlest introduction to the FreeCell family — nearly impossible to lose if you think ahead even slightly.

[Play Easy FreeCell](/easy-freecell)

### 3. TriPeaks — Quick & Casual
**Win rate:** ~90%+ | **Skill ceiling:** Low | **Forgiveness:** High

[TriPeaks](/tripeaks) is a fast-paced, mostly-luck game where you clear three pyramids by playing cards one rank higher or lower than the waste pile card. Games last 2-3 minutes and the win rate is high because partial clears still feel satisfying. Strategy is minimal but exists in choosing which chain to extend.

[Play TriPeaks](/tripeaks)

### 4. Pyramid — Matching Made Simple
**Win rate:** ~85-90% | **Skill ceiling:** Low-Medium | **Forgiveness:** Medium

[Pyramid](/pyramid) challenges you to remove pairs of cards that sum to 13 from a pyramid-shaped tableau. It's more about spotting available pairs than deep planning, though experienced players learn to prioritize uncovering buried cards.

[Play Pyramid](/pyramid) | [Pyramid Strategy](/pyramid/strategy)

### 5. Spider 1-Suit — Build & Clear
**Win rate:** ~99% | **Skill ceiling:** Medium | **Forgiveness:** High

[Spider Solitaire](/spider) with a single suit is remarkably forgiving. Since all cards are the same suit, every descending sequence is automatically a valid run. The challenge is managing ten columns efficiently, but the margin for error is wide.

[Play Spider](/spider) | [1-Suit vs 2-Suit vs 4-Suit](/spider/1-suit-vs-2-suit-vs-4-suit)

## Tier 2: Intermediate (Win Rate 60-80%)

Games that require real strategy. You'll lose sometimes even with good play, but skilled players win the majority.

### 6. Klondike Draw-1 — Classic Solitaire
**Win rate:** ~79-82% (theoretical) / ~45% (average player) | **Skill ceiling:** Medium | **Forgiveness:** Medium

[Klondike](/klondike) is what most people mean when they say "Solitaire." Draw-1 gives you the best odds because you see every stock card. The hidden tableau cards add a luck element FreeCell doesn't have, but good players learn to maximize their chances through stock management and selective building.

[Play Klondike](/klondike) | [Strategy Guide](/klondike/strategy)

### 7. Eight Off — Extra Cells, Harder Rules
**Win rate:** ~85-90% (theoretical) / ~60-75% (skilled player) | **Skill ceiling:** High | **Forgiveness:** Medium

[Eight Off](/eight-off) gives you double the free cells (8 instead of 4) but requires same-suit building and only allows Kings in empty columns. The extra storage partially compensates for the stricter rules, creating a satisfying middle ground in the FreeCell family.

[Play Eight Off](/eight-off) | [FreeCell vs Eight Off](/freecell-vs-eight-off)

### 8. Yukon — Klondike's Bolder Cousin
**Win rate:** ~70-75% | **Skill ceiling:** Medium-High | **Forgiveness:** Low

[Yukon](/yukon) is Klondike without a stock pile — all cards are dealt to the tableau at the start, many face-down. You can move any face-up card (plus everything on top of it) regardless of sequence. This creates dramatic swings: brilliant moves that crack open a deal, or reckless ones that bury critical cards.

[Play Yukon](/yukon) | [Strategy Guide](/yukon/strategy)

### 9. Baker's Dozen — No Empty Columns Allowed
**Win rate:** ~70-80% | **Skill ceiling:** Medium | **Forgiveness:** Medium

[Baker's Dozen](/bakers-dozen) deals 52 cards into 13 columns of 4 cards each, with Kings moved to the bottom. You build foundations from Ace to King, but empty columns cannot be refilled. This constraint forces careful planning — every card you remove permanently reduces your maneuvering room.

[Play Baker's Dozen](/bakers-dozen) | [Strategy Guide](/bakers-dozen/strategy)

## Tier 3: Challenging (Win Rate 40-60%)

These games will humble you. Even experienced players lose frequently. Strategy matters enormously, but some deals are genuinely unwinnable.

### 10. Baker's Game — FreeCell's Harder Parent
**Win rate:** ~75% (theoretical) / ~50-65% (skilled player) | **Skill ceiling:** Very high | **Forgiveness:** Low

[Baker's Game](/bakers-game) is what FreeCell was derived from — identical layout, but same-suit building instead of alternating colors. That single rule change cuts available moves by ~75% and drops the solvability rate dramatically. Every placement must match suits, leaving almost no room for temporary arrangements.

[Play Baker's Game](/bakers-game) | [FreeCell vs Baker's Game](/freecell-vs-bakers-game)

### 11. Spider 2-Suit — The Sweet Spot
**Win rate:** ~50-60% | **Skill ceiling:** High | **Forgiveness:** Medium

Spider with two suits is where the game gets genuinely challenging. You can build descending sequences in mixed suits, but only same-suit sequences can be moved as a group or cleared to foundations. Managing suit purity across ten columns while dealing new rows from the stock requires serious planning.

[Play Spider](/spider) | [Tips](/spider/tips)

### 12. Klondike Draw-3 — Luck Amplified
**Win rate:** ~75-79% (theoretical) / ~30-35% (average player) | **Skill ceiling:** Medium | **Forgiveness:** Low

Draw-3 Klondike is significantly harder than Draw-1 because you only see every third stock card per pass. Blocked stock cards create dead ends that no amount of skill can overcome. The gap between theoretical solvability and actual win rate is the widest of any common solitaire game.

[Klondike Draw 1 vs Draw 3](/klondike/draw-1-vs-draw-3)

### 13. Beleaguered Castle — No Free Cells at All
**Win rate:** ~50-60% | **Skill ceiling:** High | **Forgiveness:** Very low

[Beleaguered Castle](/beleaguered-castle) is FreeCell without the free cells. All 52 cards are visible in eight columns, but you have zero temporary storage. Every move must advance your position directly. One wrong move can create an unbreakable deadlock with no recovery options.

[Play Beleaguered Castle](/beleaguered-castle)

### 14. Seahaven Towers — Constrained Beauty
**Win rate:** ~50-60% | **Skill ceiling:** High | **Forgiveness:** Low

[Seahaven Towers](/seahaven-towers) combines 4 free cells (2 pre-filled) with same-suit building and Kings-only empty columns. It's elegant but unforgiving — fewer escape hatches than FreeCell, and the Kings-only restriction means empty columns are rarely useful.

[Play Seahaven Towers](/seahaven-towers) | [Strategy Guide](/seahaven-towers/strategy)

## Tier 4: Expert (Win Rate 20-40%)

Games for masochists and strategists. Winning feels like a genuine accomplishment because most deals will defeat you.

### 15. Forty Thieves — The Double-Deck Gauntlet
**Win rate:** ~25-30% | **Skill ceiling:** Very high | **Forgiveness:** Very low

[Forty Thieves](/forty-thieves) uses two decks (104 cards), ten tableau columns, and only allows you to move one card at a time. Building is by suit, and the stock deals one card at a time with no redeals. The sheer number of cards combined with the one-at-a-time movement creates an incredibly tight puzzle.

[Play Forty Thieves](/forty-thieves) | [Strategy Guide](/forty-thieves/strategy)

### 16. Spider 4-Suit — The Final Boss
**Win rate:** ~33% | **Skill ceiling:** Extreme | **Forgiveness:** Low

[Spider 4-Suit](/spider) is widely considered the hardest version of Spider and one of the hardest common solitaire games. Four suits across 104 cards means suit tangles are everywhere. Building complete same-suit sequences from King to Ace requires extraordinary foresight and patience.

[Play Spider](/spider) | [Spider Strategy](/spider/strategy)

### 17. Flower Garden — The Bouquet Challenge
**Win rate:** ~30-40% | **Skill ceiling:** High | **Forgiveness:** Medium

[Flower Garden](/flower-garden) has a unique twist: a "bouquet" reserve of 16 cards that are all visible and playable at any time. But the tableau builds by any suit in descending order, and there's no stock. Managing the bouquet wisely — knowing when to play from it and when to save key cards — is the central strategic challenge.

[Play Flower Garden](/flower-garden) | [Strategy Guide](/flower-garden/strategy)

## Tier 5: Brutally Hard (Win Rate <20%)

These games will defeat you far more often than you win. Victory requires both excellent play *and* a favorable deal.

### 18. Scorpion — The Silent Killer
**Win rate:** ~10-15% | **Skill ceiling:** High | **Forgiveness:** None

[Scorpion](/scorpion) is Spider's meaner cousin — same-suit building with no stock pile and no redeals. You get seven columns and three extra cards dealt once. If your suits are tangled when the cards run out, there's no escape. One wrong move in the first ten turns can silently doom you.

[Play Scorpion](/scorpion) | [Strategy Guide](/scorpion/strategy)

### 19. Accordion — Pure Compression
**Win rate:** ~5-10% | **Skill ceiling:** Medium | **Forgiveness:** None

[Accordion](/accordion) deals all 52 cards in a single row and challenges you to compress them by matching rank or suit with the card 1 or 3 positions to the left. It sounds simple but the win rate is staggeringly low. Most deals are mathematically unwinnable, and even solvable deals require precise move ordering.

[Play Accordion](/accordion) | [Strategy Guide](/accordion/strategy)

### 20. Clock — Pure Luck
**Win rate:** ~1-2% | **Skill ceiling:** None | **Forgiveness:** N/A

[Clock](/clock) (also called Clock Patience) has zero strategic decisions. Cards are dealt into 12 piles arranged like a clock face plus a center pile, and you simply flip cards to their corresponding positions. You win only if the last King is the last card turned. It's pure luck — a coin flip you lose 98% of the time.

[Play Clock](/clock)

## Finding Your Level

**Just starting out?** Begin with [FreeCell](/) or [TriPeaks](/tripeaks). Both are forgiving and fun.

**Ready for more?** [Klondike](/klondike) and [Spider 2-Suit](/spider) offer real challenge without being punishing.

**Want to test yourself?** [Baker's Game](/bakers-game) and [Forty Thieves](/forty-thieves) will push your strategic thinking to its limits.

**Feeling masochistic?** [Scorpion](/scorpion) and [Accordion](/accordion) will remind you that cards have no mercy.

The beauty of solitaire is that there's a game for every mood and skill level. Start wherever feels comfortable and work your way up. The [difficulty ranking page](/solitaire-difficulty-ranking) has even more detail on where each game fits.
