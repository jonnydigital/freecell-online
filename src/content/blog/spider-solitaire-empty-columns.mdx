Ask a strong Spider Solitaire player what separates their games from a beginner's, and a large part of the answer will be empty columns. Not the obvious things like "build in-suit" or "don't deal too early." The way they think about empty columns.

An empty column in Spider isn't just a blank space. It's the single most flexible tool on the board, and most players use it at about 20% of its actual potential.

## Key Takeaways

- An empty column functions as a temporary buffer that lets you rearrange sequences you couldn't otherwise reach.
- The difference between "using" an empty column and "wasting" it is whether you get the space back.
- Creating an empty column deliberately is often more valuable than completing a sequence.
- In 4-suit Spider, losing your last empty column frequently means the game is over.
- Every decision to fill an empty column should come with a plan to re-empty it.

## What an Empty Column Actually Lets You Do

The technical rule in Spider is that any card or in-suit sequence can be moved to an empty column. That sounds simple. The power comes from what that enables.

Suppose you have a J-10-9-8 sequence in column 3, and you need to reach the 7 buried two cards below the J. With no empty column, you're stuck. With one empty column, you can park the J-10-9-8 in the empty space, access the cards below, and then reassemble the sequence elsewhere. That sequence acted as a four-card roadblock. The empty column dissolved it instantly.

Now imagine you have two empty columns. You can run the same trick on a longer sequence. Three empty columns lets you maneuver sequences of up to eight cards. The math is exponential: each additional empty column roughly doubles the length of sequence you can shuffle around.

This is why advanced players talk about empty columns in terms of "how many do I currently have" rather than "do I have one." The count matters.

## Creating Empty Columns: The Deliberate Approach

Empty columns don't just appear. You create them by clearing every card from a column, which usually means building short columns down to zero, or completing a sequence in a column that was already mostly clear.

In the opening game, columns 5 through 10 start with only 5 cards each. A column with 5 face-up cards and no face-down cards is close to clearable. If the visible cards form a connected sequence, you can often stack them elsewhere and create the empty column before your first stock deal.

Creating an empty column in the first 15-20 moves is a strong signal that you're managing the opening well. It gives you routing flexibility for everything that follows.

When you're scanning the board for moves, add this to your evaluation: not just "can I build a sequence here," but "which column is closest to empty, and what moves would clear it?"

Sometimes clearing a column is two moves away. Sometimes it's ten. Knowing the difference is part of reading the board.

## The Routing Trick: Move Through, Don't Park

The most powerful use of an empty column isn't parking a card in it permanently. It's routing a card through it temporarily to land somewhere else.

Here's a concrete example. You have a 9 in column 2 that you want to move onto a 10 in column 7. But column 7's 10 has a 6 sitting on top of it. You can't make the direct move.

With an empty column: move the 6 to the empty column, place the 9 on the 10, then move the 6 somewhere else. The empty column served as a swap buffer. You moved two cards, completed a connection, and the empty column is available again.

This move pattern comes up constantly. Train yourself to look for it whenever a direct move seems blocked. The question isn't "can I make this move?" but "can I make this move with one buffer step?"

## Filling a Column: When It's Justified

Filling an empty column isn't always wrong. Here are legitimate reasons to do it.

**Completing an imminent sequence.** If you're one card away from a King-to-Ace completion and placing a card in the empty column closes it, use the column. The completed sequence removes 13 cards and frees much more space than you spent.

**Uncovering a critical face-down card.** If filling the column lets you access a buried card that unblocks a column with five face-down cards beneath it, the trade can be worth it. You're spending short-term flexibility for long-term information.

**Pre-deal positioning.** Before a stock deal, every column must have at least one card. If you're about to deal and you have an empty column, you'll be forced to fill it anyway. In that case, place the most useful card there, not whatever's convenient.

What's never justified: filling an empty column because it feels untidy, or because you have a card and nowhere better to put it. Discomfort with empty space is a beginner habit. The empty column is working for you even when nothing is in it.

## The Mid-Game Crisis: When Empty Columns Disappear

Most losing Spider games have a common shape. The player enters the mid-game with zero empty columns, a stock deal or two remaining, and six to eight columns of mixed sequences they can't untangle. There's no routing space. Every move has nowhere to go.

This state almost always traces back to early game column fills that weren't recovered. The player used the empty column to park a card, and never found a way to re-empty it.

When you sense this happening, stop and triage. Ask: which column is closest to being clearable? Is there any sequence I can complete that would free space? If a stock deal is available, would dealing a round open up new connections?

Recovering from zero empty columns is hard but not always impossible. The key is to work methodically: complete any near-complete sequences to remove cards from the board, then attack the column most likely to clear.

## 4-Suit Spider: Empty Columns Are Survival

Everything above applies to 1-suit and 2-suit Spider. In 4-suit Spider, the same principles apply but with steeper consequences. Losing your last empty column in 4-suit frequently means the game is unwinnable.

Why is the effect so much worse? Because in 4-suit, almost every sequence is mixed-suit to some degree. You need routing space constantly to sort cards by suit and build the clean in-suit runs that can be removed. Without empty columns, the sort is impossible.

Experienced 4-suit players will sometimes choose not to complete a sequence if doing so would cost them their last empty column. The completed sequence removes 13 cards but leaves the board frozen. Keeping the empty column keeps the game alive.

This is a counterintuitive trade. It feels like you're turning down a win. But in 4-suit Spider, board mobility is worth more than a single sequence completion at certain stages.

If you're moving from 2-suit to 4-suit, this shift in empty-column valuation is one of the hardest things to internalize. Give it time.

## A Simple Self-Check for Every Move

Before you place any card, run this two-second check:

1. Is this move filling an empty column?
2. If yes, what's my plan to re-empty it?

If you can't answer question two, strongly consider a different move. Empty columns that get filled and stay filled are wasted moves. They helped you once and then disappeared from your toolkit.

Staying conscious of this pattern turns empty column management from a vague concept into a real habit. And that habit, more than any specific sequence tip, is what pushes your Spider win rate upward.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I put any card in an empty column, or only Kings?**
Any card. A single card, any rank. Or a complete in-suit sequence topped by any card. In Spider, there's no restriction on what can start a new column. This is different from Klondike, where only Kings can move to empty spaces.

**How many empty columns should I aim to have at once?**
In 1-suit and 2-suit, having one or two empty columns at any point in the mid-game is healthy. Three or more is excellent. Zero is a warning sign. In 4-suit, even one empty column is a significant resource worth protecting.

**Is it ever right to deal from the stock just to fill empty columns?**
No. You have to have at least one card in every column before dealing, so you'd need to fill the empty column before dealing anyway. But dealing specifically to fill columns treats the stock like a solution. The stock is a source of complications, not opportunities. Fill empty columns with cards already on the board if possible.

**What if I don't have any moves that uncover face-down cards and my only option fills the empty column?**
Sometimes there's no clean choice. If filling the column is your best available move, make it. The goal is to track the trade consciously. You spent a resource. Now you need to recoup it later.

**Does the empty column rule change between difficulty levels?**
The rule itself is the same: any card or sequence can occupy an empty column. The strategic weight changes dramatically. In 1-suit it's a useful tool. In 2-suit it's important. In 4-suit it's often the deciding factor between a winnable and unwinnable position.
