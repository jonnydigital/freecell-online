Most first-time Spider Solitaire players lose their first dozen games without knowing why. They can read the rules fine. They just don't know what "good play" looks like in motion. This walkthrough fixes that.

We're going to walk through a complete 1-suit Spider game, move group by move group, with the reasoning behind each decision. The goal isn't to show you a specific game you'll replicate exactly. The goal is to show you the mental model so you recognize what to do in any opening position.

## Key Takeaways

- In the opening, your only goal is to reveal face-down cards. Sequences come later.
- Empty columns are more valuable than any card placement. Protect them.
- Delay every stock deal as long as humanly possible.
- In 1-suit Spider, mixed-suit conflicts don't exist, so focus entirely on column structure.
- Win rate climbs fast once you understand the opening priority order: reveal, consolidate, create space.

## The Starting Position

A 1-suit Spider game opens with 54 face-down cards and 10 face-up cards visible at the bottom of each column. Columns 1 through 4 have 6 cards each, columns 5 through 10 have 5 cards each. The stock holds 50 cards in five deals of 10.

Before making any move, scan the 10 visible cards. You're looking for two things: sequences you can build right now, and which columns have the fewest face-down cards (since those are quickest to fully uncover).

Let's say your visible cards are: K, 9, 7, Q, 8, 6, J, 10, 8, 5 (columns left to right).

## Opening Priority: Reveal Cards, Not Build Sequences

Your first instinct might be to build the J-10-9 sequence visible in columns 7, 8, and 2. Resist it. Look first for moves that flip face-down cards.

**Move 1:** 6 (column 6) onto 7 (column 3). This frees column 6's hidden card to be revealed.

**Move 2:** 5 (column 10) onto 6 (column 6, now on 7). Flip the card in column 10.

**Move 3:** 8 (column 9) onto 9 (column 2). Flip column 9's hidden card.

Every one of these moves has a purpose beyond "7 goes on 8." The purpose is information. You want to see what's under there.

After three moves, you've flipped up to three new cards. Those cards might give you more connecting moves or tell you which columns to prioritize next.

## The First Real Decision Point: When to Chase Sequences

After five to eight moves in the opening, you'll have uncovered several new cards and started to see longer potential sequences. Now you face a real choice: keep chasing individual card reveals, or start consolidating runs?

The rule of thumb is this: chase reveals until a column goes completely face-up. A fully face-up column (no hidden cards remaining) is much easier to work with because you can plan around it completely.

Once you have two or three fully face-up columns, start consolidating sequences. Stack the 10-9-8-7 onto the J if the full run is visible. Moving a long in-suit sequence frees the space that was under it.

In 1-suit Spider, any descending sequence can be moved as a group since every card shares the same suit. A 10-9-8-7-6 can move onto a J as one action. Use this constantly. It's the core mechanical advantage of 1-suit play.

## Managing the First Empty Column

At some point in the first 20 moves, you'll clear every card from one column. This is a major milestone. Treat it like a temporary workspace, not a parking lot.

Good uses for the empty column:
- Park a King that's blocking a column with many face-down cards.
- Temporarily move a sequence segment to access a card buried beneath it.
- Route a card through the empty space to complete an in-suit connection you couldn't make directly.

Bad uses:
- Dropping in a random mid-value card just because you can.
- Filling it immediately when you have no tactical need.

The moment you fill the empty column without a plan, you've spent your most valuable resource. You'll want it back within two or three moves. Hold it.

## The Mid-Game: Working the Stock Carefully

By the time you've exhausted all productive moves in the opening, you'll typically have dealt the stock once or twice. Each deal drops one card onto every column.

Before dealing, run through this checklist:
1. Have you flipped every face-down card you can possibly reach?
2. Are any sequences ready to be fully completed (King through Ace)?
3. Is there a consolidation move that would make the post-deal position cleaner?

If any of those have a "yes," do it before hitting the deal button. The deal costs you nothing except added complexity, but it's a one-way door. You can't un-deal.

After dealing, your new visible cards might complete runs you've been building. In 1-suit Spider, this happens often because every card is the same suit. Scan for immediate sequence completions first, then go back to the reveal-and-consolidate routine.

## Completing Your First Sequence

The first complete King-to-Ace run feels like a gear change. Thirteen cards leave the board. The columns it occupied are now freed or partially freed. The board gets meaningfully simpler.

Don't chase the first completion obsessively, but do notice when you're two or three cards away. If finishing the sequence requires using your empty column as a routing space, that's a legitimate trade. An empty column used well (and then recovered) beats an empty column sat idle.

After the first completion, the game often opens up quickly. You've established a pattern: reveal, consolidate, complete. Repeat across the remaining sequences.

## Endgame: When the Board Thins Out

With three or four completed sequences removed, the remaining tableau is far less crowded. The late game in 1-suit Spider is mostly bookkeeping: find the longest in-suit runs, stack them onto available connectors, and complete the final sequences.

The main trap in the endgame is over-dealing from the stock. Players who reach the late game with two stock deals remaining often panic and deal both at once. Don't. Each deal adds 10 cards to a board you're trying to thin. Deal only when you're genuinely stuck.

If you've played well to this point, the final two or three sequences should assemble naturally. You'll have good column visibility, several face-up runs, and enough space to maneuver.

## Why 1-Suit Is the Right Starting Point

This walkthrough only works as described because we're in 1-suit mode. In 2-suit and 4-suit Spider, every move described above carries an additional layer of suit management. The in-suit grouping rule means mixed sequences block your movement. Empty columns become even more critical because you need them to sort out suit conflicts.

If you can win 1-suit games consistently using this framework, you're ready to add that layer. The mental model transfers. You're just adding one more constraint: [suit priority strategy](/spider/suit-strategy) determines which builds you make.

## Practice the Pattern, Not the Sequence

No walkthrough can give you the exact game you'll play next. What it can give you is the pattern: reveal first, protect empty columns, delay dealing, complete sequences when close. Run through that loop on every deal and your 1-suit win rate will be above 80% before long.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What difficulty should a complete beginner start on?**
Start on 1-suit. The win rate in 1-suit is around 90-95% with careful play. It lets you learn the core mechanics without the added complication of suit matching that dominates 2-suit and 4-suit games.

**How many moves should I think ahead in the opening?**
Two to three moves is enough for the opening. You're mostly reacting to what you reveal. Deep planning becomes more valuable in the mid-game once you've seen most of the board.

**Why can't I just fill empty columns with Kings immediately?**
You can, and sometimes it's correct. But an empty column holding a King is still an empty column you can't route other cards through. Kings are immovable anchors. Unless completing a King-to-Ace sequence is imminent, leaving the column empty gives you more options.

**When does dealing from the stock hurt me?**
Every deal adds 10 cards on top of your current columns. If those columns have sequences you're still sorting out, the new cards bury your work. Deal only when you've exhausted productive moves.

**Is there a wrong way to complete a sequence?**
Not really. Any completed King-to-Ace run in one suit leaves the board. The question is whether the resources you spent to complete it (empty column uses, out-of-order moves) were worth it relative to other things you could have done. If the completion frees significant space, it was almost always worth it.
