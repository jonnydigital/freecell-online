The opening in Spider Solitaire feels structured. Flip cards, build short sequences, watch for empty columns. Then, somewhere around the second or third deal, the obvious moves disappear. The board is crowded. Multiple partial suited runs are in progress. No single move leads anywhere clear. This phase has a name: the mid-game, and it is where most Spider games are won or lost.

## Key Takeaways

- The mid-game starts when most face-down cards are revealed, you have dealt twice or more, and no easy flip-enabling move remains.
- Your main decision is whether to consolidate existing runs or push one suit toward completion. Consolidating too long is usually the mistake.
- Committing to one suit early gives you a first completion faster than spreading effort across all suits equally.
- Empty columns are more valuable in the mid-game than the opening. Every use of a space should serve the suit you are developing.
- A mid-game board that looks stuck usually has one non-obvious move that opens two or three follow-on plays.

## How to Know You Are in the Mid-Game

These four signs tend to arrive together around deal 2 or 3:

- Most tableau columns have face-up cards in sequences, with few or no face-down cards remaining.
- You have two or more partial suited runs, but none is close to King through Ace.
- The last several moves you made were repositioning moves rather than card-flipping moves.
- No single legal move produces a card flip or extends a run by two or more cards in one sequence.

If all four are true, you are in the mid-game. The approach that worked in the opening (follow obvious moves, flip when you can) needs to change.

## The Core Mid-Game Question: Consolidate or Commit?

Most players reach the mid-game and try to develop all their suits at once. A Queen of clubs goes here, a 7 of hearts goes there. The board stays busy but nothing finishes.

The stronger approach is to commit. Pick one suit and push it hard toward completion. The first suit you complete removes 13 cards from the board and returns an empty column. That column then makes every remaining suit easier to close out. Spreading development evenly delays every first completion.

Consolidating has its place, but it is often overused. Use it when a sequence can be merged in-suit with one move, freeing a column for development. Do not consolidate just to tidy the board if it delays committing to a suit.

## How to Pick Which Suit to Push

Three questions identify the best candidate:

**Which suit has the longest in-suit run already assembled?** A run of 7 in-suit beats a run of 4. More foundation means less work left.

**Which suit has the fewest cards still buried or unaccounted for?** If 10 of your 13 target cards are visible and in position, that suit is closer than a suit where four cards are still hidden or in mixed-suit piles.

**Which suit's high cards are most accessible?** The King and Queen anchor the sequence. If your target King is free and an adjacent Queen is on a reachable pile, that suit is ready to develop now.

Pick the suit that scores best across those three questions. In [4-suit games](/spider/1-suit-vs-2-suit-vs-4-suit), this decision has the most impact, since the board fills up fastest and windows for first completions are narrow.

## What to Try When You Feel Stuck

Before dealing, run two checks:

**Look for in-suit repositioning moves.** A card currently in a mixed-suit pile might be moveable to a position where it continues an in-suit run. These moves are easy to miss because they don't look dramatic, but they often unlock two or three follow-on plays.

**Run the board-reading framework.** The [four-point position check](/blog/spider-solitaire-position-reading) is most useful in the mid-game. Count empty columns, count remaining face-down cards, identify near-complete runs, and confirm your deal count. That read usually surfaces a non-obvious move.

If neither produces a play, dealing is correct. Check the [pre-deal checklist](/blog/spider-solitaire-when-to-deal) first.

## The Empty Column Trap in Mid-Game

In the opening, an empty column appears and you fill it with whatever card makes sense. In the mid-game, that instinct becomes a liability.

An empty column in the mid-game is worth protecting. Every fill is a decision with lasting consequences. A King that enters a space and immediately starts building an in-suit sequence is doing work. A King that sits alone in a space for six turns is occupying a resource you cannot afford to waste.

Before dropping a card into an empty column in the mid-game, ask what you need the space for in the next two to four moves. If you need it as a relay for the suit you are developing, hold it. If the King you are placing will immediately attract usable cards, fill it. The [empty column guide](/blog/spider-solitaire-empty-columns) covers the full decision tree for both opening and later stages.

## How the Deal Count Changes Your Priorities

Where you are in the five-deal sequence shapes how aggressively you should play.

On deal 2 or 3, you still have runway to recover from speculative moves. Commit to a suit and start clearing the path now.

On deal 4, protect your existing structure. Any partial run you sacrifice may not be rebuilt before the stock runs out.

On deal 5, the board is what it is. Push the suit you committed to as hard as possible and use empty columns only for that purpose. If you are still in the mid-game at deal 4 or 5, the position is likely losing. Play assertively for any single completion, since one removed suit changes the math significantly.

## Transitioning Out of the Mid-Game

You leave the mid-game when one suit is within four or five cards of completion. At that point, shift fully into completion mode. Everything on the board is now subordinate to closing that one suit out. Use empty columns only for that sequence. Protect the near-complete run from being broken by a rushed deal.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between the mid-game and the endgame in Spider Solitaire?

The mid-game is when you are developing suits but none is close to completion. The endgame starts when at least one suit is within four or five cards of finishing and your moves become focused on closing it out. In practice, the mid-game is the longest and most demanding phase.

### Is it normal to feel stuck in the Spider mid-game?

Yes. The mid-game is the phase where the board has the most competing demands and the fewest obvious paths. Most players hit a wall here and deal too soon. Run the position-reading check and look for a repositioning move before touching the stock.

### Should I undo through a bad mid-game deal?

If undo is available and you can see a better pre-deal setup, yes. A mid-game deal that buries your primary suited run is worth undoing, replanning, and redoing. Just make sure you are fixing the actual problem (wrong pre-deal state) and not cycling through undo looking for a lucky card arrangement.

### Can I win Spider Solitaire if I'm still in the mid-game on deal 5?

Sometimes. If you have one suit nearly assembled and enough empty column room to route it, a deal-5 mid-game can still be won. But the margin is narrow. Most deal-5 mid-games end in a loss. Play for the best possible outcome and note what earlier decisions let the game reach deal 5 without a completion.
