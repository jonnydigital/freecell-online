Most people know that Solitaire came with Windows. Not everyone knows that Klondike, the game they usually mean by "Solitaire," shipped with Windows 3.0 in 1990, while Spider Solitaire didn't appear until Windows 98 Plus!, eight years later. That gap matters. Spider wasn't bundled because it was popular. It was bundled because someone at Microsoft decided it should be.

The story of how that happened, and why Spider eventually became the preferred solitaire variant among committed players, is worth understanding.

## Key Takeaways

- Spider Solitaire first shipped with Windows 98 Plus! in 1998, eight years after Klondike shipped with Windows 3.0.
- The game was included in Windows 98 Plus! as part of a package of add-on entertainment content, not as a core OS feature.
- Spider became a standard Windows inclusion starting with Windows XP in 2001, where it shipped with multiple difficulty settings.
- The game's longevity among power users stems from its depth: the 4-suit variant offers strategic complexity that Klondike simply doesn't match.
- Microsoft's decision to include difficulty tiers was probably more responsible for Spider's staying power than any single design choice.

## Klondike First: The Mouse Training Theory

Klondike Solitaire shipped with Windows 3.0 in 1990, and its original purpose was at least partly practical. The Klondike implementation by Wes Cherry was designed to help new PC users get comfortable with the mouse, specifically with clicking and dragging. The drag-and-drop gesture required to play Solitaire was exactly the gesture new users needed to master for the desktop environment.

This origin story is well documented. Cherry discussed the design intent in multiple interviews, including a 2020 piece in The Verge. The game was a training tool that happened to be entertaining.

Spider's inclusion, eight years later, had no such practical rationale. By 1998, mouse proficiency wasn't a concern. Spider came in through the entertainment door.

## Windows 98 Plus! and the Entertainment Expansion

Windows 98 Plus! was a retail add-on package for Windows 98, released in 1998 by Microsoft. It included desktop themes, Internet Explorer tools, and a collection of enhanced games. Spider Solitaire was part of this games package.

The Plus! series was Microsoft's way of offering additional content to users willing to pay for it. Games in Plus! were positioned as more sophisticated than the bundled utilities. Spider fit this framing: compared to Klondike's relatively simple deal structure, Spider's dual-deck, sequence-building format genuinely felt like a step up.

The exact internal decision process behind including Spider in Plus! isn't part of the public record. Microsoft hasn't published design documents from that period for the games team. What we know is the outcome: Spider shipped with Plus!, it was well received, and Microsoft made it a standard OS inclusion with Windows XP.

## Windows XP: Difficulty Tiers Change Everything

The version of Spider Solitaire that shipped with Windows XP in 2001 was more significant than the original Plus! release, for one specific reason: it introduced the three difficulty settings that define modern Spider.

Before XP, the Plus! version of Spider was a single-suit game. The 2001 XP version added 2-suit and 4-suit options at game start. Players could choose Easy (1-suit), Medium (2-suit), or Difficult (4-suit).

This was a substantial design decision. The three-tier system created the progression that made Spider engaging over months and years rather than weeks. Players could master 1-suit, move to 2-suit, and eventually tackle 4-suit. The game had a skill ceiling. Klondike, by comparison, had no meaningful difficulty ladder. You played the same game every time.

The XP difficulty system is probably the most important thing Microsoft did for Spider's long-term popularity. It created a reason to keep playing after you'd learned the basics.

## Why Power Users Migrated to Spider

By the mid-2000s, players who had worked through Spider's three difficulty levels were significantly more engaged with the game than they'd ever been with Klondike. The reasons are structural.

Klondike deals are heavily luck-dependent. Some estimates suggest only 79-90% of Klondike deals are theoretically winnable (the exact figure is disputed), and even winnable deals require card knowledge that isn't available to the player at move time. You make decisions with incomplete information, and many losses are genuinely outside the player's control.

Spider, especially at 2-suit and 4-suit, is harder but more transparent. All cards are dealt face-down in defined columns with predictable flip patterns. As you reveal cards, you build a complete picture. The game is still probabilistic (you don't know what's in the stock until you deal), but the skill component is higher and losses are more often attributable to specific decisions.

Players who wanted to feel like their choices mattered found Spider more satisfying than Klondike. The 4-suit variant in particular rewarded the kind of systematic, forward-looking play that Klondike simply couldn't accommodate.

This preference shows up in enthusiast communities. Online solitaire forums from the mid-2000s onward tend to treat 4-suit Spider as the pinnacle of mainstream solitaire, with FreeCell as a strong competitor. ## The Microsoft Solitaire Collection and Beyond

When Microsoft launched the Microsoft Solitaire Collection in 2012 as a Windows 8 app, Spider was one of five included games alongside Klondike, FreeCell, Pyramid, and TriPeaks. The Collection format, later available on iOS, Android, and as a browser game, turned individual solitaire titles into a suite.

The Collection's daily challenges introduced a competitive dimension: tracked performance, monthly events, and difficulty-scaled challenge formats. Spider's daily challenges tend to be among the most played in the Collection, which gives some indirect evidence of the game's sustained popularity relative to simpler variants.

Microsoft has not published granular data on which games in the Collection are played most. But the investment in Spider content within the Collection suggests it's been a significant contributor to engagement.

## What Made Spider Stick Where Others Didn't

Windows has bundled many games over the years. Few have lasted as long in the cultural memory as Solitaire variants. The ones that lasted did so because they're genuinely good games.

Spider's durability comes from the combination of accessibility and depth. The 1-suit version is approachable enough for any player. The 4-suit version is demanding enough to hold a serious player's attention across thousands of games. Very few single-deck card games can make the same claim.

The history is less the story of a company making smart choices and more the story of a game format that earned its place. The Windows distribution got it in front of hundreds of millions of people. The design kept them playing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Did Microsoft create Spider Solitaire?**
Microsoft developed or commissioned the Windows versions of Spider, but the game itself predates computers by decades. Spider Solitaire is based on a traditional card game documented in print as far back as the early 20th century. The "Solitaire" in the name refers to the category; the spider imagery refers to the four suits building up like the eight legs of a spider (one sequence per leg, eight total across two decks).

**Was the original Windows 98 Plus! Spider Solitaire different from later versions?**
Yes. The Plus! version was single-suit only. The multi-suit difficulty system that defines modern Spider came with the Windows XP version in 2001.

**Why didn't Spider ship with the base version of Windows 98?**
Spider was part of the Plus! add-on package, which was a paid retail supplement. Base Windows 98 included Klondike, FreeCell, Minesweeper, and Hearts. Plus! content was positioned as premium. With Windows XP, Microsoft moved Spider into the base OS installation.

**Has the core rules of Spider Solitaire changed since Windows XP?**
The fundamental rules have stayed consistent since XP. Modern versions on various platforms sometimes add features like hints, undo histories, and animation settings, but the game structure is the same.

**What happened to Spider Solitaire in Windows 8 and later versions?**
Windows 8 replaced the standalone game executables with the Microsoft Solitaire Collection app, which grouped all five classic solitaire games under one interface. Spider remains available within the Collection. The standalone executable from Windows 7 and earlier was discontinued.
