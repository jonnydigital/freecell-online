Most Spider players decide what to move before they decide whether to move. They spot a legal play and take it. Strong players work in the opposite order: they read the board first, figure out what they need, and then find the move that gets them closer to it.

The difference sounds small. Over a full game, it is the gap between winning 1-suit and losing 4-suit.

This guide covers the four things worth checking before every significant move in Spider Solitaire.

## Key Takeaways

- Empty columns are your most valuable asset. Count them before you touch anything.
- Face-down cards show you where the game is still hidden. High face-down counts mean high uncertainty.
- Partial suited runs are investments. Know which ones are close to moving as a block.
- Deal count shapes how aggressively you need to play right now.

## Check 1: Count Your Empty Columns

Before you do anything, count your empty columns, or identify which columns could open up in the next one or two moves.

An empty column in Spider is the most flexible resource on the board. It can hold any card or run temporarily, which lets you reorganize sequences you could not otherwise reach. In 4-suit games especially, empty columns are the difference between a buildable position and a stuck one.

The key question is not just "do I have an empty column?" It is "what is this column currently worth, and am I about to fill it with the wrong card?"

Never fill an empty column by reflex. If you have a move available that sends a card into empty space, pause and ask whether that card actually needs to go there, or whether you could use the space more effectively two moves from now. Our [empty columns guide](/blog/spider-solitaire-empty-columns) covers exactly this decision.

## Check 2: Count Face-Down Cards

Face-down cards are unknown cards. Every one of them is a potential blocker or a potential lifesaver, and you do not know which until you flip it.

Look at each column and note how many cards are still face-down. A column with five face-down cards under a short face-up stack is a slow, expensive excavation project. A column with one face-down card near the surface is one move from yielding new information.

This check tells you two things:

**Where to dig first.** The columns with the fewest face-down cards are the ones you can open most efficiently. When you have a choice between flip-enabling moves, prioritize the column that is almost clear over one that is deeply buried.

**How much of the game is still unknown.** If most columns have three or more face-down cards, you are still in the dark half of the game and every committed structure is a guess. If most columns are fully revealed, you can plan more concretely.

## Check 3: Inventory Your Partial Suited Runs

A partial suited run is any sequence of same-suit cards you have built but not yet completed. Once a run covers King to Ace in one suit, it leaves the board. Until then, it is both an asset (a moveable block once it is long enough) and a liability (a target that needs protection).

Before making any significant move, scan for partial runs that are close to becoming moveable as units. A run of four same-suit cards in sequence is strong. A run of six is almost complete and should be protected.

The move that extends a partial suited run by one card is often worth more than the move that looks flashier. Our [suit strategy guide](/blog/spider-solitaire-suit-strategy) covers how to decide which suit to develop first. The board-reading step sits upstream of that: you need to know what you already have before you decide what to build.

Also check for runs that are almost complete but have a gap. A sequence of K-Q-J-9 in spades, with the 10♠ still buried somewhere, is a target worth tracking. Knowing the gap exists tells you to look for that 10 while there is still time to place it.

## Check 4: Know Your Deal Count

The stock in Spider holds exactly 50 cards, dealt in five rounds of ten. Each deal covers your entire board with a new layer of cards. Count which deal you are on before making any risky structural move.

On deal one or two, you have time to recover from a mistake. Your priority is building suited runs and keeping options open.

On deal four or five, recovery windows are narrowing. Any structure you sacrifice now might not be rebuilt before the stock runs out. This is the point where protecting a partial suited run matters more than gaining a short-term advantage elsewhere.

One specific habit: before you accept a deal, make sure you have made every available productive move on the current board. Our [guide to deal timing](/blog/spider-solitaire-when-to-deal) covers the full pre-deal checklist. The deal count check here is simpler: just know the number, so your moves in the current position reflect where you actually are in the game.

## Putting the Four Checks Together

Here is how it runs in practice.

You look at the board. Before you touch anything, you run the four checks:

1. **Empty columns:** one available, column 4 could open in two moves.
2. **Face-down cards:** columns 2 and 7 each have three remaining, column 9 has only one.
3. **Partial runs:** 8-7-6-5♣ in column 3, 10-9♥ in column 8.
4. **Deal count:** deal three of five, two deals left.

Now you know the board. Column 9 is worth opening fast because it is nearly clear. The ♣ run is four deep and close to moveable, so protecting it has real value. Two deals left means structural risk carries a cost now.

From that read, the right move is usually obvious. It often is not the legal move you spotted first.

This is what separates players who win Spider consistently from those who win occasionally. The game is not about finding moves. It is about reading which moves serve the position. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does this framework work in 1-suit Spider too?

The four checks still apply, but 1-suit games are forgiving enough that you can usually recover from missed reads. Use 1-suit to practice the habit at low cost, then bring it to 2-suit and 4-suit where it actually changes outcomes.

### How long does the board read take?

About 15 seconds once it is a habit. New players skip it because it feels slow. After a few games of practicing it deliberately, it becomes a fast scan rather than a step-by-step procedure.

### What if two checks give conflicting guidance?

Deal count usually resolves the conflict. If you are late in the game and the suited-run check says "develop ♥" but the face-down check says "dig column 7," let the deal count break the tie. With one deal left, preservation beats development almost every time.

### Should I do this before every single move?

Not every move. For obvious continuations, a quick glance is fine. Use the full four-point check before any move that fills or empties a column, commits to developing a specific suit, or risks burying a partial run you have built. Those are the moves that are hardest to undo.
