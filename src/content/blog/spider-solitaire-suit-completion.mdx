Spider Solitaire games are decided not by whether you build sequences, but by whether you can close them. Completing a suit removes 13 cards from the board and returns an empty column. It is the clearest win condition in the game, and the path to it is less obvious than it looks until you understand what the final phase actually requires.

## Key Takeaways

- A suit completes when 13 cards of the same suit run King through Ace in a single column, with nothing on top.
- The last three to four cards of any suit are usually the hardest part. They require empty column space and careful sequencing.
- Identifying which suit is closest to completion early lets you concentrate effort before the board gets crowded.
- Completing your first suit is a turning point: 13 cards lift off and a free column opens. Every later suit becomes easier.
- In [4-suit Spider](/spider/1-suit-vs-2-suit-vs-4-suit), suit completion takes priority over balanced development once a suit is within reach.

## What Completing a Suit Actually Requires

Spider uses two full decks of 52 cards. A completed suit means 13 cards of the same suit, ordered King through Ace, in a single column with nothing on top. When that sequence is complete, the game removes it automatically and opens a column.

In practice, this means gathering 13 specific cards from across a crowded board, all of the same suit, and assembling them in order without any other cards breaking the run.

In [1-suit Spider](/spider/1-suit-vs-2-suit-vs-4-suit), every card is the same suit, so any K-through-A sequence in a single column completes. In 2-suit and 4-suit games, suit tracking becomes the central challenge because mixed-suit sequences do not count for completion, even if they are in the right rank order.

## Finding Your Nearest Suit to Complete

Before you can close a suit, you need to know which one is closest to ready. After the first two or three deals, survey the board.

For each suit in the game, count how many cards of that suit are face-up versus still buried face-down. The suit with the most visible cards, especially if those cards cluster in the same columns, is the one to target.

Pay attention to Kings and Aces specifically. A King is the anchor card that starts the sequence from the top. If you do not have a free King of your target suit, you need to find it before the rest of the work matters. An Ace is the final card that closes the sequence from the bottom. Both ends of the sequence are often the bottlenecks.

Our [suit strategy guide](/blog/spider-solitaire-suit-strategy) covers how to read suit distribution in the opening. That same read applies here: suit completion runs through whichever suit showed the strongest footprint in the early board.

## Building Toward Completion: The Middle Phase

Once you have identified a target suit, the middle phase looks like this:

- Group same-suit cards in the same column whenever possible, even if it means accepting mixed-suit stacks elsewhere temporarily.
- When you have a choice between a same-suit connection and a cross-suit connection of similar value, take the same-suit move.
- Use empty columns to route suited cards into position rather than to park mixed-suit stacks.

The [Spider suit strategy page](/spider-suit-strategy) covers the full framework for same-suit versus mixed-suit decision-making. The key point here is that once you commit to a target suit, that suit gets priority on your empty column budget. You are not splitting resources evenly.

## The Final Push: Closing the Last Four Cards

This is where most players stall. You have 9 or 10 cards of your target suit in sequence, and the next card is buried under a mixed stack or sitting in a column where it cannot connect.

The close almost always follows this pattern:

1. Identify the next card you need to add to your sequence.
2. Find where that card is on the board.
3. Clear the cards sitting on top of it. This usually requires moving them into empty columns or onto other tableau stacks temporarily.
4. Once the target card is exposed, connect it to your sequence.
5. Repeat until the run reaches from King to Ace in a single column.

Empty columns are the critical resource in this phase. If you are out of empty columns and the next card you need is buried, you need to [create an empty column](/spider/how-to-empty-a-column) before you can continue. That may mean doing some consolidation work first to free a column.

## What Changes After a Suit Completes

When 13 cards lift off the board, several things happen at once:

- You get back an empty column immediately.
- The board shrinks, which means shorter columns and more face-up cards visible.
- Your next target suit becomes easier to work on because the board has more room.

The first completion is the hardest. Every subsequent completion is easier because you have fewer cards in play and more open space to maneuver. Players who complete one suit early tend to win more often because the advantage compounds through the rest of the game.

Our [Spider endgame strategy](/spider-endgame-strategy) page covers how to close out a game once two or three suits are complete. That phase plays very differently from the mid-game, and the guide walks through the transition.

## When to Accelerate Your Timeline

Mid-game, you may be building two suits roughly in parallel. Balanced development is not always wrong. But there is a threshold where you should shift fully to closing out your nearest suit.

That threshold: when your nearest suit has 10 or more cards visible and in reachable positions, drop balanced development and go for the completion. The resources you spend closing a near-complete suit pay back more than continuing to develop a suit that is still five or six cards from done.

In 4-suit games especially, a completed suit changes the whole dynamic of the board. Completing one suit at move 40 in a 4-suit game is often the difference between winning and not finishing. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does the sequence have to be in one single column?

Yes. The 13 cards must be in a single column, in order from King at the top to Ace at the bottom, all of the same suit, with nothing on top. If your run is split across two columns, it does not complete until the pieces are connected into one.

### Can I complete a suit using cards from the stock?

Stock cards contribute to suit completions once they are dealt to the tableau through a normal deal. You cannot reserve cards in the stock. They land where they land, and you work them into position from the tableau.

### How do I know when a suit is within reach?

A suit is within reach when you can see 9 or more of its 13 cards face-up and they are spread across no more than three or four columns. At that point, the remaining buried cards are likely to surface with a deal or two. Start concentrating resources on that suit instead of spreading attention equally.

### Is completing one suit early always better than building multiple suits at once?

Usually yes, particularly in 4-suit games. A completed suit gives back a column and removes 13 cards for good. Split effort across multiple suits tends to produce long mixed-suit runs that are hard to convert into completions. Focused effort beats balanced effort in most Spider positions.
