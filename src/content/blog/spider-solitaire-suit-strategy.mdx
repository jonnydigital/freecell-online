In 1-suit Spider, there's no suit decision to make. Every card is the same suit. The game is about column management and timing.

In 2-suit and 4-suit Spider, suit strategy is the game. Which suit do you develop first? When do you abandon one suit to work on another? When do you invest resources in a suit that's currently buried?

Most players answer these questions intuitively, which works up to a point. But intuition tends to break down in the mid-game when you're juggling multiple sequences and limited empty columns. A more deliberate framework helps.

## Key Takeaways

- In 2-suit Spider, pick one suit to develop early and lean on it until you get close to a completion.
- Suit selection should follow card availability: build the suit where you can see the most cards in the opening.
- Avoid splitting your empty column budget evenly between suits. Concentrated effort beats balanced effort in most positions.
- In 4-suit Spider, finishing one complete sequence early shifts board dynamics significantly. Prioritize the suit closest to completion over balanced development.
- Mid-game suit switching is sometimes correct, but should be triggered by a clear threshold, not frustration.

## The Opening Survey: Reading the Suit Distribution

Before your first move in a 2-suit or 4-suit game, spend 15-20 seconds reading the visible cards and estimating suit distribution.

You won't see every card. Most are face-down. But the 10 visible cards (one at the bottom of each column) give you a rough signal. If you can see four or five cards of the same suit in the opening layout, that suit has visible anchors in multiple columns. You can start building it more easily.

In 2-suit Spider, you're looking at Spades and Hearts (or whichever pair is in play). Count the visible cards of each suit. If you see 6 Spades and 4 Hearts, Spades is your suit to develop first. You'll find connectors more easily and waste fewer moves on suit mismatches.

In 4-suit Spider, the same survey applies but with more variables. You're looking for a suit that has visible cards at both the high end (King, Queen, Jack) and the low end (3, 2, Ace), because completing a sequence requires the whole range. A suit with a visible King and several mid-range cards in multiple columns is a strong candidate for early development.

## What "Developing a Suit" Actually Means

Developing a suit means concentrating your moves on building in-suit sequences in that suit, accepting that other suits will get less attention for a while.

Practically, this means:
- When you have a choice between a Spades connection and a Hearts connection, prefer the Spades move.
- When you need to use an empty column, use it to route a Spades card into position rather than solving a Hearts problem.
- When you deal from the stock, scan the new cards for Spades connectors first.

This isn't absolute. You still take obviously good moves in other suits, and you still uncover face-down cards regardless of suit. But when priorities are roughly equal, your chosen suit gets the tie-breaking vote.

The payoff from this concentration is that your development suit's sequences grow longer faster, and longer sequences can be moved as units. A six-card in-suit sequence is a powerful piece on the board. It can be repositioned as needed, and it's one step closer to a completion that removes 13 cards.

## When to Switch Development Focus

Suit strategy isn't static. You should reconsider your primary suit when any of the following happens.

**Your development suit runs out of visible connectors.** If you've built your Spades sequences as far as the visible cards allow, and progress requires uncovering buried cards before you can continue, shift focus to another suit while those reveals happen naturally.

**A different suit is suddenly much closer to completion.** Suppose you're 70% through building your first Spades sequence when you deal a round that gives you a Queen, Jack, and 10 of Hearts in adjacent columns. Hearts might be completable in 8 moves. Spades might take 20 more moves. Pivot to Hearts.

**Your empty columns are exhausted and one suit is blocking the other.** Sometimes two suits get entangled: you need to move a Hearts sequence to reach a buried Spades card, but you don't have the column space to do it. In this situation, work on clearing Hearts first so you can eventually access the Spades cards beneath.

The key is that suit switching should be triggered by a concrete board condition, not by restlessness or the feeling that you've been working on one suit too long.

## The Completion Priority Trap

One of the most common mid-game mistakes in 4-suit Spider is developing all four suits simultaneously at low levels of completion, rather than pushing one suit to completion early.

It's tempting. You see connections across multiple suits and you make them all. The board looks like it's developing evenly. Then you realize your Spades sequence is at J-10-9-8-7, your Hearts sequence is at Q-J-10-9, your Diamonds sequence is at J-10-9, and your Clubs sequence is at K-Q-J. None of them are close to completion. And you've used all your easy moves.

Compare this to a player who spent the same number of moves pushing Spades from K down through A. They have a completed sequence. Thirteen cards are gone. The board is smaller. They have breathing room to work on the remaining three suits.

Completed sequences aren't just scoring events. They simplify the board in a way that no amount of partial progress replicates. In 4-suit Spider, every completed sequence is like having an easier game for the next 10-15 moves.

**The practical rule:** when one suit is within 4-5 cards of completion and you have reasonable access to those cards, prioritize it over developing any other suit.

## Suits and Empty Columns: The Budget Framework

Think of each empty column as a unit of currency. Every move that requires an empty column spends some of that currency (temporarily, if you recover the column, or permanently if you fill it and can't re-empty it).

Different suits draw on this budget differently. A suit where you can see most of the sequence and have many cards already in order requires fewer empty column moves. A suit where critical cards are buried under off-suit sequences requires many empty column moves to excavate.

When you're picking a suit to develop, factor in the empty column cost. A suit that looks 80% complete but requires three empty column routing operations to finish might be less accessible than a suit that looks 60% complete but can progress with normal surface moves.

This budget thinking also helps with the "when to switch" question. If your primary suit is going to require four empty column operations in a row, and you currently have two empty columns, you don't have the budget. Switch to the suit that fits your current column count.

## 2-Suit vs 4-Suit: How the Strategy Scales

In 2-suit Spider, the suit strategy framework above applies but is simpler to execute. With only two suits, "pick one and develop it" is less of a strategic debate. You're mainly deciding which suit to finish first and whether to divert resources briefly to uncover cards in the other suit.

The main 2-suit-specific consideration is balance. Finishing both suits requires eventually shifting attention. A player who pushes Spades to completion before touching Hearts will enter the Hearts development phase with a simpler board (fewer cards in play). A player who tries to develop both simultaneously often gets neither close to completion and runs out of moves.

In 4-suit Spider, the framework becomes critical. Four suits, 104 cards, and a limited empty column budget mean every decision about where to concentrate has compounding effects. The opening suit survey matters more. The completion priority rule matters more. Suit switching at the right time matters more.

If you're struggling with 4-suit Spider, audit your suit prioritization first. Most losing games have a common shape: development spread too thin, no suit near completion, board congestion with no routing space. Concentrated suit development is the fix. ## Frequently Asked Questions

**In 4-suit Spider, does it matter which suit I develop first?**
Yes. The best choice is the suit where you can see the most cards across the full King-to-Ace range in the opening. A suit with visible high cards (King, Queen) and visible low cards (3, 2, Ace) in multiple columns is the most developable. Avoid leading with a suit where most of the sequence is buried face-down with no visible connectors.

**What if I can't tell which suit is best in the opening?**
Pick the suit with the most visible face-up cards across different columns. More visible cards means more known connections. When in doubt, Spades is often a reasonable default in apps that use Spades-heavy distributions, but this varies.

**Should I ever completely ignore a suit for the early and mid-game?**
Sometimes yes. If one suit has very few visible cards and significant face-down coverage, it's reasonable to leave it alone until you've uncovered more of the board. Just don't ignore it so long that you have no idea where its cards are when you finally need to build it.

**Does building off-suit ever help my suit strategy?**
Yes. Off-suit building is sometimes the only way to uncover face-down cards in your target suit. The cost is a fragmented sequence you'll need to sort later. The benefit is access to a card you need. If the card you're uncovering is a high-priority suit connector, the trade is usually worth it.

**When should I deal from the stock relative to my suit strategy?**
Deal when you've exhausted productive moves in your primary suit. The stock deal might bring the connectors you've been missing, or it might complicate your position. Either way, you want to have made every available suit progress move before adding 10 more variables to the board. For more on stock timing, see the [Spider strategy guide](/spider/strategy).
