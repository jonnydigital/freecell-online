Spider Solitaire looks straightforward at first. Deal the cards, build some sequences, clear the board. But new players lose far more games than they should — not because Spider is impossibly hard, but because a handful of common mistakes quietly sabotage their chances.

If you've been playing [Spider Solitaire](/spider) and feel like you're stuck in a losing streak, chances are good that one or more of these seven mistakes is the culprit. Fix them, and your win rate will climb.

## Mistake #1: Starting With 4-Suit Spider

This is the biggest beginner mistake in Spider Solitaire, and it's the easiest to fix.

Spider comes in three difficulty levels based on the number of suits in play: 1-suit, 2-suit, and 4-suit. Many players jump straight to 4-suit because it feels like the "real" version of the game. That's a fast track to frustration.

**4-suit Spider has a win rate of around 10-15%** even for experienced players. You're dealing with all four suits across 104 cards, and only cards of the same suit form completed sequences. It's one of the hardest solitaire variants that exists.

**1-suit Spider has a win rate of 90-95%** with careful play. Since every card is the same suit, every descending sequence is automatically valid. You can focus entirely on learning Spider's core mechanics — column management, stock timing, sequence building — without the added complexity of suit matching.

Start with 1-suit. Get comfortable. Move to 2-suit when you're winning regularly. Then consider 4-suit. Our [breakdown of all three difficulty levels](/spider/1-suit-vs-2-suit-vs-4-suit) explains exactly what changes at each step.

## Mistake #2: Filling Empty Columns Immediately

An empty column in Spider is one of the most powerful tools you have. It's temporary storage — a place to park a card while you rearrange sequences behind it. Experienced players treat empty columns like gold.

Beginners tend to fill them immediately, stacking whatever card seems convenient into the open space. Once it's filled, that flexibility is gone.

**The fix:** Before placing a card in an empty column, ask yourself: "Am I using this space strategically, or just filling it because it's there?" If you don't have a specific plan that requires the column to be filled, leave it open. You'll need it later.

That said, don't leave columns empty forever, either. If filling a column lets you uncover face-down cards or complete a sequence, that's a good trade. The key is being intentional about it.

## Mistake #3: Building Mixed-Suit Sequences Without Thinking

In 2-suit and 4-suit Spider, you can stack any descending card on top of another regardless of suit. A black 7 can go on a red 8. But here's what beginners miss: **mixed-suit sequences can't be moved as a group.**

If you build a sequence of 10-9-8-7 where the suits don't match, you can only move the top card individually. A fully in-suit sequence of 10-9-8-7 can be moved as a complete unit. This difference is enormous.

Mixed-suit building isn't always wrong — sometimes it's your only option, or it lets you uncover a critical face-down card. But every time you place an off-suit card, you're creating a future problem. That sequence is now fragmented, and you'll need free columns or additional moves to fix it later.

**The fix:** When you have a choice between building in-suit and off-suit, always prefer in-suit. Only build off-suit when there's a clear tactical benefit — like uncovering a face-down card or preventing a dead-end. ## Mistake #4: Dealing From the Stock Too Early

Spider's stock pile contains 50 cards dealt in five rounds of 10 — one card added to each column. Every deal makes the board more complex. New cards land on top of whatever you've been building, potentially burying key cards and breaking sequences.

Beginners often deal from the stock when they feel stuck, hoping fresh cards will open things up. Sometimes they do. More often, they make the situation worse by adding 10 new problems to a board you hadn't finished solving.

**The fix:** Exhaust every possible move before dealing. Flip every face-down card you can reach. Build every sequence you can build. Create empty columns if possible. Only deal when you've genuinely run out of productive moves.

Remember: you must have at least one card in every column before dealing. If you have an empty column, you'll need to fill it before you can deal — which means you're using a valuable resource just to access the stock. Plan your column usage around upcoming deals.

## Mistake #5: Ignoring Face-Down Cards

Spider starts with 54 face-down cards spread across the tableau. Every face-down card is hidden information — you don't know what's under there, and you can't complete the game until every card is revealed.

Beginners tend to focus on building pretty sequences rather than uncovering hidden cards. They'll stack a long run of visible cards while leaving face-down piles untouched in other columns.

**The fix:** Prioritize moves that reveal face-down cards. If you have a choice between extending an existing sequence and making a move that flips a new card, flip the card. Information is power in Spider. The sooner you see all 104 cards, the better you can plan.

This is especially important in the early game. The first several moves should almost always focus on uncovering as many face-down cards as possible. For more on early-game priorities, check out our [Spider tips page](/spider/tips).

## Mistake #6: Trying to Complete Sequences Too Early

A completed sequence in Spider — King down through Ace in a single suit — is automatically removed from the board. That's satisfying, and it's the whole point of the game. But beginners sometimes fixate on completing one sequence at the expense of everything else.

If you pour all your resources into finishing one King-to-Ace run while neglecting the rest of the board, you'll end up with one completed sequence and a chaotic mess of tangled columns. Spider is a game of managing all 10 columns simultaneously, not tunnel-visioning on one.

**The fix:** Think of completed sequences as a natural byproduct of good board management, not the primary goal of each move. Keep your columns organized, reveal face-down cards, maintain empty columns when possible, and completed sequences will come together organically.

The exception: if you're close to finishing a sequence and completing it would free up significant column space, it's worth prioritizing. But "close" means two or three cards away, not seven.

## Mistake #7: Not Planning Ahead

Spider punishes reactive play. If you're only looking at the current move without considering what comes next, you'll constantly paint yourself into corners.

This is especially true in 2-suit and 4-suit games, where suit management adds layers of complexity. But even in 1-suit Spider, short-term thinking leads to boards that look fine now and collapse three moves later.

**The fix:** Before every move, look at least two or three moves ahead. Ask yourself:
- What does this move enable?
- What does it block?
- Will I need an empty column for what I'm planning, and do I have one?
- Am I about to bury a card I'll need soon?

You don't need to plan the entire game — Spider has too many variables for that. But thinking a few moves ahead separates beginners from intermediate players. Our [Spider how-to-play guide](/spider/how-to-play) can help you build the right mental framework.

## Putting It All Together

Spider Solitaire has a reputation for being difficult, and at 4-suit it genuinely is. But most beginner losses come from these avoidable mistakes, not from the game being impossible.

Start with 1-suit. Protect your empty columns. Build in-suit whenever possible. Don't deal until you've exhausted your options. Prioritize uncovering face-down cards. Think a few moves ahead. Do these things consistently, and you'll go from losing most of your games to winning most of them.
