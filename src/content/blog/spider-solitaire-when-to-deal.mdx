Clicking the stock in Spider Solitaire is the only move you cannot take back with careful play. Ten new cards land at once, one on every column, and every run you left exposed is now buried. Most blown Spider games trace back to a deal made one move too early. This guide gives you a concrete checklist to run before you touch the stock.

## Key Takeaways

- Never deal while a productive move exists. "Productive" means it flips a card, extends an in-suit run, or opens a column.
- You must fill every empty column before dealing. Choose the filler card deliberately, not by reflex.
- Before dealing, consolidate runs in-suit wherever possible so the new cards do the least damage.
- The stock holds exactly five deals of ten. Count them. The last deal decides most close games.

## What a Deal Actually Does to Your Board

Each deal drops one card face-up on each of the ten columns. That means:

- Every ordered run you built now has a random card sitting on top of it.
- Every column you were one move from flipping is now two moves away.
- Any empty column you had is gone, filled by a random card.

New cards are not free progress. They are ten new problems, and the question is whether you have squeezed the current position dry before accepting them.

## The Pre-Deal Checklist

Run through these five checks in order. Deal only when you get through all of them with nothing left to do.

### 1. Have you made every card-flipping move?

Scan each column for a face-down card that one more move would reveal. Flips are the most valuable moves in Spider, and a deal makes every pending flip one card harder. If a flip is available, take it before dealing.

### 2. Can any run be rebuilt in-suit?

A run of mixed suits moves one card at a time. A run in a single suit moves as a block. Before you deal, look for swaps that convert mixed runs into suited ones, like moving a 7♠ from a mixed pile onto an 8♠ instead of leaving it on an 8♥. Our [suit strategy guide](/spider-suit-strategy) covers these rebuilds in depth. Post-deal chaos is much easier to manage when your existing runs move as units.

### 3. Is there an empty column, and what will you put in it?

Standard Spider rules will not let you deal while any column is empty. But do not just drop the nearest card into the space. An empty column is the strongest asset in the game, and what you park there matters. A King is a common choice since Kings cannot move onto anything else. A card that breaks up a mixed run is often better. Our [empty columns deep-dive](/blog/spider-solitaire-empty-columns) walks through the options.

### 4. Where will the damage land?

You know ten random cards are coming. Look at your board and ask which columns you care about least. If your long suited runs are spread across many columns, every one of them takes a hit. When you can, stack your good structure into fewer columns before dealing so the incoming cards mostly land on junk piles. Sacrificial columns are a real technique, and the [column tactics guide](/spider-column-tactics) covers how to designate them.

### 5. Have you counted your remaining deals?

The stock starts with 50 cards: five deals of ten. In a 104-card game, that means roughly half the deck arrives through deals. If you are three deals in with little suited structure built, the position is telling you to take more risk now, because the last two deals will only add noise. If you are one deal in and stuck, slow down and re-scan, since you probably missed a rebuild.

## Dealing Early on Purpose: The One Exception

There is one position where dealing before you are fully stuck is defensible: when every remaining move would wreck structure you want to keep. If your only legal moves break suited runs apart or bury a card you need reachable, a deal that changes the landscape can be the least bad option.

Treat this as rare. In [1-suit games](/spider/1-suit-vs-2-suit-vs-4-suit) it almost never applies, because moves are cheap and structure is easy to rebuild. In 4-suit games it comes up a few times per game, and recognizing it is part of what separates intermediate players from strong ones.

## A Worked Example

Suppose column 3 shows 9♥-8♥-7♥ with one face-down card beneath, and column 6 has a bare 10♥ on top of junk. Moving the 9♥ run onto the 10♥ is productive twice over: it extends an in-suit run to four cards, and it exposes column 3's face-down card, which you flip before dealing.

Now suppose you had dealt first. The new card lands on your 9♥-8♥-7♥, the 10♥ gets its own random cover, and the flip in column 3 is buried a card deeper. Same board, one impatient click, and three kinds of progress lost.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I have to fill empty columns before dealing?

Yes, under standard rules the deal button is locked while any column is empty. This is a rules constraint, but treat it as a strategy prompt: the game is forcing you to decide what your most valuable real estate holds before new cards arrive.

### How many times can you deal in Spider Solitaire?

Five. The stock holds 50 cards and each deal places ten. Once the stock is empty, the cards on the board are all you get.

### Does dealing reshuffle the stock?

No. The order of the stock is fixed when the game is dealt. Dealing reveals the next ten cards; it does not change what they are.

### Should I deal as soon as I run out of obvious moves?

Not immediately. "Out of obvious moves" and "out of moves" are different. Check for in-suit rebuilds and single-card repositions first; they are easy to overlook. When the checklist genuinely comes up empty, deal with a clear conscience.
