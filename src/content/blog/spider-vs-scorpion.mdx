Spider Solitaire and Scorpion Solitaire look related on the surface. Both use 52-card single-deck layouts. Both involve building sequences by suit. Both finish when you've assembled four complete King-to-Ace runs.

The resemblance ends there. Spider and Scorpion are built on different structural principles, reward different skills, and fail for different reasons. If you know Spider well and want to understand Scorpion, or if you're deciding which to invest time in, this comparison should save you some confused early games.

## Key Takeaways

- Spider uses two full decks across 10 columns; Scorpion uses one deck across 7 columns.
- In Spider, only fully in-suit sequences can be removed. In Scorpion, you can move any card in a column onto any valid connector, regardless of what's on top of it.
- Spider's core challenge is suit management across a large card pool. Scorpion's core challenge is column congestion with far less room to maneuver.
- Scorpion is generally considered harder than 1-suit Spider but easier than 4-suit Spider.
- Neither game is objectively better. They're genuinely different disciplines within the same family.

## The Structural Difference: Two Decks vs One

Spider deals 104 cards (two standard decks) across 10 columns, with a 50-card stock available in five deals. The scale is part of the game's identity. You're managing a large, complex board, and the stock gives you five distinct waves of new cards to work with.

Scorpion uses a single 52-card deck across 7 columns. Seven columns start with 7 cards each, three of which are face-down. Three remaining cards form a small reserve of three. There's no stock in the traditional Spider sense; the three reserve cards are dealt one to each of the first three columns if you get completely stuck.

The smaller scale creates a very different feel. In Spider, the board feels crowded early and gradually opens up as you complete sequences. In Scorpion, the board starts tighter and stays tight. You're working with a constrained set of columns and very little surplus card distribution.

## The Movement Rule That Changes Everything

This is the most important mechanical difference between the two games.

In Spider, you move cards (or in-suit sequences) only from the top of columns. You can move the top card, or a group of cards that form a complete in-suit sequence, but you can't reach into the middle of a column.

In Scorpion, you can move any card in a column directly onto a valid connector elsewhere. If there's a 9 of Hearts buried mid-column, and you need it on a 10 of Hearts somewhere else, you can move the entire stack of cards sitting on top of the 9 as a unit to another column, and then place the 9 (plus whatever was on it) where it needs to go.

This rule is powerful and creates Scorpion's distinctive flavor. You can access cards that Spider would leave permanently buried until you cleared everything above them. But it comes with a catch: moving a stack of cards to access a buried card means that entire stack now lives somewhere else. The column you moved it from might be more useful. The column you moved it to is now more congested. Every "access" move is also a "rearrangement" move with cascading consequences.

Beginners to Scorpion often overuse this rule. They dig freely for buried cards without considering where the moved stacks land. The board gets reorganized into a state that's harder to work with than what they started with.

## How Building and Removal Work in Each Game

**Spider:** You can place any card on any card one rank higher, regardless of suit. A 7 of Clubs on an 8 of Hearts is legal. But only a complete King-to-Ace sequence in a single suit gets removed from the board. Mixed-suit runs are legal movements but don't score.

This creates Spider's central tension: off-suit building is sometimes necessary to uncover cards, but every off-suit placement creates a problem you'll need to solve later.

**Scorpion:** All building must be in-suit. A 7 of Clubs can only go on an 8 of Clubs. There's no off-suit building option at all. This sounds more restrictive, but because you can access any card in a column, you're not trapped by a single-suit surface card as badly as you might be in Spider.

Sequence removal works the same way: a complete King-to-Ace run in one suit is removed. Four completions wins the game.

## Comparing the Skill Requirements

**In Spider**, the primary skill is suit management and sequence consolidation. You're constantly deciding when to build off-suit (for card access) versus in-suit (for sequence quality). Empty column management is critical. The five-wave stock system means timing your deals is a significant strategic layer. Read more about how that plays out in our [Spider opening strategy guide](/spider-opening-strategy).

**In Scorpion**, the primary skill is stack depth awareness and congestion management. You're deciding how to access buried cards without congesting your 7 columns beyond recovery. The in-suit-only building rule removes Spider's suit-mixing dilemma, but it makes finding legal moves harder. You'll spend more time scanning for in-suit connectors and less time deciding whether to break suit.

Players who find Spider's suit management the interesting part tend to prefer Spider. Players who find Spider's scale exhausting but like the sequence-building concept often prefer Scorpion's tighter format.

## Win Rates and Difficulty

Reliable comparative win rate data for Scorpion is harder to find than for Spider. The game is less widely tracked by solitaire apps.

Community estimates put Scorpion's win rate for experienced players in the 25-35% range, though figures vary. That would place it considerably harder than 1-suit Spider (roughly 90-95% for skilled players) and roughly comparable to 2-suit Spider (roughly 40-50%) or slightly below it.

Some experienced solitaire players argue Scorpion is harder than 2-suit Spider because of the column congestion combined with in-suit-only building. Others find 2-suit Spider harder because of the larger card pool and stock management layer. The honest answer is that direct comparisons depend on individual skill profiles.

What most sources agree on: Scorpion is not 4-suit Spider hard. If you're comfortable with 2-suit Spider and want a different type of challenge rather than a harder version of the same challenge, Scorpion is worth trying.

## Where Spider Fits You vs Where Scorpion Fits You

Play Spider if:
- You like managing large, complex boards.
- The strategic depth of suit prioritization across 104 cards appeals to you.
- You want a game with a clear difficulty ladder (1-suit to 4-suit).
- You're interested in the highest possible skill ceiling in the spider-family genre.

Play Scorpion if:
- You prefer tighter, single-deck games with less sprawl.
- You find Spider's two-deck scale overwhelming or tedious.
- You want in-suit-only building without the suit-mixing tension of Spider.
- You like the feel of digging through stacks rather than managing surface-level sequences.

The games serve different moods and different play styles. Strong Spider players who try Scorpion often find it disorienting at first, because the access rules are more permissive but the building rules are stricter. Give it at least 10 games before judging it.

## A Word on Other Spider-Family Variants

The spider-family is larger than just these two games. Spiderette uses a single deck in Spider's format, scaled down for quicker games. Will o' the Wisp is another compact variant. Wasp has similarities to Scorpion with some movement differences.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is Scorpion Solitaire harder than Spider?**
It depends on which Spider you compare to. Scorpion is harder than 1-suit Spider and roughly comparable to 2-suit Spider in difficulty. It's not as hard as 4-suit Spider. The difficulty comparison is also complicated by the different skill types each game requires, so players with different strengths will rank them differently.

**Can I move any card in Scorpion, or are there restrictions?**
You can move any card in a column to a valid in-suit connector elsewhere, taking all cards above it as a unit. Cards can't be moved to an empty column unless the card is a King (in most standard Scorpion rules). Face-down cards must be uncovered before they can be moved.

**Does Spider have anything like Scorpion's mid-column access rule?**
No. Spider requires moving cards from the top of the column only. In Spider, a card buried mid-column is inaccessible until everything above it has been moved elsewhere. This is a fundamental structural difference between the two games.

**Which game is better for beginners?**
1-suit Spider is probably the better starting point because the rules are simpler and the win rate is high enough to be encouraging. Scorpion's in-suit building rule and mid-column access rule add up to a more confusing learning curve for someone new to the solitaire family.

**Are there other games similar to Scorpion?**
Yes. Wasp Solitaire is the closest relative to Scorpion, using similar movement rules but a different deal layout. Both trace back to the same spider-family mechanics. If you like Scorpion, Wasp is a natural next step.
