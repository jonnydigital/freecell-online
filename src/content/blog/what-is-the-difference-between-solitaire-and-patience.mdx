If you have spent any time reading about card games, you have probably encountered both "Solitaire" and "Patience" used to describe what appears to be the same thing. Are they different games? Different rules? The answer is simpler than you might expect.

## The Short Answer

Solitaire and Patience are two names for the same family of single-player card games. "Solitaire" is the term used primarily in North America. "Patience" is the term used in the United Kingdom, Europe, and much of the rest of the world.

There is no rule difference, no gameplay variation, and no technical distinction. They are synonyms.

## Why Two Names?

The divergence traces back to the origins of single-player card games in 18th-century Europe.

### The European Roots of "Patience"

The earliest known single-player card games appeared in Northern Europe — likely Germany or Scandinavia — in the late 1700s. They quickly spread to France, where they became known as *jeux de patience* (games of patience). The name reflected the temperament required: careful, methodical play with no opponent to rush you.

The term "Patience" stuck across Europe. In German, the games are called *Patience* or *Patiencen*. In French, *Patience* or *Réussite*. In Swedish, *Patiens*. British English adopted "Patience" directly from the French.

### The American Adoption of "Solitaire"

When single-player card games crossed the Atlantic to North America, they acquired a new name. "Solitaire" comes from the French word *solitaire*, meaning "solitary" or "alone" — a reference to the single-player nature of the games rather than the patience they require.

Why Americans chose "solitaire" over "patience" is not entirely clear. One theory is that the word "patience" was already commonly used as a general English word (meaning the quality of being patient), and "solitaire" felt more specific as a game category. Another is that early American card-game books used "solitaire" in their titles, and the term simply caught on.

### Microsoft Cemented "Solitaire" Globally

The biggest factor in the worldwide dominance of the word "Solitaire" was Microsoft Windows. When Microsoft bundled [Klondike as "Solitaire"](/klondike) in Windows 3.0 in 1990, hundreds of millions of people around the world learned to call the game "Solitaire" — regardless of what term their country traditionally used.

Today, even in countries where "Patience" is the traditional term, many younger players use "Solitaire" because that is what they see on their screens.

## Is There Any Real Difference?

In modern usage, no. Both terms mean "single-player card games." However, there are some subtle connotations:

- **"Solitaire"** sometimes refers specifically to Klondike (the default Windows game) rather than the entire family
- **"Patience"** almost always refers to the entire family of games, never just one specific variant
- **"Solitaire"** can also refer to non-card games played alone (like peg solitaire, a marble-jumping board game)
- **"Patience"** is exclusively used for card games

In card-game books and serious card-game communities, "Patience" is often preferred because it is more precise — it always means card games, never board games.

The global trend is toward "Solitaire" displacing "Patience," largely due to the influence of digital games and American English in technology.

## A Game by Any Name

Whether you call it Solitaire or Patience, you are playing from the same family of single-player card games that has entertained people for over 200 years. The rules are identical. The strategy is identical. The satisfaction of clearing a difficult board is identical.

The only difference is which side of the Atlantic taught you the name.
