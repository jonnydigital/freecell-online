You've been staring at the board for 15 minutes. Every path leads to a dead end. Is this deal actually impossible, or are you just missing something?

The comforting truth: **almost certainly, you're just missing something.** Out of the original 32,000 Microsoft FreeCell deals, only **one** — [Deal #11982](/blog/is-every-freecell-game-winnable) — is provably unsolvable. That's 0.003%.

But understanding *why* deals become unsolvable helps you play the other 99.997% better.

## What Makes Deal #11982 Impossible?

The infamous deal has a specific structural problem: critical low cards are buried beneath cards that require those same low cards to be freed. It creates a circular dependency — Card A needs Card B moved, but Card B needs Card A moved first.

Mathematicians and computer scientists verified this through exhaustive search. Every possible move sequence was tested. None lead to a win.

## The Three Patterns That Kill Games

Even in solvable deals, you can create unsolvable *positions* through bad play. Here are the patterns:

### 1. Circular Dependencies

The deadliest pattern. It happens when:
- The 5♠ is trapped under the 3♥
- The 3♥ needs the 4♥ freed
- The 4♥ is under the 5♠

This circular lock means no card in the chain can move. If you can't break the cycle through free cells or empty columns, the game is over.

**How to avoid it:** Before burying a card, trace forward: "What cards does this card need? Where are those cards?" If they depend on each other, find a different move.

### 2. Resource Exhaustion

FreeCell gives you exactly 4 free cells and starts with 0 empty columns. These are your total workspace. When all 4 cells are full and no tableau moves are legal, you're stuck.

This doesn't mean the deal is unsolvable — it means you used your resources too aggressively. The solution is usually 10-15 moves back, where you should have kept a cell open instead of filling it.

**How to avoid it:** The [2-cell rule](/bakers-game/tips) — always keep at least 2 free cells open. One for the current operation, one as emergency reserve.

### 3. Foundation Trapping

This subtle one catches even experienced players. You rush the 8♦ to the foundation because you can. But now the 7♦ in the tableau has nowhere to go — it could have gone on that 8♦, but you moved it to the foundation. The 7♦ is now blocking a critical card underneath.

Foundation moves are permanent. Unlike tableau moves, you can't take cards *back* from the foundation. Overly aggressive foundation building narrows your options.

**How to avoid it:** Only auto-play cards to foundations when both cards of the next-lower rank (in the other color) are already on foundations. The game's auto-move system handles this correctly, but manual plays can get you in trouble.

## How to Tell If You're Stuck vs. Actually Stuck

When you feel stuck, run through this checklist:

1. **Are all 4 free cells full?** If not, you have options. Use a cell temporarily.
2. **Can any card go to a foundation?** Even a move that seems unhelpful might unblock something.
3. **Is there a tableau move you missed?** Check every column against every other column. Cards can sometimes go to surprising places.
4. **Can you undo to a branch point?** Most "stuck" positions have a [solution hiding 5-10 moves back](/blog/how-to-win-freecell-every-time).

If you've truly exhausted all options after checking all four? Use the [FreeCell solver](/solver) to verify. It'll either show you the path you missed, or confirm the position is genuinely unsolvable (usually because of earlier moves, not the deal itself).

## The Mathematical Beauty

FreeCell sits in a fascinating sweet spot: complex enough that solving a general deal is computationally hard (it's NP-complete), but structured enough that almost every deal has a solution.

This isn't an accident. The game's design — 4 free cells, 8 cascades, alternating-color building — creates just enough flexibility that blocking patterns almost always have an escape route. Add one more column and the game would be trivially easy. Remove one free cell and it would be frustratingly hard (which is why [3-Cell FreeCell](/freecell/3-cell) and [Baker's Game](/bakers-game) exist for players who want that challenge).

## The Bottom Line

If you're losing a game, it's almost certainly solvable. The deal isn't impossible — you just haven't found the path yet. Use [undo liberally](/tips), try different approaches, and remember that the answer usually lies in moves you *didn't* make, not moves you did.
